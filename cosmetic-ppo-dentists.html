The menu

What cosmetic dentistry covers

Cosmetic dentistry is the work that makes a healthy smile look better: veneers, whitening, bonding, and contouring. It overlaps with restorative dentistry, which repairs damaged teeth, and that overlap is exactly where your insurance does or does not step in.

Here is the short menu in plain terms:

  • Veneers: thin shells bonded to the front of teeth to change shape, color, and alignment. Porcelain lasts longest and looks most natural; composite is cheaper and faster but wears sooner.
  • Teeth whitening: professional bleaching, either in-office in a single visit or with a custom take-home tray from your dentist. Stronger and more even than drugstore strips.
  • Dental bonding: tooth-colored resin sculpted onto a tooth to fix a chip, close a small gap, or reshape an edge. Quick, no lab, less expensive than a veneer.
  • Cosmetic contouring and reshaping: gently reshaping enamel or gum line to even out a smile. Subtle and usually quick.
  • Smile makeover: a planned combination of the above, often paired with restorative work like crowns, to redesign a whole smile.

The coverage line

What PPO does and does not cover

Most PPO dental plans do not cover purely cosmetic work. They pay only when a procedure is restorative or medically necessary. The same procedure can land on either side of that line depending on why it is done, and that is the part most cost pages skip.

Usually covered (restorative)

  • A crown on a cracked, decayed, or root-canaled tooth
  • A filling on a tooth with decay
  • A veneer or crown that is the clinically appropriate way to restore a tooth broken by trauma or significant decay (with documentation)
  • Replacing a failed restoration that is causing a problem

Usually not covered (cosmetic)

  • Teeth whitening on healthy teeth
  • Veneers placed to change the look of healthy teeth
  • Cosmetic reshaping or contouring for appearance
  • Gum contouring for a more even smile line

The one real exception: when a veneer or crown is genuinely restorative, some plans such as Delta Dental and Cigna may cover part of it with prior authorization, often 30 to 50 percent of the approved fee, subject to your annual maximum (usually $1,000 to $2,000). It is the exception, not the rule. Always request a written predetermination from your carrier before treatment so there are no surprises.

The smart split: a real smile makeover often needs restorative work too, like a crown on a chipped tooth or a build-up under a veneer. Use your PPO for that covered portion at the in-network rate, and pay cash or finance the purely cosmetic layer. Questions

Cosmetic PPO dentist FAQ

Do PPO dental plans cover cosmetic dentistry?
No, as a rule. Whitening, veneers for appearance, and cosmetic reshaping are standard PPO exclusions. A PPO pays only when a procedure is restorative or medically necessary, such as a crown on a cracked tooth or a filling on a decayed one.
How much do veneers cost in 2026?
Porcelain veneers run about $900 to $2,500 per tooth, with a national average near $1,765. A full set of six to eight teeth commonly costs $12,000 to $24,000. Composite veneers are cheaper at about $250 to $1,500 per tooth but last fewer years.
How much does professional teeth whitening cost?
In-office professional whitening typically costs $400 to $800 per session, averaging around $508. Laser whitening can cost slightly more. A take-home kit from your dentist usually costs less than an in-office session.
When does PPO insurance ever pay toward a veneer or crown?
When the work is restorative, not cosmetic. If a veneer or crown is the clinically appropriate way to restore a tooth damaged by trauma or significant decay, some PPO plans may cover part of it with prior authorization, often 30 to 50 percent, subject to the annual maximum. Request a predetermination first.
What is the difference between cosmetic and restorative dentistry?
Restorative dentistry repairs damaged or decayed teeth and is usually covered by PPO plans. Cosmetic dentistry improves the look of healthy teeth and is usually not covered. The same procedure can fall on either side depending on why it is done.
