First, the triage

What is a dental emergency?

A dental emergency is a problem that needs urgent care to stop pain, save a tooth, or prevent a spreading infection. Some things need a dentist today; others can safely wait for a regular appointment.

See a dentist today

  • Severe or throbbing pain that painkillers do not control
  • A knocked-out tooth (act within 30 to 60 minutes)
  • A broken or cracked tooth, especially if the nerve is exposed
  • A lost filling or crown that exposes the tooth
  • A swollen abscess, sometimes with a bad taste or fever
  • Uncontrolled bleeding after an extraction

Usually can wait

  • A small chip with no pain
  • A dull ache that responds to painkillers
  • A lost crown with no pain (still book soon)
  • Mild, brief sensitivity to hot or cold
  • Food stuck between teeth that flosses out
Right now

What to do right now

A few minutes of the right first aid can save a tooth and ease the pain while you reach a dentist. Here is the short version by problem.

  1. Knocked-out tooth

    Pick it up by the crown, not the root. Rinse gently with water, then try to slip it back into the socket. If you cannot, keep it in a cup of milk and get to a dentist within 30 to 60 minutes.

  2. Severe pain or an abscess

    Rinse with warm salt water and take over-the-counter pain relief as directed on the label. Do not place aspirin directly on the gum. Call for the soonest urgent visit.

  3. Broken or cracked tooth

    Rinse your mouth, save any pieces, and use a cold compress on the cheek to limit swelling. Avoid chewing on that side until you are seen.

  4. Lost filling or crown

    Keep the area clean, avoid chewing on it, and book a same-day or next-day visit to re-cement or replace it before bacteria get in.

Go to the ER, not a dentist, if

Swelling is spreading toward your eye or down your neck, you have trouble breathing or swallowing, a high fever, uncontrolled bleeding, or a serious facial injury. Those are medical emergencies. Call 911 or go to the nearest emergency room first, then follow up with a dentist.

Where to go

ER or dentist for tooth pain?

For ordinary tooth pain, see a dentist, not the ER. Emergency rooms have no dental chair or drill, and ER doctors cannot fill, root canal, or extract a tooth. A dental ER visit usually ends with antibiotics, a painkiller, and a referral back to a dentist.

The cost gap is real. A dental ER visit commonly runs about $400 to $1,500, with recent data showing average bills around $2,437, versus roughly $90 to $200 for a dentist visit for the same complaint. The American Dental Association estimates there are about 2 million U.S. hospital ER visits for dental pain each year, most of which a dentist could handle, which would save the system an estimated $1.7 billion. The ER treats the symptom; a dentist fixes the tooth.

Use the ER only for a true medical emergency (spreading facial swelling, trouble breathing or swallowing, high fever, uncontrolled bleeding, or trauma). For everything else, an emergency dentist is faster, cheaper, and actually solves the problem.

Same-day and coverage

Same-day care and PPO emergency coverage

Same-day emergency dentistry is widely available, including after-hours slots. Call ahead, describe your symptom, and ask for the soonest urgent appointment. With a PPO plan, the cost is lowest when the office is in your network.

Here is how PPO emergency coverage generally works:

  • Palliative (pain-relief) treatment for a flare-up is typically covered, so the office can stabilize you and stop the pain.
  • The underlying procedure such as an extraction, root canal, or crown is paid at the plan's normal category rate, usually around 50% to 80% after your deductible and any waiting period.
  • In network your share is based on the negotiated fee schedule, the cheapest path. Out of network you may owe the difference between the plan's payment and the dentist's full charge.

What counts as a dental emergency?
Severe tooth pain that painkillers do not control, a knocked-out or broken tooth, a lost crown or filling, a swollen abscess, jaw swelling, or uncontrolled bleeding. A small painless chip or mild sensitivity can usually wait for a regular appointment.
Can I see an emergency dentist the same day?

Should I go to the ER or a dentist for tooth pain?
See a dentist. ER doctors cannot drill, fill, root canal, or extract teeth, so an ER visit usually ends with antibiotics, a painkiller, and a referral, after a bill that often runs $400 to $1,500 or more. Use the ER only for spreading facial swelling, trouble breathing, or trauma.
Does PPO insurance cover emergency dental visits?
Usually yes. PPO plans typically cover emergency palliative treatment to relieve pain, and the underlying procedure such as an extraction or root canal at the plan's normal rate. In network is cheapest because your share is based on the negotiated fee schedule. Verify your specific plan.

What should I do for a knocked-out tooth?
Handle it by the crown, not the root, rinse it gently, and try to reseat it in the socket or keep it in milk. Get to a dentist within 30 to 60 minutes for the best chance of saving it.
