Dental savings plans for implants

Implants are where insurance is weakest: capped, delayed, or excluded. A savings plan does the opposite. It discounts the full amount immediately, with no annual maximum and no waiting period.

The honest verdict

For implants, a savings plan often beats a new PPO. An implant runs $3,000 to $6,000, while a new PPO usually makes you wait, caps the payout at $1,000 to $2,000, and may exclude implants entirely. A savings plan discounts the whole amount right away with no cap. The percentage saved is smaller than on a cleaning, but on a four-figure procedure it is usually the larger real saving.

Why insurance struggles here

Three reasons a PPO falls short on implants

1

The annual maximum

A PPO caps payouts at $1,000 to $2,000 a year. A single implant blows past that, leaving you to pay the rest at full price.

2

The waiting period

Major work on a new PPO often waits up to 12 months, then pays only half. A savings plan applies immediately.

3

The missing tooth clause

Many PPOs exclude replacing a tooth lost before the policy started. A savings plan has no such exclusion.

Questions

Implants and savings plans FAQ

Do dental savings plans cover implants?+
Savings plans do not cover implants the way insurance does, they discount them. The advantage is that the discount applies immediately, with no waiting period and no annual maximum, on a procedure many insurance plans cap or exclude. Discounts commonly run 15 to 40 percent on implants.
Is a savings plan better than insurance for implants?+
Often, yes. An implant runs 3,000 to 6,000 dollars. A new PPO usually imposes a waiting period, caps the payout at 1,000 to 2,000 dollars, and may exclude implants entirely. A savings plan discounts the full amount immediately with no cap, which is frequently the larger real saving on a four-figure procedure.
How much can I save on an implant?+
On a typical implant plus crown, discounts commonly save several hundred to over a thousand dollars depending on the plan, dentist, and region. For example, one published schedule shows an implant body plus implant crown dropping from about 4,091 dollars to about 2,724 dollars.
Can I use a savings plan after my insurance maxes out?+
Yes. Because a savings plan is not insurance, it can run alongside a PPO. Once your insurance hits its annual maximum, the savings plan keeps discounting the remaining cost with no ceiling.

One thing worth checking first

A savings plan discount comes out of the dentist's pocket

A dental savings plan lowers your price because the dentist agrees to a smaller fee. No insurance carrier pays them the difference. That discount is the office doing you a favor at its own expense, which is part of why some premium and cosmetic dentists do not take these plans.

Individual dental insurance works differently. The carrier shares the cost of care, so you pay a smaller portion and the dentist is still paid a reimbursement on top of what you owe. When you can plan ahead and time bigger treatment to a year where your coverage is high and any waiting period has cleared, that shared cost often means you pay less and the office is paid more fairly for the same work.

A savings plan still has its place. It is ready right away with no waiting period, and it has no annual maximum, which helps when you need care now or your treatment plan runs past what a policy would cap. If your timing is flexible, it is worth seeing what an individual PPO plan would cover before you decide.
