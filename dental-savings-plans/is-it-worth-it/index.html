Is a dental savings plan worth it?

Short answer: it depends on how much dental work you need this year. For one major procedure it almost always pays for itself. For two cleanings, often not. Here is the real math.

The honest verdict

A dental savings plan is worth it when you need real dental work soon, especially major or cosmetic work a PPO would delay, cap, or exclude. It is usually not the best choice if you are healthy and only need cleanings, since a PPO that covers preventive at 100 percent tends to win there. Confirm your dentist participates and estimate your year of dental needs before enrolling.

The honest pros and cons

Worth it for

  • Immediate major work, with no waiting period.
  • Anyone who already hit a PPO annual maximum.
  • Cosmetic work that insurance excludes.
  • Seniors and the recently uninsured.
  • People who want a low yearly cost.

Not worth it for

  • Healthy people who only need cleanings.
  • Anyone whose dentist does not participate.
  • People who want insurance that pays a share of claims.
  • Anyone who needs coverage that meets a requirement.

Two things people miss

A discount is not free

You still pay the discounted fee yourself. The plan never reimburses you. The value is real, but it is savings, not coverage, so compare the actual dollar price, not just the percentage off.

It only works in network

The discount applies only at participating dentists. The most common regret is buying a plan your dentist does not accept. Verify yours first, which CoverCapy can do for you.

Worth it FAQ

Is a dental savings plan worth it?+
It is usually worth it when you need real dental work soon, especially major or cosmetic work that a PPO would delay, cap, or exclude. It is usually not the best choice if you are healthy and only need routine cleanings, because a PPO that covers preventive care at 100 percent tends to win there. Confirm your dentist participates and estimate your year of dental needs before enrolling.
How much can you save?+
Savings commonly run 15 to 60 percent depending on the plan and procedure. A single crown can save roughly 500 dollars and a molar root canal roughly 700 dollars, often several times the annual membership fee. Routine cleanings save less, usually around 40 percent.
What are the downsides?+
It is not insurance and never pays for anything, so you pay the full discounted fee yourself. It only works at participating dentists, preventive care is discounted rather than free, and the discount percentage varies by procedure and ZIP. Billing and cancellation terms are worth reading before you enroll.
Who should not buy one?+
Someone who is healthy and only needs cleanings is usually better served by a PPO that covers preventive care at 100 percent. Someone whose preferred dentist does not participate, or who wants insurance that pays a share of claims or meets a coverage requirement, should also look elsewhere.

One thing worth checking first

A savings plan discount comes out of the dentist's pocket

A dental savings plan lowers your price because the dentist agrees to a smaller fee. No insurance carrier pays them the difference. That discount is the office doing you a favor at its own expense, which is part of why some premium and cosmetic dentists do not take these plans.

Individual dental insurance works differently. The carrier shares the cost of care, so you pay a smaller portion and the dentist is still paid a reimbursement on top of what you owe. When you can plan ahead and time bigger treatment to a year where your coverage is high and any waiting period has cleared, that shared cost often means you pay less and the office is paid more fairly for the same work.

A savings plan still has its place. It is ready right away with no waiting period, and it has no annual maximum, which helps when you need care now or your treatment plan runs past what a policy would cap. If your timing is flexible, it is worth seeing what an individual PPO plan would cover before you decide.
