Dental savings plans for bridges

A bridge replaces a missing tooth by anchoring to the teeth beside it, and it is a major expense that insurance tends to cap, delay, or exclude. A dental savings plan discounts it immediately, with no annual maximum.

The honest verdict

For a bridge, a savings plan is often the more practical choice. A three-unit bridge commonly runs $2,000 to $5,000, enough to exhaust a PPO annual maximum on its own, and insurance may exclude it under a missing tooth clause or make you wait. A savings plan discounts the whole amount immediately with no cap. Discounts on bridges commonly run 20 to 40 percent.

Why insurance struggles

Three reasons a PPO falls short on bridges

1

The annual maximum

A $2,000 to $5,000 bridge can exhaust a PPO annual cap by itself, leaving the balance at full price.

2

The missing tooth clause

Many PPOs exclude replacing a tooth lost before the policy began, which can rule out a bridge entirely.

3

The waiting period

Major prosthetic work on a new PPO often waits up to 12 months. A savings plan applies immediately.

Bridge or implant

Weigh the two options

A bridge

Usually less upfront than an implant, and completed faster, but it relies on the neighboring teeth. A savings plan discounts it with no cap.

An implant

Often more durable and does not touch the neighboring teeth, but costs more and takes longer. It is also discounted on a savings plan. See savings plans for implants.

Questions

Bridges and savings plans FAQ

Do dental savings plans cover bridges?+
Savings plans discount bridges rather than cover them. The discount, commonly 20 to 40 percent, applies immediately with no waiting period and no annual maximum, on a procedure many insurance plans cap or exclude.
How much does a dental bridge cost with a savings plan?+
A three-unit bridge commonly runs $2,000 to $5,000 without coverage. A savings plan discount of 20 to 40 percent can take several hundred to over a thousand dollars off, immediately, with no annual cap. Fees vary by dentist and region.
Is a savings plan or insurance better for a bridge?+
If you need the bridge soon and have no active, no-wait coverage, a savings plan usually wins, because a bridge can exhaust a PPO annual maximum and may be excluded by a missing tooth clause. If you have active insurance with room under the cap, the PPO can be better.
Does insurance cover a bridge for a tooth I already lost?+
Often not. Many PPOs have a missing tooth clause that excludes replacing a tooth lost before the policy started. A savings plan has no such clause, so it discounts the bridge regardless.
