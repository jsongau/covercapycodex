Dental savings plan and insurance glossary

The words matter. An annual maximum, a waiting period, and a deductible each change what you pay in very different ways. Here is every key term defined in plain language, so you can compare a savings plan and a PPO with clear eyes.

The honest verdict

Two terms do most of the work. An annual maximum is the ceiling on what insurance pays in a year, and a waiting period is how long a new insurance plan makes you wait for major care. Dental savings plans have neither, which is their core appeal, and they also pay nothing, which is their core tradeoff. The rest of the glossary builds on those two ideas.

Why this matters

Plain definitions, no jargon

Dental coverage is full of terms that sound similar but mean very different things. These are the ones that decide whether a savings plan or a PPO is right for you, defined plainly.

Annual maximum

The most a dental insurance plan will pay in a year, commonly $1,000 to $2,000. Once you reach it, you pay 100 percent of any further care. Dental savings plans have no annual maximum.

Waiting period

A delay, often 6 to 12 months on a new insurance plan, before it will pay for certain services, especially major work. Dental savings plans have no waiting period.

Deductible

An amount you pay out of pocket before insurance starts to pay, commonly about $50 per person. Dental savings plans have no deductible.

Coinsurance

The share of a covered procedure you pay after the deductible, for example 50 percent on major work. A savings plan has no coinsurance because it does not pay a share at all.

Premium

The recurring amount you pay for insurance, whether or not you use it. A savings plan charges an annual membership fee instead of a premium.

Membership fee

The annual fee for a dental savings plan, commonly $100 to $200 for an individual. It unlocks discounted fees but pays nothing toward your care.

Discount plan, or savings plan

A membership, not insurance, that gives you a lower, pre-negotiated price at participating dentists, which you pay directly at the visit.

Discount plan organization, or DPO

The company that operates a dental savings plan membership, such as Careington or New Benefits, sometimes behind a carrier brand.

Fee schedule

The list of discounted prices a participating dentist agrees to charge savings-plan members. You pay these fees directly.

UCR fees

Usual, customary, and reasonable fees, an insurer's reference for what a procedure should cost in an area. A percentage off UCR can be misleading, so check the actual dollar price.

Missing tooth clause

An insurance exclusion that denies coverage for replacing a tooth lost before the policy began. Savings plans have no such clause.

In-network

A dentist who participates in a given plan's network and honors its rates. A savings plan discount only applies in-network.

Preventive care

Routine cleanings, exams, and X-rays. Insurance often covers these at 100 percent, while a savings plan only discounts them.

Minimum creditable coverage

A standard for what counts as real insurance under some rules. A dental savings plan does not meet it, because it is not insurance.

Medicare dental gap

Original Medicare does not cover routine dental, leaving many seniors to pay full price or use a savings plan or standalone dental plan.

Questions

Common term questions

What is a dental annual maximum?+
The most a dental insurance plan will pay in a year, commonly $1,000 to $2,000. Once you reach it, you pay 100 percent of any further care that year. Dental savings plans have no annual maximum.
What is a dental waiting period?+
A delay, often 6 to 12 months on a new insurance plan, before it will pay for certain services, especially major work like crowns and implants. Dental savings plans have no waiting period.
What is the missing tooth clause?+
An insurance exclusion that denies coverage for replacing a tooth that was lost before the policy started. It is a common reason implants and bridges are not covered. Savings plans have no missing tooth clause.
What are UCR fees?+
Usual, customary, and reasonable fees, an insurer's reference for what a procedure should cost in an area. Because a percentage off UCR can be inflated, always check the actual dollar price, not just the percentage.
