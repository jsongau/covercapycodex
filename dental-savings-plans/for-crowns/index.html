Dental savings plans for crowns

A crown is one of the most common major procedures, and one of the most common reasons people reach for coverage in a hurry. A dental savings plan discounts it right away, with no waiting period and no annual cap.

The honest verdict

For a crown, a savings plan is often the smartest quick option. A typical crown runs $1,000 to $1,500, and a savings plan can drop a $1,500 crown to about $949, immediately, with no waiting period. A brand-new PPO usually makes you wait up to 12 months and then pays only half up to the annual cap, so in year one the savings plan frequently wins. If you already have active, no-wait insurance with room under the cap, the PPO can be better.

Questions

Crowns and savings plans FAQ

How much does a crown cost with a dental savings plan?+
A typical crown runs $1,000 to $1,500 without coverage. With a savings plan, a $1,500 crown can drop to about $949, a saving of roughly $551, applied immediately with no waiting period. Fees vary by dentist and region.
Is a savings plan or insurance better for a crown?+
If you need the crown soon and have no active, no-wait coverage, a savings plan usually wins, because a new PPO makes you wait up to 12 months and then pays only half up to the cap. If you already have active insurance with room under the annual maximum, the PPO can be better.
Can I get a crown covered immediately?+
A savings plan discounts a crown immediately with no waiting period. Some no-waiting-period insurance plans also exist, usually at a higher premium. Confirm your dentist participates before enrolling in either.
Can I use a savings plan after my insurance maxes out on a crown?+
Yes. A savings plan is not insurance, so it can run alongside a PPO. Once you hit the annual maximum, the savings plan keeps discounting the remaining cost with no ceiling.

One thing worth checking first

A savings plan discount comes out of the dentist's pocket

A dental savings plan lowers your price because the dentist agrees to a smaller fee. No insurance carrier pays them the difference. That discount is the office doing you a favor at its own expense, which is part of why some premium and cosmetic dentists do not take these plans.

Individual dental insurance works differently. The carrier shares the cost of care, so you pay a smaller portion and the dentist is still paid a reimbursement on top of what you owe. When you can plan ahead and time bigger treatment to a year where your coverage is high and any waiting period has cleared, that shared cost often means you pay less and the office is paid more fairly for the same work.

A savings plan still has its place. It is ready right away with no waiting period, and it has no annual maximum, which helps when you need care now or your treatment plan runs past what a policy would cap. If your timing is flexible, it is worth seeing what an individual PPO plan would cover before you decide.
