The honest verdict

UnitedHealthcare Dental Savings is appealing for its low monthly cost and high average savings claim, and it bundles vision, hearing, and prescription discounts. The catch is the network. At about 50,000 providers it is materially smaller than the Aetna Dental Access network behind many competitor cards, so it only pays off if your dentist is in it. Verify that first.

What it is

One product, two names

1

Dental Savings Complete

Sold on uhc.com as Dental Savings Complete and on UnitedHealthOne as the Dental Discount Card, the same underlying membership. You pay the discounted rate directly to the provider.

2

No insurance limits

No copays, deductibles, annual limits, or maximums, and no waiting period. The ID card may take up to 72 hours after enrollment.

3

Bundled discounts

Vision and hearing discounts, plus a prescription discount card through Optum Perks. These are discount programs, not insurance.

4

Not UHC dental insurance

UnitedHealthcare also sells real dental insurance with premiums, deductibles, and annual maximums. This discount plan is a separate, non insurance product.

Who runs it

An in house network, not Aetna or Careington

This is the genuine differentiator, and the tradeoff worth understanding.

1
Administered by Dental Benefit Providers, Inc.A UnitedHealthcare company. UHC owns both the network and the discount fee schedule, rather than licensing someone else's.

vs a PPO

When it beats a PPO, and when it does not

UHC Dental Savings wins when

  • You want the lowest monthly cost and your dentist is in the network.
  • You need work excluded or already maxed out under your PPO, with no annual cap.
  • You want bundled vision, hearing, and prescription discounts.
  • You need care now, with no waiting period.

A PPO wins when

  • You want the plan to actually pay a share of claims.
  • You mainly need preventive cleanings a PPO covers at 100 percent.
  • Your dentist is outside the smaller 50,000 provider network.

Questions

UnitedHealthcare Dental Savings FAQ

Is Dental Savings Complete insurance?+
No. Dental Savings Complete, also sold as the Dental Discount Card, is a membership discount program, not insurance. It is administered by Dental Benefit Providers, Inc. Members pay the discounted rate directly to the provider. There are no copays, deductibles, annual limits, or maximums, and it is separate from UnitedHealthcare's dental insurance.
How much does it cost?+
It is commonly about 11.99 dollars a month for an individual and about 14.99 dollars a month for a family, with a one time enrollment fee of around 20 dollars. Pricing can vary by state, so confirm the current price on the official site before enrolling.
How much can I save?+
The plan advertises potential savings of an average of 53 percent off many common dental services, with no annual maximums or limits and no waiting period. Savings vary by provider and service.
How big is the network?+
It uses the UnitedHealthcare Dental Savings Network with more than 50,000 participating providers. This is a proprietary, in house network and is smaller than the Aetna Dental Access network behind many competitor discount cards, so confirming your dentist participates matters even more.
What extras are included?+
It bundles vision and hearing discounts and a prescription discount card through Optum Perks. These are discount programs, not insurance.

One thing worth checking first

A savings plan discount comes out of the dentist's pocket

A dental savings plan lowers your price because the dentist agrees to a smaller fee. No insurance carrier pays them the difference. That discount is the office doing you a favor at its own expense, which is part of why some premium and cosmetic dentists do not take these plans.

Individual dental insurance works differently. The carrier shares the cost of care, so you pay a smaller portion and the dentist is still paid a reimbursement on top of what you owe. When you can plan ahead and time bigger treatment to a year where your coverage is high and any waiting period has cleared, that shared cost often means you pay less and the office is paid more fairly for the same work.

A savings plan still has its place. It is ready right away with no waiting period, and it has no annual maximum, which helps when you need care now or your treatment plan runs past what a policy would cap. If your timing is flexible, it is worth seeing what an individual PPO plan would cover before you decide.
