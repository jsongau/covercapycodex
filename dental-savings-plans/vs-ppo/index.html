Dental savings plan vs PPO

Two good tools for two different jobs. A PPO can make routine care nearly free but caps you and makes you wait. A savings plan never makes anything free, but it works immediately and never caps you. Here is how to tell which fits your year.

The short answer

Choose a savings plan when you need real work soon, especially major or cosmetic work a PPO would delay, cap, or exclude, or when you have no coverage. Choose a PPO when you are healthy and mostly need cleanings, since a PPO that covers preventive at 100 percent tends to win there. Sometimes the smartest move is both. Real dollars

Three worked examples

A new crown

A $1,500 crown costs about $949 with a savings plan, saving roughly $551 today. A new PPO usually makes you wait up to 12 months for major work, then pays only half, so in year one the savings plan often wins.

A single implant

An implant runs $3,000 to $6,000. A new PPO imposes a waiting period, caps the payout at $1,000 to $2,000, and may exclude it. The savings plan discounts it immediately with no cap, usually the larger real saving.

Two cleanings a year

Healthy and just need checkups? A PPO that covers preventive at 100 percent makes routine care effectively free after the premium. A savings plan only discounts cleanings about 40 percent, so the PPO tends to win.

Who fits where

Match it to your situation

A savings plan fits when

  • You need a crown, root canal, or implant soon.
  • You want cosmetic work a PPO will not cover.
  • You already hit your PPO annual maximum.
  • You are on Medicare or recently lost coverage.
  • You want the lowest yearly cost and no waiting.

A PPO fits when

  • You are healthy and mainly need cleanings.
  • You need a large covered procedure that fits under the annual maximum.
  • Your employer pays part of the premium.
  • You want an insurer to share the cost and protect against a bad year.

Often overlooked

You can use both

Stack them

Keep a PPO for free preventive care and the annual-max payout, and add a savings plan to keep discounting once you hit the cap. A savings plan is not insurance, so the two can run side by side.

Bridge with one

Use a savings plan now for urgent work, then move to a PPO at the next enrollment window for ongoing coverage. This is the get cover today, see a dentist tomorrow path.

Questions

Savings plan vs PPO FAQ

Is a dental savings plan better than a PPO?+
Neither is better in the abstract. A savings plan wins when you need major or cosmetic work soon, when you have no coverage, or when you have already hit a PPO annual maximum, because it works immediately with no cap. A PPO wins for healthy people who mainly need preventive care, since a PPO often covers cleanings at 100 percent while a savings plan only discounts them.
What is the main difference?+
A savings plan is a discount membership: you pay a discounted price yourself, with no annual maximum and no waiting periods, but nothing is paid for you. A PPO is insurance: it pays a share of covered procedures after a deductible, often covering preventive care at 100 percent, but it caps payouts at an annual maximum and usually makes you wait for major work.
Can I use both together?+
Yes. A savings plan is not insurance, so it can run alongside a PPO. This is especially useful once you hit your PPO annual maximum, because the savings plan keeps discounting with no ceiling for the rest of the year.
Which is cheaper?+
A savings plan usually has a lower yearly cost, commonly about 100 to 200 dollars for an individual versus roughly 240 to 600 dollars in PPO premiums. But cheaper to join does not always mean cheaper overall. For someone who only needs cleanings, a PPO that covers preventive at 100 percent can cost less in practice.

One thing worth checking first

A savings plan discount comes out of the dentist's pocket

A dental savings plan lowers your price because the dentist agrees to a smaller fee. No insurance carrier pays them the difference. That discount is the office doing you a favor at its own expense, which is part of why some premium and cosmetic dentists do not take these plans.

Individual dental insurance works differently. The carrier shares the cost of care, so you pay a smaller portion and the dentist is still paid a reimbursement on top of what you owe. When you can plan ahead and time bigger treatment to a year where your coverage is high and any waiting period has cleared, that shared cost often means you pay less and the office is paid more fairly for the same work.

A savings plan still has its place. It is ready right away with no waiting period, and it has no annual maximum, which helps when you need care now or your treatment plan runs past what a policy would cap. If your timing is flexible, it is worth seeing what an individual PPO plan would cover before you decide.
