## 2. Patient-Friendly Definition

A CoverCapy Rating is an independent 0-to-100 score that summarizes how useful a PPO dental plan is likely to be for a typical adult patient. It weighs four factors: how many types of care the plan covers, how high its annual benefit cap is, how long you wait before major services kick in, and how much coverage you actually get for each dollar of monthly premium. The higher the score, the more well-rounded the plan tends to be across all four of those dimensions.

---

## 3. How the Score Is Computed

The rating combines four weighted sub-scores. Each sub-score is normalized to 0-100 before being combined, so no single factor dominates unfairly.

### Factor 1: Coverage Breadth (30 points)
This sub-score reflects how many categories of dental care the plan includes. Plans that cover only preventive care score low. Plans that also cover basic restorative work (fillings, extractions) score higher. Plans that add major restorative (crowns, bridges, dentures), orthodontics, and implants score highest. A plan must cover at least preventive and basic restorative to score above 50 on this factor.

### Factor 2: Annual Maximum Benefit (25 points)
Most PPO plans cap how much they pay out in a calendar year. A $1,000 annual maximum is near the industry floor and scores poorly. A $2,000 maximum scores near the midpoint. Plans with $3,000 or higher caps, or with no annual maximum, score best. This factor matters most if you anticipate needing crowns, root canals, or multiple fillings in the same year.

### Factor 3: Average Waiting Period (25 points)
Many plans impose waiting periods before they cover certain services, especially major restorative work. A plan with no waiting periods on any service scores 100 on this factor. A plan with a 12-month wait on major work scores near 50. Plans with waits longer than 12 months, or waits on basic services like fillings, score low. This factor is especially important if you need care soon after enrolling.

### Factor 4: Value per Dollar of Premium (20 points)
This factor compares the plan's potential benefit payout against its monthly cost. It is calculated by dividing the annual maximum benefit by the annualized premium, then adjusting for coverage breadth. A plan charging $50 per month with a $2,000 annual maximum scores better than a plan charging $80 per month with the same maximum. Plans with very low premiums but very limited coverage are penalized on this factor because the coverage breadth adjustment pulls the value score down.

### Combined Score
```
CoverCapy Rating = (Breadth x 0.30) + (Annual Max x 0.25) + (Waiting Period x 0.25) + (Value per Dollar x 0.20)
```
The result is rounded to the nearest whole number and displayed on a 0-100 scale. A score of 70 or above indicates a well-rounded plan. Scores below 50 typically signal a plan with at least one significant structural limitation.

## 4. Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: The highest-rated plan is always the cheapest.**
Not true. The value-per-dollar factor rewards efficiency, but a high overall rating can come from a plan with a generous annual maximum or wide coverage breadth, even if its premium is above average. Two plans can score similarly while differing by $30 a month in premium. Always check the premium column alongside the rating.

**Misconception 2: A high rating means the plan covers everything.**
The rating reflects relative strength across four measurable factors. It does not guarantee that every procedure you need is covered, nor does it reflect your specific dentist's in-network status. A plan rated 85 may still exclude cosmetic services, orthodontics for adults, or implants. Always read the summary of benefits for the services you expect to use.

**Misconception 3: Ratings change only once a year.**
CoverCapy recalculates ratings whenever a carrier files updated plan documents with CoverCapy's data team. Plan terms, annual maximums, and waiting periods can change at open enrollment or when a carrier updates its product lineup. The rating shown reflects the most recently verified plan data on file.

## 5. Real-World Example

**Plan A: DeltaCare Flex 2000**
- Coverage breadth: Preventive + basic restorative + major restorative (no ortho, no implants)
- Annual maximum: $2,000
- Waiting period: 6 months on major restorative
- Monthly premium: $48

**Plan B: Anthem PPO Select 1500**
- Coverage breadth: Preventive + basic restorative only
- Annual maximum: $1,500
- Waiting period: None
- Monthly premium: $34

**How the scores differ:**

Plan A scores 71. Its annual maximum and coverage breadth are both strong, but the 6-month wait on major work costs it points on the waiting-period factor.

Plan B scores 58. No waiting period is a significant advantage and pulls that sub-score to 100, but the lower annual maximum and limited coverage breadth hold the overall score back. The lower premium helps on value per dollar, but there is simply less benefit on offer.

**Why this matters in practice:** If you need a crown soon after enrolling, Plan A's 6-month wait is a real obstacle despite its higher rating. If you only expect cleanings and occasional fillings in the next year, Plan B's lower premium and no waiting period may suit you better even with the lower rating. The rating gives you a starting point; the details help you decide.

## 6. Why It Matters

Shopping for dental insurance without a standardized score means reading dozens of summary-of-benefits PDFs and doing your own math on annual maximums, waiting periods, and coinsurance percentages. The CoverCapy Rating compresses that research into a single number so you can quickly identify which plans deserve a closer look and which ones are likely to underdeliver before you even open the fine print.

## 7. Related Terms

- **annual-maximum** — The dollar cap a plan pays toward your dental care in a calendar year; one of the four factors in the CoverCapy Rating.
- **coinsurance** — The percentage of a covered procedure's cost that the plan pays after you meet your deductible; not directly in the rating formula but affects real out-of-pocket costs.
- **waiting-period** — The months you must be enrolled before certain services are covered; weighted at 25 points in the rating.
- **ppo** — Preferred Provider Organization; the plan structure CoverCapy rates. PPO plans let you see any licensed dentist, with lower costs at in-network providers.

## 9. FAQ

**Q: Does a higher CoverCapy Rating always mean I will pay less out of pocket?**
A: Not necessarily. The rating measures plan quality across four structural factors, not your personal out-of-pocket costs. Your actual costs depend on which procedures you need, whether your dentist is in-network, and what coinsurance percentages apply to those services. A highly rated plan can still leave you with significant out-of-pocket costs if you need extensive major work beyond the annual maximum.

**Q: Can a carrier pay CoverCapy to improve their rating?**
A: No. CoverCapy ratings are calculated algorithmically from publicly filed plan data and carrier benefit documents. No carrier, broker, or insurer has any ability to influence a plan's score. CoverCapy does not accept payment from carriers in exchange for placement or higher ratings. This independence is the foundation of how the ratings are built.

**Q: How often is my plan's rating updated?**
A: Ratings are recalculated each time CoverCapy's data team verifies updated plan documents from a carrier. This typically happens at the start of each plan year, but can occur mid-year if a carrier modifies its plan terms. The date of the most recent data verification is shown on each plan's detail page.

## 10. CoverCapy Angle

CoverCapy ratings are editorially independent. No carrier pays for a higher score, no advertiser can influence ranking, and the algorithm applies the same four-factor formula to every plan in the database regardless of who underwrites it. The goal is to give patients the same objective analysis a benefits consultant would run through before advising a client, delivered in under 10 seconds on a mobile screen.

This independence is what makes the rating useful as a starting filter rather than a marketing tool. When you see a plan rated 78, that number came from the plan's own filed documents, not from the carrier's sales team.
