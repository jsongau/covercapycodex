## 1. Featured Snippet Sentence

The allowed amount is the maximum dollar figure your dental insurance carrier will recognize for a specific procedure when billed by an in-network dentist -- a negotiated cap that is almost always lower than the dentist's full fee, and the number from which your coinsurance and out-of-pocket share are calculated.

## 2. Patient-Friendly Definition

The allowed amount is the maximum fee your insurance plan has agreed to recognize for a dental procedure performed by an in-network provider. Your dentist may charge more, but the carrier will only apply your benefits against this pre-negotiated figure. Because the in-network dentist has contracted to accept the allowed amount as payment in full, you are protected from being billed for the difference between the full fee and that cap.

---

## 3. Clinical and Technical Context

**Fee schedule negotiation.** Carriers negotiate allowed amounts directly with dental practices through provider contracts. The result is a fee schedule -- a grid of CDT (Code on Dental Terminology) procedure codes paired with the maximum the carrier will pay for each code in a given ZIP code or region. Larger networks and high-volume carriers typically negotiate steeper discounts, which is why the same crown procedure can have very different allowed amounts across Delta Dental, Aetna, Cigna, and MetLife PPO networks.

**Relationship to ADA/CDT codes.** Every covered dental service maps to a five-digit CDT procedure code (for example, D2750 for a porcelain-fused-to-metal crown on a posterior tooth). The allowed amount is always indexed to the CDT code, not to a generic service name. When a claim is submitted, the carrier looks up the CDT code in its fee schedule and applies the contracted allowed amount for that specific code, in that specific network tier.

**How coinsurance applies.** Coinsurance is calculated as a percentage of the allowed amount, not the dentist's submitted fee. If your plan pays 50% coinsurance on major restorative work and the allowed amount for a crown is $1,100, the plan pays $550 and your share is $550 -- regardless of whether the dentist originally charged $1,800. This is the single most important arithmetic distinction patients miss when reading an Explanation of Benefits (EOB).

**In-network vs. out-of-network.** Out-of-network dentists have not signed a fee schedule agreement, so carriers often apply a different benchmark -- typically the UCR (Usual, Customary, and Reasonable) fee -- which may be set lower than the dentist's actual charge. That gap can legally be billed back to the patient as a balance bill, meaning your out-of-pocket cost can exceed what the EOB appears to show.

## 4. Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "The allowed amount is what the dentist charges."**
The allowed amount is what the carrier has pre-negotiated the dentist will *accept*, not what the dentist originally billed. The submitted fee on a claim is almost always higher. Seeing a line that says "Billed: $1,800 / Allowed: $1,100" on an EOB does not mean the patient owes the $700 difference -- it means the dentist has contractually written it off.

**Misconception 2: "My coinsurance is a percentage of the full billed fee."**
Coinsurance is applied to the allowed amount only. A plan that pays "50% of covered services" pays 50% of the allowed amount -- not 50% of whatever the dentist charged. Patients who calculate their expected share against the full fee will overestimate their cost when in-network and potentially underestimate it when out-of-network.

**Misconception 3: "All PPO plans use the same allowed amounts."**
PPO networks negotiate separately and independently. The allowed amount for a crown in the Delta Dental Premier network, the Delta Dental PPO network, the Cigna DPPO network, and the MetLife PDP network can differ by hundreds of dollars -- even for the same dentist's office if that practice participates in multiple networks.

## 5. Real-World Example

**Procedure:** Porcelain-fused-to-high-noble crown on molar (CDT D2750)

| Line item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Dentist's full submitted fee | $1,800 |
| Carrier's allowed amount (in-network fee schedule) | $1,100 |
| Amount written off by dentist (contractual adjustment) | $700 |
| Plan pays: 50% coinsurance applied to allowed amount | $550 |
| Patient responsibility (after coinsurance, before deductible) | $550 |
| Patient responsibility if $100 deductible already met | $550 |
| Patient responsibility if $100 deductible not yet met | $650 |

Key takeaway: the patient's $550 share is 50% of $1,100 (the allowed amount), not 50% of $1,800 (the full fee). Choosing an in-network provider saved this patient $700 in potential balance billing before the coinsurance calculation even begins.

## 6. Why It Matters

The allowed amount is the single number that controls what you actually pay at the dental office -- every coinsurance percentage, deductible application, and annual maximum calculation runs through it, so misreading it by even a small margin can throw off a patient's budget by hundreds of dollars on a single procedure. Confirming the allowed amount for a planned procedure *before* the appointment, not after the EOB arrives, is the most reliable way to avoid surprise dental bills.

## 7. Related Terms

- **coinsurance** -- the percentage of the allowed amount the patient pays after the deductible is met
- **in-network** -- a dentist who has signed a fee schedule contract and therefore accepts the allowed amount as payment in full
- **ada-fee** -- the full, undiscounted fee a dentist sets before any carrier negotiation
- **cdt** -- the CDT procedure code system that maps each dental service to the allowed amount on the fee schedule
- **balance-billing** -- the practice of billing a patient for the gap between a provider's full fee and the carrier's allowed amount, which in-network dentists are contractually prohibited from doing

## 9. FAQ

**Q: If my dentist charges $1,800 for a crown but the allowed amount is $1,100, do I owe the $700 difference?**
A: Not if your dentist is in-network. In-network providers sign a contract agreeing to accept the allowed amount as payment in full and to write off the remaining balance as a contractual adjustment. You pay only your share of the $1,100 allowed amount. If your dentist is out-of-network, that $700 gap -- called a balance bill -- can legally be charged to you, which is why network status matters so much before scheduling major work.

**Q: Does my deductible apply to the allowed amount or the full fee?**
A: Your deductible applies to the allowed amount. If your plan has a $100 deductible and the allowed amount for your procedure is $1,100, you pay the first $100 of that $1,100 figure, then coinsurance kicks in on the remaining $1,000. The full submitted fee of $1,800 is irrelevant to how your deductible and coinsurance are applied.

**Q: Can I find out the allowed amount before my appointment?**
A: Yes, and you should. Call the member services number on your insurance card and ask for a pre-treatment estimate for the specific CDT codes your dentist plans to use. The carrier will send a written estimate showing the allowed amount, your expected coinsurance, and how much of your deductible remains. This estimate is not a guarantee of payment, but it gives you an accurate picture of your out-of-pocket cost before the procedure happens.
