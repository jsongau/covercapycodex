## 1. Featured Snippet Sentence (under 50 words)

CDT codes are five-character billing codes that identify every dental procedure on your insurance claim or treatment estimate. They all start with the letter D, followed by four digits. Your insurer uses these codes, not your dentist's office notes, to calculate exactly what it will pay.

---

## 2. Patient-Friendly Definition (2-3 sentences)

CDT stands for Current Dental Terminology, a standardized code set published by the American Dental Association that every dental office and insurance company in the United States uses. Each code starts with the letter D and is followed by four digits, for example D0120 for a periodic exam or D2740 for a porcelain crown. When a code appears on your explanation of benefits or your pre-treatment estimate, it tells your insurer precisely which procedure was performed so it can apply the correct coverage rule.

---

## 3. Clinical and Technical Context

### How the CDT system works

The ADA owns and updates the CDT code set annually. Dentists and their billing staff assign one or more CDT codes to every service performed, then submit those codes on a standardized claim form (the ADA Dental Claim Form) to the patient's insurer. The insurer's fee schedule maps each CDT code to an allowed amount, which is the maximum it will reimburse for that procedure at in-network offices.

### The nine major categories (D0 through D9)

| Category | Range | What it covers |
|----------|-------|----------------|
| Diagnostic | D0100-D0999 | Exams, X-rays, screenings |
| Preventive | D1000-D1999 | Cleanings, sealants, fluoride |
| Restorative | D2000-D2999 | Fillings, crowns, inlays |
| Endodontics | D3000-D3999 | Root canals, pulp therapy |
| Periodontics | D4000-D4999 | Gum treatments, scaling |
| Prosthodontics (removable) | D5000-D5899 | Dentures, partials |
| Maxillofacial prosthetics | D5900-D5999 | Specialized prosthetics |
| Implant services | D6000-D6199 | Implant placement, restoration |
| Prosthodontics (fixed) | D6200-D6999 | Bridges, fixed partial dentures |
| Oral and maxillofacial surgery | D7000-D7999 | Extractions, jaw surgery |
| Orthodontics | D8000-D8999 | Braces, aligners |
| Adjunctive general services | D9000-D9999 | Anesthesia, consultations |

### Why the same code pays the same at every in-network office

Your PPO plan assigns a contracted fee to each CDT code for all in-network dentists in your area. If your plan allows $1,200 for D2740, it pays that amount whether you go to a solo practice in your neighborhood or a large group practice across town. The code, not the dentist's standard fee, drives the reimbursement calculation. This is why comparing estimates by CDT code is far more reliable than comparing dollar totals alone.

## 4. Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "A higher CDT code number means a more expensive procedure."**
The numeric range loosely groups procedures by category (diagnostic, restorative, surgical, etc.), but the number itself does not rank cost or complexity. D0120, a routine periodic exam, costs far less than D9230, nitrous oxide analgesia, even though D9230 has a higher number.

**Misconception 2: "If two offices list the same CDT code, they charge the same price."**
In-network dentists agree to a contracted fee for each code, but their standard (pre-discount) fees before the PPO adjustment can vary widely. The CDT code controls what your plan pays; it does not freeze what the dentist originally billed.

**Misconception 3: "CDT codes are the same as ICD-10 medical diagnosis codes."**
CDT codes describe dental procedures. ICD-10 codes describe diagnoses and are used primarily in medical billing. Some dental claims also include ICD-10 codes when a procedure has a medical necessity component, but the two systems are separate. Your dental EOB will show D-codes for procedures, not ICD-10 codes.

## 5. Real-World Example: D2740 Porcelain Crown at Two Offices

Maria needs a crown on her upper molar. Her dentist gives her an estimate showing D2740 (crown, porcelain/ceramic substrate). She wants a second opinion and gets an estimate from another in-network office. It also shows D2740.

| | Office A | Office B |
|--|---------|---------|
| Standard fee | $1,650 | $1,420 |
| PPO contracted fee (D2740) | $1,200 | $1,200 |
| Plan pays (50% after deductible) | $600 | $600 |
| Maria's out-of-pocket | $600 | $600 |

Because both offices are in-network and her plan's allowed amount for D2740 is $1,200 regardless of location, Maria's cost is identical at both offices. The standard fee difference of $230 is irrelevant once the PPO write-off is applied. Knowing the CDT code let Maria compare apples to apples instead of getting confused by two different dollar totals.

## 6. Why It Matters (2 sentences)

Understanding CDT codes lets you verify that what your dentist submitted to insurance is exactly what was done, which is the first line of defense against billing errors and upcoding. When you shop for a second opinion or a new dentist, asking for estimates by CDT code is the only way to make a true cost comparison across different offices.

## 7. Related Terms

- **ada-fee** (ADA fee survey) — the national benchmark dentists use to set their standard fees before PPO discounts
- **allowed-amount** — the maximum your insurer will pay for a specific CDT code at an in-network provider
- **in-network** — a dentist who has agreed to the insurer's contracted fee schedule for each CDT code
- **coinsurance** — your percentage share of the allowed amount after your deductible is met, applied per CDT code on your EOB

## 9. FAQ (3 Q&A pairs)

**Q: Where do I find the CDT code for a procedure I'm having done?**
A: Ask your dentist's front desk for a pre-treatment estimate, sometimes called a predetermination or prior authorization form. It will list every planned procedure alongside its CDT code and your plan's projected payment. You can also check your explanation of benefits after the claim is processed, which shows the code, the allowed amount, and your share of the cost.

**Q: Can a dentist use the wrong CDT code on a claim?**
A: Yes, and it happens more often than most patients realize, usually from clerical error rather than intent. If the code on your EOB does not match the service you received, contact your dentist's billing department first. If the issue is not resolved, you can file a complaint with your state dental board or your insurer's fraud tip line. Comparing your post-visit EOB to the original estimate code by code is the fastest way to catch a discrepancy.

**Q: Does every CDT code have the same coverage percentage under my plan?**
A: No. Most PPO plans group CDT codes into benefit tiers, typically called diagnostic and preventive, basic restorative, and major restorative. Preventive codes like D1110 (adult cleaning) are often covered at 100 percent, while a major code like D2740 (porcelain crown) might be covered at only 50 percent. Your plan's summary of benefits lists each tier and its coverage percentage, and your EOB shows which tier each submitted code fell into.
