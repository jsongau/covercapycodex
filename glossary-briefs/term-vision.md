## 2. Patient-Friendly Definition

A vision add-on bundles routine eye-exam coverage and an eyewear allowance -- typically for prescription glasses or contact lenses -- directly with your dental plan so you pay one premium instead of managing two separate policies. The added benefit usually covers one eye exam per calendar year, plus a set dollar credit toward frames, lenses, or contacts. Humana Extend is a well-known example: it layers a vision benefit on top of its PPO dental coverage, making it one of the most accessible bundled options available to individuals and families.

## 3. Clinical and Technical Context

### Which PPO carriers include or offer vision

| Carrier / Plan | Vision Included | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Humana Extend | Yes (bundled) | Eye exam + $150 eyewear allowance per year |
| Ameritas PrimeStar Grow | Yes (bundled) | Exam + frames/contacts credit |
| Delta Dental + EyeMed (rider) | Optional rider | Available through select employer groups |
| Cigna Dental + Vision | Optional bundle | Individual market availability varies by state |
| UnitedHealthcare Dental + Vision | Optional bundle | Sold together; underwritten separately |
| Spirit Dental + Vision | Yes (bundled) | No waiting periods on vision benefit |

Stand-alone vision carriers (VSP, EyeMed, Davis Vision) typically charge $13 to $25 per month for individual coverage. When a dental plan bundles vision for an incremental $8 to $15 premium increase, the bundle almost always wins on cost per covered benefit.

### Typical vision benefit amounts

- **Eye exam:** 100% covered (one exam per calendar year at an in-network optometrist)
- **Frames allowance:** $100 to $200 per year; patient pays the overage
- **Lenses:** single-vision covered in full; progressive lenses often carry a copay of $50 to $80
- **Contact lens allowance:** $100 to $150 per year in lieu of eyewear

### When bundling beats buying separately

Bundling wins in three common scenarios:

1. **Individual or self-employed buyers** who cannot access employer group rates and face retail pricing on stand-alone vision policies.
2. **Families** where the per-person premium on separate plans adds up faster than the incremental cost of a bundled upgrade.
3. **People with light-to-moderate vision correction needs** (annual exam plus modest frames) who would spend more out of pocket buying two policies with two separate deductibles.

Bundling may underperform for patients with high-prescription specialty lens needs or who require multiple fittings per year, because stand-alone vision plans from VSP or EyeMed often carry deeper in-network discounts on premium lens options.

## 4. Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Dental and vision are always sold together."**
They are not. Most major dental PPOs -- Delta Dental, MetLife, Guardian -- are dental-only by default. Vision must be added as a rider or purchased through a separate carrier. True bundled plans like Humana Extend are the minority, not the standard.

**Misconception 2: "A vision add-on covers LASIK or other elective procedures."**
Standard vision add-ons cover routine exams and corrective eyewear only. LASIK, PRK, and other refractive surgeries are considered elective and are excluded from virtually all add-on benefits. Some carriers offer a separate LASIK discount program, which is not the same as insurance coverage.

**Misconception 3: "If I don't use the vision benefit, it rolls over."**
Vision allowances on add-on plans almost universally operate on a use-it-or-lose-it basis within the plan year. Unused exam benefits and eyewear credits do not accumulate across years.

**Option B -- Humana Extend bundle:**
- Humana Extend PPO with vision: $44/mo
- Includes: one annual eye exam (100% covered, in-network), $150 eyewear allowance, and full Delta-equivalent dental PPO benefits

**Result:** Maria saves $132 per year and eliminates one insurance relationship. The only tradeoff: Humana Extend's in-network optometrist list is smaller than VSP's standalone network, so she confirmed her optometrist was in-network before enrolling.

## 6. Why It Matters

Dental and vision problems are among the most common reasons adults delay preventive care -- overwhelmingly because of cost, not access. Consolidating both benefits into a single bundled premium lowers the perceived barrier to staying current on exams, which means fewer missed early-stage diagnoses (dry eye, glaucoma, early decay) that become expensive later. For CoverCapy users shopping individual-market coverage, a vision add-on is often the highest-value upgrade available for under $15 per month.

## 9. FAQ

**Q1: Can I add a vision benefit to any dental PPO plan?**
Not all dental PPO plans offer a vision rider. Bundled vision is most common on plans sold directly to individuals and families (individual market), including Humana Extend, Ameritas PrimeStar, and Spirit Dental. Group plans through an employer may offer vision as a separate election during open enrollment. If your current dental plan does not include vision, switching to a bundled plan at your next renewal is typically more cost-effective than buying a stand-alone vision policy at retail rates.

**Q2: How much does a vision add-on typically cost per month?**
On bundled individual plans, the incremental cost of adding vision ranges from $8 to $18 per month above the dental-only premium. Given that a single in-network eye exam without insurance runs $100 to $175 in most US markets, the add-on pays for itself after one exam per year for the majority of policyholders. Eyewear allowances of $100 to $200 per year make the math even more favorable for patients who need new glasses or contacts annually.

**Q3: Does a vision add-on cover my children's eye exams too?**
Family dental and vision bundles typically cover dependent children up to age 26, the same as dental benefits under ACA rules. Children's eye exams are often covered at a higher benefit level than adults on family plans -- some bundles cover pediatric exams at 100% with no copay -- because the ACA classifies pediatric vision as an essential health benefit. Always confirm the dependent age cutoff and pediatric benefit structure when comparing family bundle options.
