## 2. 20 FAQ ENTRIES FOR THE GLOSSARY PAGE

Each answer opens with a standalone fact sentence, runs under 60 words, uses no em-dashes, and is written to be extracted as a featured snippet.

### FAQ 1
**Term:** PPO Dental Plan
**Q:** What is a PPO dental plan?
**A:** A PPO (Preferred Provider Organization) dental plan is the most common type of dental insurance, covering over 80% of plans in the US. You can visit any licensed dentist, but you pay less when you choose an in-network provider. There are no referrals required, and most major procedures are covered after your deductible is met.

---

### FAQ 2
**Term:** PPO Dental Plan
**Q:** Can I see a dentist who is not in my PPO network?
**A:** Yes. PPO plans allow you to visit out-of-network dentists, but your out-of-pocket costs will be higher. In-network dentists have agreed to negotiated rates, so your coinsurance and copay apply to a lower base fee. Out-of-network visits may also expose you to balance billing for the difference above your plan's allowed amount.

---

### FAQ 3
**Term:** Dental Deductible
**Q:** What is a dental insurance deductible?
**A:** A dental deductible is the fixed dollar amount you pay out of pocket each year before your insurance begins covering basic and major procedures. Most individual PPO plans carry a $50 deductible. Preventive care such as exams and cleanings is typically covered at 100% with no deductible required.

---

### FAQ 4
**Term:** Dental Deductible
**Q:** Does the dental deductible reset every year?
**A:** Yes. Dental deductibles reset at the start of each plan year, which is usually January 1 for calendar-year plans. The reset means you must meet your deductible again before insurance pays for basic and major services in the new year. Preventive visits are not affected by the deductible reset.

---

### FAQ 5
**Term:** Annual Maximum
**Q:** What is a dental insurance annual maximum?
**A:** A dental annual maximum is the total dollar amount your plan will pay toward covered services in one plan year. Most PPO plans set this between $1,000 and $2,000, though new regulations taking effect in 2026 require many plans to raise the minimum to $2,500. Once you hit the maximum, you pay 100% of remaining costs until the year resets.

---

### FAQ 6
**Term:** Annual Maximum
**Q:** Does preventive dental care count toward the annual maximum?
**A:** In most PPO plans, preventive care such as cleanings and exams does not count against your annual maximum. That means your full benefit cap is preserved for fillings, crowns, and other restorative work. Check your Summary of Benefits to confirm how your specific plan handles preventive services.

---

### FAQ 7
**Term:** Coinsurance
**Q:** What is dental coinsurance?
**A:** Dental coinsurance is the percentage of a covered procedure's cost that you pay after your deductible is met. If your plan has 20% coinsurance on fillings, you pay $40 on a $200 filling and your insurer pays $160. Coinsurance rates vary by procedure category: typically 0% for preventive, 20% for basic, and 50% for major work.

---

### FAQ 8
**Term:** Coinsurance
**Q:** What is the 100-80-50 rule in dental insurance?
**A:** The 100-80-50 rule describes how most PPO dental plans split costs by procedure type. Preventive care such as cleanings is covered at 100%. Basic services such as fillings are covered at 80%, meaning you pay 20%. Major procedures such as crowns and bridges are covered at 50%, leaving you responsible for the other half.

---

### FAQ 9
**Term:** Waiting Period
**Q:** What is a dental insurance waiting period?
**A:** A dental insurance waiting period is the span of time after enrollment before coverage begins for certain procedures. Preventive care is covered immediately under nearly all plans. Basic services such as fillings often carry a 3 to 6 month wait. Major procedures such as crowns may require 6 to 12 months before the plan will pay.

---

### FAQ 10
**Term:** Waiting Period
**Q:** Why do dental insurance plans have waiting periods?
**A:** Waiting periods prevent people from buying insurance only when they need an expensive procedure and then canceling coverage afterward. By requiring a waiting period for basic and major services, insurers reduce adverse selection and keep premiums lower for all plan members. Preventive care, which helps avoid costly treatment, is always covered immediately.

---

### FAQ 11
**Term:** In-Network vs. Out-of-Network
**Q:** What does in-network mean for dental insurance?
**A:** An in-network dentist has a contract with your insurance company and has agreed to accept reduced, negotiated fees for services. Because your insurer pays based on those lower contracted rates, your coinsurance and copays cost less. Out-of-network dentists charge their standard rates, which may exceed what your plan allows, leaving you to cover the gap.

---

### FAQ 12
**Term:** In-Network vs. Out-of-Network
**Q:** How much more does out-of-network dental care cost?
**A:** Out-of-network dental care can cost 40% to 60% more out of pocket compared to seeing an in-network provider. Your plan pays a lower percentage of a higher base fee, and out-of-network dentists can bill you for the balance above your plan's allowed amount. PPO plans still provide partial coverage out of network, unlike HMO plans.

---

### FAQ 13
**Term:** UCR (Usual, Customary, and Reasonable)
**Q:** What does UCR mean on a dental insurance EOB?
**A:** UCR stands for Usual, Customary, and Reasonable. It is the maximum fee your insurance company will recognize for a given procedure in your geographic area. If your dentist charges more than the UCR rate, you are responsible for the difference even after your insurer pays its share. UCR levels are set by each insurer independently and are not standardized.

---

### FAQ 14
**Term:** UCR (Usual, Customary, and Reasonable)
**Q:** Why did my dental insurance not cover the full amount?
**A:** Your insurer likely paid based on the UCR rate for your area, which may be lower than your dentist's actual fee. Your plan covers a percentage of the UCR amount, not the total billed amount. The remaining balance, called the write-off or balance bill, is your responsibility if your dentist is out of network.

---

### FAQ 15
**Term:** Predetermination
**Q:** What is a dental predetermination?
**A:** A dental predetermination is an estimate submitted to your insurer before treatment begins, showing what procedures are planned and their costs. The insurer reviews the request and tells you how much they will likely pay, what your share will be, and whether your deductible or annual maximum applies. Predeterminations are typically required for treatment plans over $300.

---

### FAQ 16
**Term:** Predetermination
**Q:** Does a dental predetermination guarantee that my insurance will pay?
**A:** No. A predetermination is an estimate, not a guarantee of payment. Final payment depends on your eligibility and remaining benefits at the time the claim is actually processed. If your annual maximum resets or you change plans between predetermination and treatment, your actual coverage may differ from the estimate.

---

### FAQ 17
**Term:** EOB (Explanation of Benefits)
**Q:** What is a dental EOB?
**A:** An Explanation of Benefits, or EOB, is a document your insurer sends after processing a dental claim. It shows the procedure performed, the amount billed by your dentist, the amount your plan allows, what the insurer paid, and what you owe. An EOB is not a bill; it is a record of how your benefits were applied.

---

### FAQ 18
**Term:** EOB (Explanation of Benefits)
**Q:** Is an Explanation of Benefits the same as a bill?
**A:** No. An EOB is sent by your insurance company to explain how a claim was processed. A bill comes from your dental office and shows the amount you actually owe after insurance has paid. Always wait for the EOB before paying a dental bill to confirm the insurance payment was applied correctly.

---

### FAQ 19
**Term:** Dental Premium
**Q:** What is a dental insurance premium?
**A:** A dental insurance premium is the monthly fee you pay to keep your plan active, regardless of whether you visit the dentist. Individual PPO premiums typically range from $20 to $50 per month. Family plans range from $50 to $150 per month. Employer-sponsored plans often cost less because the employer covers part of the premium.

---

### FAQ 20
**Term:** Dental HMO vs. PPO
**Q:** What is the difference between a dental HMO and a dental PPO?
**A:** A dental HMO (DHMO) requires you to choose a primary care dentist and stay within the network; specialist visits require a referral. A PPO lets you see any dentist with partial coverage even out of network, requires no referrals, and comes with a deductible and annual maximum. HMOs have lower premiums; PPOs offer greater flexibility.

## 4. ANSWER FORMAT RULES

These rules govern every FAQ answer on the glossary page. Follow them precisely to maximize featured snippet extraction probability.

### Rule 1: Open with a standalone definition sentence
The first sentence must define the term completely on its own. Google uses the first 40 to 60 words most often for featured snippets. If the first sentence is cut off, it must still make sense. Bad: "This is a question many patients have." Good: "A dental deductible is the fixed dollar amount you pay each year before insurance begins covering procedures."

### Rule 2: Keep answers under 60 words
Featured snippets are pulled from answers between 40 and 60 words. Longer answers dilute the signal. If the topic requires more depth, break it into a second FAQ question rather than extending the answer.

### Rule 3: Use plain declarative sentences
Write in active voice. Subject, verb, object. Avoid hedging language such as "may," "might," "could," "it depends," or "typically varies." Where variation exists, state the most common scenario directly: "Most PPO plans carry a $50 deductible" not "Deductibles can vary widely by plan."

### Rule 4: No em-dashes
Never use em-dashes in FAQ answers. Use a comma, a period, a colon, or a parenthetical instead. Em-dashes interrupt the sentence pattern Google uses to parse standalone answer blocks.

### Rule 5: Mirror PAA phrasing in the question
Write the Question field to match how patients type the query. Use "What is," "How does," "Can I," "Why do," "Does dental insurance" as openers. Avoid clever or branded phrasing in the question. The answer can carry the brand voice; the question should be invisible.

### Rule 6: Avoid lists in the primary answer sentence
Google will sometimes extract bullet lists, but paragraph answers are extracted more reliably for FAQ schema. If your answer naturally has 3 items (such as "preventive, basic, major"), write them as a sentence: "Preventive care is covered at 100%, basic procedures at 80%, and major work at 50%."

### Rule 7: Include a concrete number or example
Answers with a specific dollar amount, percentage, or time frame outperform vague answers in PAA extraction. "Most individual PPO plans cost $20 to $50 per month" is more extractable than "PPO premiums vary by plan and location."

### Rule 8: Never store or reference member IDs
Per CoverCapy product rules, no FAQ answer should suggest the patient needs to share a member ID number. Reference "your member ID" as a category without asking for the number itself.

## 5. FAQ PLACEMENT STRATEGY

### Primary placement: Anchored at the bottom of each term entry
Each glossary term on the page should be structured as: H2 term name, definition paragraph, key stats row, then an inline FAQ block with 1 to 2 questions specific to that term. This anchors FAQ content directly beneath the term it answers, which Google favors for associating structured data with semantic context.

### Secondary placement: Standalone "Common Questions" section at page bottom
After all glossary terms, include a consolidated FAQ section with the 10 schema-marked entries. This gives the FAQPage schema a clean, contiguous block to anchor to. Label the section with an H2 tag: "Common Questions About Dental Insurance."

### Do not place FAQs in a sidebar or modal
FAQ content in sidebars or hidden behind accordions (collapsed by default) is less reliably indexed. Accordions that are open by default are acceptable; collapsed accordions should be avoided for schema-marked content.

### Hierarchy of FAQPage schema placement
Place the FAQPage JSON-LD in the `<head>` or immediately before the closing `</body>` tag. Do not split the schema across multiple `<script>` blocks. All FAQ entries must be in a single FAQPage schema object per page.

### Page-level pairing: Link FAQs to CTA
Each FAQ section should be followed by a soft CTA linking to the verification flow or the plan comparison page. The FAQ answers questions; the CTA converts the intent. Example: After the "What is a PPO plan?" answer, link to `/compare-ppo-dental-plans` with anchor text "Compare PPO plans from $30/mo."

### AI Overview signal
As of 2026, Google's AI Overviews pull from structured FAQ content when the page has high E-E-A-T signals. Pages with clear authorship, schema, and concise answers are most likely to appear in AI Overview citations. For glossary pages, this means including a brief "Written by" attribution line and a last-updated date in the page head.

## 6. REFRESH CADENCE

### Evergreen terms (review annually)
These definitions are structurally stable and unlikely to change unless regulations shift:
- PPO vs. HMO
- Deductible
- Coinsurance
- EOB
- Annual maximum (structure, not amounts)
- UCR
- Predetermination
- Waiting period (structure)

**Refresh trigger:** Material regulatory change, or if a major insurer changes standard terminology.

### Rate-sensitive terms (review every 6 months)
These contain specific numbers that shift with market conditions and regulatory minimums:
- Annual maximum amounts (2026 regulatory floor of $2,500 is new)
- Premium ranges ($20 to $50/mo individual is current; verify semi-annually)
- Coinsurance percentages (HMO vs. PPO competitive dynamics shift these)
- Waiting period lengths (competition for no-wait plans is increasing)

**Refresh trigger:** Any insurer press release about plan changes, or ACA/state regulation changes to dental coverage minimums.

### High-volatility terms (review quarterly)
These are being actively contested in PAA results and carry the highest risk of stale information:
- No waiting period plans (new entrants, CoverCapy's own positioning)
- Delta Dental SSN requirement (Delta Dental's dual-carrier structure creates ongoing confusion)
- Annual maximum regulatory minimums (2026 rule is new; enforcement may create FAQ updates)
- AI-assisted verification (new category, no settled PAA pattern yet)

**Refresh trigger:** Any change to Delta Dental's dual-network structure, new state mandates, or if CoverCapy launches new plan products.

### Schema refresh rule
When any FAQ answer changes, regenerate and redeploy the full FAQPage JSON-LD block. Do not leave schema out of sync with visible page copy. Google will downgrade or remove rich results if the schema answer differs meaningfully from the visible answer text.
