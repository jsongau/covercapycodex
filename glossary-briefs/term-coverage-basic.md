---

## 2. Patient-Friendly Definition

Basic services are the middle tier of your PPO dental plan, sitting between preventive care (cleanings and X-rays) and major care (crowns and root canals). This tier typically includes composite and amalgam fillings, simple tooth extractions, periodontal scaling and root planing, and certain emergency palliative treatments. Most PPO plans reimburse basic services at 80 percent of the allowed amount, leaving you responsible for the remaining 20 percent coinsurance plus any outstanding deductible.

---

## 3. Clinical and Technical Context

### Waiting Periods
PPO plans commonly impose a waiting period of 0 to 6 months before basic services are covered. Employer-sponsored group plans frequently waive waiting periods entirely. Individual and family plans purchased on the open market are more likely to enforce a 3-to-6-month wait. Some plans use a 12-month waiting period for basic services when purchased outside open enrollment.

### Year 1 vs. Year 2 Step-Ups
Some PPO structures use a "step-up" or "increasing benefit" schedule:

- **Year 1:** Basic services covered at 50 percent.
- **Year 2:** Coverage increases to 70 percent.
- **Year 3+:** Full 80 percent coinsurance kicks in.

Step-up plans are most common on marketplace (ACA) dental policies. Employer plans more often start at 80 percent on day one.

### What Falls Where: Basic vs. Preventive vs. Major

| Category | Typical Procedures |
|---|---|
| Preventive | Exams, cleanings (prophylaxis), bitewing X-rays, fluoride, sealants |
| Basic | Fillings (D2140-D2394), simple extractions (D7140), emergency palliative treatment (D9110), periodontal scaling and root planing (D4341, D4342), debridement (D4355) |
| Major | Crowns, bridges, dentures, implants, surgical extractions, root canals, bone grafts |

Note: some plans classify root canals as basic rather than major. Always check your Summary of Benefits and Coverage (SBC) for your specific plan's tier assignments.

## 4. Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "My plan covers fillings at 100 percent because they are preventive."**
Fillings are almost never categorized as preventive. Preventive care targets maintaining healthy teeth (cleanings, exams, X-rays). Once decay has formed and a dentist needs to drill and restore a tooth, that procedure moves into the basic tier and the standard 80/20 coinsurance applies.

**Misconception 2: "If I hit my deductible, my plan pays 80 percent of whatever the dentist charges."**
Your plan pays 80 percent of the plan's *allowed amount* (also called the contracted fee or maximum plan allowance), not 80 percent of the dentist's full fee. If your dentist is out-of-network, the allowed amount may be lower than the billed amount, leaving you responsible for the difference (called balance billing) on top of your 20 percent coinsurance.

**Misconception 3: "A waiting period means I cannot see the dentist at all."**
Waiting periods apply only to the coverage tier, not to your ability to receive care. You can get a filling during a waiting period; you simply pay the full cost out of pocket until the waiting period ends. Preventive services are almost always excluded from waiting periods, so cleanings and exams are typically covered from day one.

## 5. Real-World Example: Composite Filling, Lower Molar

**Scenario:** Maria has an individual PPO plan with an $50 deductible (already met), an 80 percent basic coinsurance, and a $1,500 annual maximum. She needs a two-surface composite filling on a lower molar (CDT D2392).

| Line Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Dentist's billed fee | $230 |
| Plan's allowed amount (contracted rate) | $185 |
| Plan pays (80% of $185) | $148 |
| Maria's coinsurance (20% of $185) | $37 |
| Maria's deductible remaining | $0 (already met) |
| **Maria's total out-of-pocket** | **$37** |
| Remaining annual maximum after this claim | $1,352 |

If Maria had not yet met her $50 deductible, she would pay the first $50 and the plan would cover 80 percent of the remaining $135, leaving her total responsibility at $77.

## 6. Why It Matters

Basic services cover the most common dental problem adults face: tooth decay requiring a filling. Understanding your basic services coinsurance rate and whether a waiting period applies before your appointment can prevent an unexpected bill that is three to five times higher than anticipated.

## 9. FAQ

**Q: Does dental insurance cover fillings?**
A: Yes. Fillings fall under the basic services tier in virtually all PPO dental plans. Most plans pay 80 percent of the allowed amount once you have met your annual deductible, leaving you responsible for the remaining 20 percent. Some plans impose a waiting period of up to 6 months before basic coverage activates, so check your plan documents before scheduling.

**Q: What is the difference between basic and preventive dental coverage?**
A: Preventive coverage applies to services designed to keep healthy teeth healthy: routine cleanings, periodic exams, and diagnostic X-rays. These are typically covered at 100 percent with no deductible. Basic coverage applies once something has already gone wrong and needs to be repaired, such as a cavity requiring a filling or a tooth that must be extracted. Basic services usually carry an 80 percent reimbursement rate and are subject to your deductible.

**Q: How do I know if my plan has a waiting period for fillings?**
A: Check your plan's Summary of Benefits and Coverage (SBC) or Evidence of Coverage document. Look for a section titled "Waiting Periods" or "Benefit Limitations." Employer-sponsored group plans frequently have no waiting period for basic services. Individual plans purchased outside of an employer frequently have a 3-to-6-month wait.
