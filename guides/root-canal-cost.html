Quick answer

How much does a root canal cost?

A root canal typically costs about $700 to $1,600 without insurance, depending on the tooth, and roughly $1,500 to $3,500 all-in once you add the crown most teeth need afterward. Dental PPO insurance usually covers 50% to 80% of the procedure, and the remaining balance can be spread into monthly payments, including 0% and no-credit options.

Pricing

How much does a root canal cost in 2026?

A root canal in 2026 typically costs $700 to $1,600 without insurance, depending on which tooth it is. Front teeth sit at the low end, molars at the high end because they have more canals. Add the crown most teeth need afterward and the all-in total lands around $1,500 to $3,500.

The "root canal price" you see quoted is usually just the endodontic procedure. The real bill is the procedure plus the build-up and crown that protect the tooth afterward. Budget for the complete restoration, not the headline number.

Paying for it

Are there financing options available for root canal treatment?

Yes, and you have more than you think. A root canal can be financed through 0% APR healthcare credit cards, in-house dental payment plans, dental savings plans, an FSA or HSA, a personal loan, or a PPO plan that pays a share of the cost. No-credit-check plans exist for many patients, and several options require little or no money down. Here is the complete map, ranked roughly from the cheapest to the most expensive way to cover it.

A PPO dental plan that pays toward it

  • Usually the cheapest route overall
  • Pays 50% to 80% after deductible and any waiting period
  • Right plan can pay back its own premium on one root canal
  • Some carriers activate fast and shorten or skip waiting periods

0% APR healthcare financing

  • CareCredit and Sunbit offer 6 to 24 month 0% periods
  • Pay it off in the window and you owe no interest
  • Sunbit approves a wide range of credit profiles
  • Watch deferred-interest terms if you miss the payoff window

In-house dental payment plans

  • Split the cost into monthly installments paid to the office
  • Often interest-free with no third party
  • The most flexible and least talked about option
  • Ask the front desk directly (script below)

Dental savings (discount) plans

  • Flat annual fee, roughly $100 to $200
  • Cut procedure prices 10% to 60%
  • No waiting period
  • Useful when you need treatment now

FSA / HSA pre-tax dollars

  • A root canal is an eligible expense
  • Effectively a 20% to 35% discount
  • Depends on your tax bracket
  • Stacks on top of any coverage

Personal loan or low-cost care

  • A fixed-rate loan stretches larger all-in cases over years
  • Dental school clinics charge 40% to 60% less
  • Federally Qualified Health Centers use sliding-scale fees
  • Slower, but genuinely lower cost

Coverage

Does dental insurance cover a root canal?

Most PPO dental plans cover root canals, paying 50% to 80% of the cost after your deductible and any waiting period. Endodontics is usually classed as a "basic" or "major" service, so the exact percentage and any waiting period depend on the carrier, which is why choosing the right plan matters more than people realize.

The catch is waiting periods. A plan that covers root canals "at 80%" but makes you wait 6 to 12 months is useless for a tooth that hurts today. The plans worth shopping are the ones that activate fast or waive the wait:

  • UHC Primary Dental, next-day activation, strong preventive base. See plan
  • Ameritas PrimeStar Complete, pays about 20% on major work from day one, with no waiting period (verify current terms). See plan
  • Humana Extend 5000, about 50% on major work after a 6-month wait. See plan
  • Guardian, strong carrier, but mind the waiting periods.

Upfront cost

Can I get a root canal with no money down?

Often, yes. Several healthcare-financing providers approve no-money-down, deferred-interest plans for qualifying patients, and many dental offices will start an in-house plan with little or nothing upfront. Whether you qualify depends on the provider and your situation, but "I can't pay it all today" rarely means "I can't get treated today."

The smart move: combine a fast-activating PPO plan (to knock down the base price) with a 0% or in-house plan on the remainder. That stacks a discount on top of a payment schedule, the lowest realistic monthly.

Timing

What happens if you wait? The real cost of delay.

Delaying a root canal almost always costs more. An untreated infected tooth does not stabilize, it progresses toward an abscess and, eventually, extraction. Replacing an extracted tooth with an implant or bridge typically runs several times the cost of saving it now, so "waiting to afford it" usually makes it less affordable.

If cost is the only thing standing between you and treatment, that is exactly the problem financing solves. The cheapest version of this procedure is the one you do before it becomes an emergency.
