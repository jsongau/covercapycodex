The Downey Years A small office on a quiet street.

Before Downey, he was already an excellent dentist.
He simply did not yet know what he was missing.

The line between a good clinician and a transformed one is rarely drawn by a procedure. It is drawn, almost always, by a person — and by the office next door.

DDS · USC DA
David Abri, DDS · Downey, early years

By the time he arrived at the Downey clinic, David Abri was already what most patients would have called an excellent dentist. His hands were steady. His margins were clean. He understood the mechanics of a tooth the way a watchmaker understands a movement — with a quiet, exacting affection for the parts.

USC had given him the architecture. The years that followed had given him the instincts. He could read a panoramic film at a glance. He could plan an implant by feel. The science of restoration — biomechanics, occlusion, surgical precision, the geometry of a well-prepared margin — had been the scaffolding of his thinking since dental school. And it had served him, and his patients, well.

The Downey office was small. A few operatories down a shared hallway, a print of the coast on the waiting-room wall, and a quieter pace than the larger Southern California clinics he had worked in before. On the other side of the wall, sharing a hallway and a coffee maker, was the acupuncture practice of a young Taiwanese L.Ac. named Ann Chien. He noticed her before he spoke to her. Most people did.

What he did not yet know was that an entire dimension of his profession was waiting to be opened. He understood dentistry as repair. He understood it as restoration. He understood it as the careful, beautiful management of damage. He did not yet understand it as healing — not in the older, larger sense of the word. That understanding was about to arrive, very quietly, from the office next door.

She did not enter loudly.
She rarely entered anywhere loudly.

An acupuncturist trained in the older traditions of Chinese medicine — and, in time, the person who would reshape his understanding of what healing actually is.

Ann Chien was Taiwanese, in her late twenties at the time, and ran an acupuncture practice on the other side of a thin shared wall. She had trained in classical Traditional Chinese Medicine — five-element theory, meridian palpation, herbal formulation, the slow diagnostic art of reading a pulse — and she practiced with the unhurried care of someone who believed the body would tell her what it needed if she gave it enough time to speak.

She had a stillness about her that was hard to explain to anyone who had not been near it. People described it differently — calming, grounded, attentive — but the word that surfaced most often was graceful. Not in the choreographed sense. Graceful the way water is graceful when it has been moving over the same stones for a long time.

Patients who came to her sometimes came to him afterward — the dental work first, the acupuncture for the jaw tension and the headaches that followed. Patients who came to him sometimes went to her — for the migraines his TMJ planning could only address from one side. They began comparing notes. They began, slowly, talking about cases.

What she noticed first about his patients was the rhythm of them. They arrived in his chair tense and left, mostly, the same way. The procedures were clean. The clinical outcomes were good. But the bodies, she would later tell him, were not yet finished. In her tradition, a treatment that healed the tissue but left the system braced was a treatment only half done.

He was, at first, politely skeptical. He was a Western clinician, trained in a language of evidence and intervention. She was, by his initial accounting, working in a vocabulary he had not been taught to take seriously. But she did not press him. She rarely pressed anyone. She asked questions, mostly — the kind that arrive softly and stay for years.

She did not try to change his mind.
She simply kept asking the questions he had quietly been asking himself.

Traditional Chinese Medicine

A two-thousand-year-old framework for whole-system care.

Ann introduced him to TCM not as a parallel medicine to be chosen between, but as a different vocabulary for the same biology — one that paid careful attention to circulation, balance, breath, sleep, and the long arc of recovery.

Where his training treated the mouth as a site of intervention, hers treated the body as a continuous network. She walked him through pulse diagnostics, constitutional patterns, and the way classical practitioners read inflammation, fatigue, and stress as related signals from a single regulating system — not unrelated symptoms to be addressed in isolation.

Acupuncture & Meridian Healing

Circulation as conversation, not just plumbing.

She showed him how the body, in classical practice, is mapped as channels — pathways through which signal, warmth, and circulation travel. The jaw, in particular, sits at a dense crossroads of meridians related to digestion, stress, and the nervous system.

He began to understand why patients with chronic dental pain often presented with shoulder, neck, and gut tension as well. The mouth was rarely just the mouth. After implant placement and significant restorative work, Ann would treat his patients with gentle needling along the relevant meridians — and the recovery times, anecdotally at first, then unmistakably, began to shorten.

Nervous System Regulation

A regulated patient is, biologically, a different patient.

Ann taught him to look at the chair the way she looked at her treatment table — as an instrument of regulation, not just intervention. A patient in sympathetic overdrive is not merely uncomfortable. Their cortisol is up, their peripheral circulation is down, and their tissue is already braced before he picks up an instrument.

He began studying vagal tone, paced breathing, and the measurable difference in healing markers between anxious and calm patients. He started checking in with patients between phases of a procedure — not as politeness, but as clinical work. He learned to recognize the small signals of dysregulation in a jaw that wouldn't soften, a tongue that wouldn't rest, a shoulder that stayed near the ear. Regulation, he came to believe, was a clinical variable.

Stress & Inflammation

Inflammation as a message, not just a target.

In her framework, inflammation was a signal — the body's attempt to coordinate repair under specific conditions. A patient whose system was chronically stressed produced messier inflammation. A patient whose system was supported produced cleaner, faster resolution.

David, who had been trained to suppress inflammation pharmacologically when needed, began to also ask the older question: what is this inflammation actually responding to? Sleep, diet, jaw clenching, unprocessed stress, undiagnosed sinus issues — he began addressing upstream causes alongside downstream symptoms, and his post-surgical inflammation profiles, he noticed, began to look different.

The Importance of Calmness

Stillness as technique, not bedside manner.

Calmness, in Ann's hands, was not a soft skill. It was a clinical tool. She moved slowly because moving slowly produced better outcomes. She spoke softly because soft speech regulated the room. She gave patients time to settle before she did anything — because a settled patient was a different physiological subject.

David began applying the same principles to his own practice. He started building two-minute settling periods into the start of complex appointments. He stopped speaking quickly when patients were anxious. He learned, from watching her, that presence — actual, undistracted presence — was perhaps the single most undervalued instrument in a dental operatory.

Bone Regeneration & Whole-Body Healing

Bone responds to the body it lives in.

The most consequential idea Ann gave him was also the simplest: bone is a living tissue inside a regulating system. It rebuilds best when the body around it is cooperative — well-circulated, well-rested, well-nourished, and not chronically braced.

David, who had always thought of bone as a substrate to be worked, began thinking of it as a participant. He became more interested in pre-surgical regulation — sleep, nutrition, stress — than most of his colleagues. He started writing pre-implant protocols that read, partly, like wellness plans. And he began to notice something his Western training had not predicted: when the whole body was supported, the bone arrived to its own healing more reliably.

A Gentler Surgical Philosophy Minimally traumatic placement. Long-arc thinking.

Over time, his surgical approach quieted.

The changes were subtle enough to miss if you weren't paying attention. The patients, of course, were the ones paying attention. Tap each principle to read how it shapes a case.

He began to value preservation over aggression. He became more careful about tissue trauma — the small bruises and tensions a faster operator might overlook. He grew interested in regenerative approaches: how bone, given the right conditions and the right pace, could be coaxed rather than rebuilt. He studied gentler oral surgery techniques and quietly retired the more forceful ones from his own practice.

And he began, before every procedure, to attend to the patient in front of him as a system — not merely as a tooth, a socket, or a treatment plan. He asked different questions in the chair. He moved more slowly when slowness mattered. He let silence do some of the work that explanation used to do.

Principle I

Bone Preservation

Treat existing bone as the most valuable material in the room. Preserve first; intervene only where preservation is no longer possible.

In practice, this means atraumatic extraction techniques, periotomes over forceps where appropriate, and a deliberate slowness around the buccal plate. Bone that has not been disturbed does not need to be regenerated.

Read more
Principle II

Minimally Traumatic Placement

Choose techniques that ask less of the tissue. The body's quietest cooperation is earned by the clinician's quietest hand.

Flapless approaches when anatomy allows. Carefully staged osteotomies. Lower-RPM drilling with chilled saline. Less heat, less trauma, less inflammatory load — and a meaningfully calmer healing curve.

Read more
Principle III

Nervous-System-Aware Planning

Plan around the patient's regulation as carefully as around their anatomy. A regulated patient is, biologically, a different patient.

Pre-procedure breathing protocols. Quiet operatories. Conversational pacing that matches the patient's, not the clinician's. Time built into the schedule for settling. None of this is decoration; all of it is technique.

Read more
Principle IV

Long-Term Integration

Measure success not at the moment of placement but at the months of integration that follow. Patience is, often, the strongest material.

Longer integration windows in regulated patients. Staged loading where biology favors it. Follow-up cadences designed around the body's healing arc, not the practice's calendar. The implant is not done when it is placed; it is done when it is quiet.

The Rise of Holistic Implant Dentistry Patients began seeking him out — for the way he worked.

Word travels quietly, but it travels.

Over the years, his practice began to attract a particular kind of patient. People who had been through harder procedures with harder operators. People whose first implant had not gone well. People who had read about holistic dentistry and wanted to know whether it was real — or whether it was just softer marketing.

What they found, in David's chair, was something more grounded than either expectation: a USC-trained Western clinician who had, in plain sight, expanded what he meant by the word healing — and who had built protocols around it.

D & A

What he admires about her, friends will tell you, is not easily summarized. It is the cumulative effect of being near someone who is, in the most ordinary moments, paying genuine attention. She is the person in any room who notices the quiet one first. She is the practitioner who, asked a hard question, will think for a long moment before answering.

He brought to her his exactness, his discipline, the long architecture of his training. She brought to him her stillness, her patience, and a worldview in which precision and warmth are not in tension but in service to each other. They make each other's work better. They make each other's days softer.

People who have known them both for years describe their partnership the same way they describe Ann herself — unhurried, gracious, and unmistakably whole.

She changed the way he thought about care.
And then she changed the way he thought about everything else.
A Quiet Announcement

There is one detail this profile has, until now, withheld.

Ann Chien did not become an episode in his story. She became its continuing chapter. They have built, in the years that followed Downey, a quiet and remarkable partnership — both philosophical and personal.

And this summer, in the soft light of a California afternoon, David Abri and Ann Chien will mark the next chapter of that partnership. They are, with the unhurried grace that has defined everything else between them, engaged.
