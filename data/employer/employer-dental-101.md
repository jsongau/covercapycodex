## The one idea: group dental is bought differently from individual dental
Individual dental plans (the ones in /data/plans/ like Humana Extend 5000) have fixed, published prices and benefits. Group/employer dental does not. The employer (with a broker or platform) chooses the plan design from a carrier menu — annual maximum, coinsurance, deductible, whether to add orthodontia, whether to add implants — and the carrier quotes a per-group rate based on the company's census (how many employees, their ages, the ZIP code, the industry, how many enroll). That is why no honest source publishes a single "group dental price." Anyone who quotes you a flat national group rate is guessing.

What this means for the owner: you are not shopping a shelf product, you are configuring a benefit. CoverCapy's job is to make that configuration feel like a concierge choosing for you, not a spreadsheet.

## Who pays what: employer contribution, employee payroll, and dependents
Three moving parts:

1. Employer contribution. The employer decides what share of the premium to pay. Most carriers want the employer to fund at least 50% of the employee-only premium (Cigna, UHC, and the SHOP tax-credit threshold all sit at ~50%). Some carriers (Humana, Ameritas, Principal, Beam) let the employer pay as little as 0% and run the plan fully voluntary. Paying more isn't just generosity — it usually unlocks lower participation requirements and waived waiting periods.

2. Employee payroll deduction. Whatever the employer doesn't cover, the employee pays through pre-tax payroll deduction. Because it's pre-tax, the real cost to the employee is lower than the sticker premium.

3. Dependents. Adding a spouse or children moves the employee to a higher rate tier (the standard four tiers are Employee only / Employee + Spouse / Employee + Child(ren) / Employee + Family). The dependent cost is almost always borne by the employee, not the employer. This is the honest answer to "won't people just add all their kids for free?" — they can add their kids, but they pay the family-tier rate to do it, and each covered person still has their own deductible and their own annual maximum. The plan is not a free-for-all; it is tiered and capped.

## The "copay" question: how cost-sharing works at the chair
There is no flat per-visit copay on a PPO dental plan (flat copays are a DHMO/prepaid feature). On a PPO, the patient's share is the coinsurance plus the deductible:

- Preventive (cleanings, exams, x-rays): typically 100% covered, no deductible. The patient pays nothing in-network.
- Basic (fillings, simple extractions): plan pays ~80%, patient pays ~20% after the deductible.
- Major (crowns, dentures, root canals, implants where covered): plan pays ~50%, patient pays ~50% after the deductible.
- Above the annual maximum: the patient normally pays 100% of further costs that year. (Humana is the exception — its Extended Annual Maximum keeps paying 30% after the ceiling, so the patient pays 70%, not 100%.)

So the "co-pay so they don't abuse it" instinct is already built into every PPO: the deductible and the rising coinsurance (0% → 20% → 50%) are the natural brake. Preventive is free on purpose, because cheap cleanings prevent expensive crowns.

## The annual maximum (and why $5,000 is rare with ortho)
The annual maximum is the most the plan pays per person per year. Most published group designs land between $1,000 and $2,500. A genuine $5,000 annual maximum on a group plan is uncommon and only confirmed on a few carriers:
- Humana group: employer-selectable from $1,000 up to Unlimited (so $5,000+ is easy to configure), with no waiting periods at 5+ enrolled.
- Beam Ultra: up to $5,000.
- MetLife: documented $5,000 "Platinum" PDP Plus design.
- Ameritas: a $5,000 marketplace tier exists (detailed grid to be confirmed at quote).

Watch the trap: Delta Dental's headline $5,000 plan is an individual product that excludes orthodontia and implants. There is no published Delta group plan that pairs $5,000 with braces. When an owner wants "the biggest plan, with braces, that my team can actually use right away," the honest shortlist is Humana group, Beam Ultra, and MetLife.

Rollover features soften a modest max: Guardian's Maximum Rollover, Ameritas's Dental Rewards, Principal's Maximum Accumulation, and UHC's MaxMultiplier all let unused annual maximum carry forward (usually if the member files at least one claim that year). A $2,000 plan with rollover can quietly become a $4,000 cushion over a couple of light years.

## Orthodontics: braces for the kids (and sometimes adults)
Ortho is almost always a separate rider with its own separate lifetime maximum (commonly $1,000-$2,500 per person), paid at 50%, and it does not draw on the annual maximum. Two things vary by carrier:
- Child vs adult. Many designs cover children only (often appliance placed before age 18-19). Several carriers offer adult + child ortho when the employer elects it (confirmed: Humana, MetLife on some designs, Ameritas, Aetna, Guardian, Cigna, Beam Select/Ultra). Principal's filed examples are child-only.
- Group-size gates. Ortho is sometimes restricted by group size (UHC: 10+ employees with 8+ enrolled). Humana offers ortho at 2+ enrolled.

For an employer, "I included braces for your kids" is one of the most emotionally resonant lines in the whole benefit — lead with it.

## Implants
Implants are usually covered as a Major service (~50%) and draw on the annual maximum, with no separate implant sub-limit on most group designs. Exceptions to know: UHC excludes implants from the base plan (needs the FlexAppeal Enhanced buy-up); Humana offers implants only at 10+ enrolled; Cigna/some designs exclude implants on the cheapest tier. A missing-tooth clause (teeth lost before coverage began aren't covered for replacement) commonly applies — Beam notably has no missing-tooth clause.

## Waiting periods — and why groups beat individuals here
This is the quiet superpower of employer dental. On individual plans, major work and ortho usually carry 6-12 month waits. On group plans, those waits are frequently waived:
- Humana and Guardian: no waiting periods at 5+ enrolled employees.
- Delta, Ameritas, Principal, Beam: no waiting periods on group designs (Beam by default).
- Aetna and many others: no waits, replaced by a fair 12-month "late entrant" rule for people who skip their first enrollment window.
- Takeover credit: when a company switches carriers, prior coverage is credited so waits are waived, deductibles already met are honored, and (Principal) the annual maximum is refreshed.

The talking point: "Get your team covered and they can use it now — crowns, root canals, even braces — no waiting." For most owners that is the headline.

## Guaranteed issue and minimum group size
Group dental is generally guaranteed issue: no employee has to pass a medical or dental exam to get covered, as long as the group meets participation and contribution rules. Minimum group sizes are small — Principal and Humana and Beam write employer-paid groups down to 2 enrolled (Principal: 25 in Massachusetts); Mutual of Omaha has a 2-9 small-business path. A solo owner with no W-2 employees usually can't buy a group plan (Cigna requires at least one W-2 common-law employee) and should be routed to an individual plan instead.
