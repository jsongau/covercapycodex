# 03 — Benefits Breakdown: What You Actually Get

The discount structure in real dollars. The single most useful thing on the page is a believable fee table — "save 15–50%" means nothing until a reader sees a $1,500 crown become $949.

---

## Structural features (the no-no-no-no list)

These are the four things a savings plan does that insurance does not, and they are the whole value proposition. All confirmed by DentalPlans.com (page modified May 7, 2026) and 1Dental/Careington.

- **NO annual maximum.** "No limit on how much you can use your plan or how much you can save each year." Insurance typically cuts you off at $1,000–$2,000; this never does.
- **NO waiting periods.** Usable on major procedures immediately after activation. Insurance commonly makes you wait 6–12 months for major work.
- **NO deductibles.** Nothing to meet before the discount applies.
- **NO exclusion for pre-existing conditions.** You can enroll with existing dental problems — a cracked tooth, an abscess, a missing tooth — and use the plan on them right away. Insurance often excludes work tied to pre-existing issues (the "missing tooth clause").

Plus: **cosmetic procedures are discounted** (implants, veneers, whitening) — insurance usually excludes these entirely — and **orthodontics is included** for both adults and children.

## Three worked examples for the page

- **A single crown.** A ~$1,500 porcelain crown costs about **$949** — roughly $551 saved on one procedure. That alone is several times the annual membership fee.
- **A molar root canal.** A ~$1,535 molar root canal costs about **$791** — roughly $744 saved, again on a single visit.
- **A full ortho case for one child.** ~$6,099 braces drop to about **$4,235** — roughly $1,864 saved, with no lifetime cap (unlike a PPO ortho benefit).

The point to land: for anyone facing one major procedure, the plan pays for itself many times over in a single visit. For someone who only needs a cleaning, the math is much tighter (see `05` and `07`).

## How the discount is calculated

The member pays the dentist's **Aetna Dental Access contracted fee** — a pre-negotiated fee schedule — not a loose "percent off whatever the dentist feels like charging." Per DentalPlans.com: *"Members will be charged the provider's Aetna Dental Access contracted fees."* 1Dental describes it the same way: the office charges according to its Aetna fee schedule and you pay the office directly.

Two honesty caveats the page must keep:
1. **The published schedule is a guide, not a guarantee.** "Individual dentist fee schedules may differ. Aetna makes no guarantee as to the accuracy of any particular fee amount." Fees vary by ZIP and by provider.
2. **Lab fees may be extra** and are not part of the quoted discounted fee.

## Activation and eligibility

- **Activation:** same day to about four days, depending on the retailer. DentalPlans.com says "activates in 4 days" with effective-date materials within 24 hours; 1Dental/Careington says "ready for use immediately." The common "1–3 days" claim sits inside this range.
- **Family coverage:** cardholder, spouse, and legal dependents up to age 18 (or up to 25 if full-time students).
- **No health questions, no age limit on the primary member, no underwriting.** You can enroll with existing dental problems.

---

## Exclusions and limits (the honest fine print)

- The plan **pays providers nothing** — you pay the full discounted charge yourself, at the visit.
- Discounts apply **only at participating providers.** No savings out of network.
- **Lab fees may be additional.**
- **Specialty work** (endodontics, oral surgery, ortho, perio, prosthodontics, pediatric) is often shown as "call" rather than a fixed percentage on DentalPlans.com — savings at specialists vary and aren't always published.
- The 15–50% range is **not guaranteed on every procedure** — orthodontics in particular runs lower.
- It does **not** meet ACA minimum creditable coverage; **not available in Vermont or Washington** (per 1Dental/Careington).
