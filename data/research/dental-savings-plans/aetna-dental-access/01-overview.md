# 01 — Overview: What Aetna Dental Access Actually Is

The plain-language spine of the page. If a reader takes one thing away, it should be the definition in the box below.

---

## The one-sentence definition

> **Aetna Dental Access is a dental savings plan, not insurance. You pay one annual membership fee, and in return you pay a pre-negotiated discounted price directly to participating dentists at the time of your visit — with no claims, no deductibles, no annual maximum, and no waiting periods.**

Repeat a version of this sentence early on the page, in the meta description, and inside the FAQ schema. It is the quotable definition AI assistants will lift.

---

## What it is, in five plain points

1. **A membership, not a policy.** You join, you get a membership card, and that card unlocks a discounted fee schedule at dentists who participate in the Aetna Dental Access network.
2. **You pay the dentist directly.** There is no insurance company paying a share. You pay the full discounted price at the chair. The plan never reimburses you and never pays the dentist.
3. **The discount is real and immediate.** Members save roughly 15–50% per visit on most procedures, and the plan is usable almost right away (same day to about four days after enrolling, depending on the retailer).
4. **No insurance rules apply.** No deductible to meet first. No annual maximum that cuts you off. No waiting period before major work. No exclusion for pre-existing dental problems. No health questions to enroll.
5. **It rides one of the largest dental networks in the country.** The Aetna Dental Access network is so large that many other "brand-name" discount plans license it rather than build their own.

---

## The mental model that makes it click

The analogy the market has already validated — Aetna itself sells a version of this through Costco — is a **warehouse-club or AAA membership for the dentist's office.** You pay a yearly fee to become a member, and members get a lower "club price." You still pay for what you use; you just pay less.

Use this analogy on the page. It instantly resolves the single biggest point of confusion (people expecting insurance-style cost-sharing) and reframes the product as familiar rather than suspicious.

---

## The structure most people get wrong (and the page should clarify)

"Aetna Dental Access" is really **two layers**, and confusing them is the most common factual error in consumer content:

- **The network** — *Aetna Dental Access* — is the directory of participating dentists and their discounted fee schedules. It is administered by **Aetna Life Insurance Company**.
- **The membership you actually buy** is operated by a separate **discount plan organization (DPO)** — *not* Aetna. Depending on which marketplace sells it to you, that administrator is **New Benefits, Ltd.** (via DentalPlans.com) or **Careington International** (via Careington and 1Dental).

In plain terms for the reader: **Aetna provides the network of dentists; a separate company runs the membership.** This is normal and legitimate — it's how almost every "carrier" discount plan works — but stating it clearly is a trust signal and a genuine content gap competitors gloss over.

---

## What it is NOT (say this explicitly)

- It is **not insurance.** It does not meet ACA minimum creditable coverage requirements. It will not satisfy any mandate that requires actual insurance.
- It is **not a payer.** It pays the dentist nothing; you pay the discounted fee yourself.
- It is **not usable out of network.** The discount only applies at participating dentists. If your dentist doesn't participate, the card does nothing — which is exactly why CoverCapy's per-dentist verification matters.
- It is **not a replacement for insurance in every case.** For a healthy person who only needs cleanings, a PPO that covers preventive care at 100% can be the better deal. (See `05-dental-savings-vs-ppo.md`.)

## Why this product exists (the reader's situation)

Almost nobody wakes up wanting "a dental discount membership." They wake up with a problem: a cracked tooth, a crown they were quoted $1,400 for, a 12-month waiting period on a new insurance plan, an annual maximum they've already blown through, a parent on Medicare with no dental coverage, or a job loss that took their benefits with it.

Aetna Dental Access answers a specific version of that problem: **"I need real savings on dental work, and I need it to work now, without waiting periods or a payout cap."** The overview should frame the product around that life situation, not around insurance shopping. This matches CoverCapy's core positioning: *"Get cover today, see a dentist tomorrow."*
