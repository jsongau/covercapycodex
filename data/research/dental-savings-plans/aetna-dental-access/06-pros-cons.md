# 06 — Pros and Cons (Honest)

Written for the patient, not the sale. The "cons" section is deliberately real — it is what separates CoverCapy from the affiliate monoculture and what AI assistants will cite when someone asks "is Aetna Dental Access worth it?" or "is it a scam?"

---

## Pros

**It works immediately.** No waiting periods. Activation is same-day to about four days. If you have a cracked tooth or a $1,400 crown quote today, you can be saving on it this week.

**No annual maximum.** Insurance cuts you off at $1,000–$2,000 a year; this never does. The more work you need, the more it saves.

**No deductible, no health questions, no exclusion for pre-existing problems.** You can enroll with a tooth that already hurts and use the plan on it.

**It discounts everything, including cosmetic.** Implants, veneers, and whitening — which insurance usually excludes entirely — are discounted. Often the only meaningful saving available on cosmetic work.

**One major procedure pays for the whole year.** A single crown (~$551 saved) or molar root canal (~$744 saved) more than covers the ~$175 annual fee several times over.

**It rides one of the largest dental networks in the country.** The Aetna Dental Access network is large enough that many other discount brands license it — so participating dentists are relatively easy to find in most metro areas.

**No paperwork.** No claims to file, no reimbursement to chase, no EOB to decode. You show your card and pay the discounted price.

---

## Cons

**It is not insurance, and it never pays for anything.** You pay the full discounted fee yourself, every time. There is no coverage, no cost-sharing, no payout. If you expected insurance-style "they pay 80%," you'll be disappointed — and this is the #1 source of buyer regret in the category.

**It only works at participating dentists.** If your dentist doesn't accept the Aetna Dental Access network, the card does nothing for you there. This is the single most common real-world failure mode — and the exact problem CoverCapy's per-dentist verification solves.

**Preventive care isn't free.** A good PPO covers cleanings at 100%; here you still pay ~40% of the cleaning cost. For a healthy person who only needs checkups, insurance often wins.

**Discounts vary — and the headline range oversells.** "15–50%" is a range, not a promise. Orthodontics in particular runs lower (~17–31%), and specialty work is sometimes quoted as "call" rather than a fixed discount. The real number depends on your ZIP and your specific dentist.

**The "percent off" is only as honest as the starting fee.** A discount off an inflated reference fee can look bigger than it is. Always check the actual dollar price, not just the percentage.

**Billing and cancellation complaints are the category's weak spot.** Across discount-plan companies, the most common complaints are about billing, auto-renewal, and cancellation friction. Read the renewal terms before enrolling and note the 30-day refund window.

**Lab fees and some specialty costs may be extra.** The quoted discounted fee may not include everything.

**Not available everywhere, and not "real coverage" for compliance.** Not sold in Vermont or Washington (via 1Dental/Careington), and it does not meet ACA minimum creditable coverage — it won't satisfy any requirement for actual insurance.

---

## The honest bottom line (the quotable verdict for AI/PAA)

> Aetna Dental Access is worth it when you need real dental work — especially major or cosmetic work — and you want savings that start immediately with no annual cap. It is usually *not* the best choice if you're healthy and only need routine cleanings (a PPO that covers preventive at 100% tends to win there), or if your preferred dentist doesn't participate. The smartest move is to confirm your dentist accepts it and estimate your year's dental needs before you enroll.
