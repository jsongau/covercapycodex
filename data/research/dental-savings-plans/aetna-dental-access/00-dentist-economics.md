# 00 — Dentist Economics: Why Your Dentist Says Yes to a Discount Plan

> **This is the centerpiece document.** It answers the founder's question directly and is the single most defensible piece of content in the entire initiative. No carrier, no affiliate, and no comparison site publishes this honestly, because none of them has an incentive to explain the supply side. CoverCapy does.

---

## The founder's hypothesis, and the verdict

**Hypothesis:** *"Why would a dentist even accept a dental savings plan? Is it to upsell at higher markups for major services, since major services on PPO insurance usually have a waiting period or low % coverage?"*

**Verdict: right instinct, wrong mechanism.**

The instinct is correct about *where* the pressure comes from. PPO insurance caps payouts, makes patients wait 6–12 months for major work, and excludes cosmetic care entirely. Those gaps are exactly what a savings plan exploits. But "higher markup" is not how dentists make money on savings plans. No source — not a single carrier, consultant, or trade publication — found dentists charging a *higher sticker price* on a savings-plan patient. By definition a savings plan is a **discount off the dentist's own full fee**. There is no markup.

The real revenue engine is three things stacked on top of each other:

1. **No insurance write-off.** The dentist forgives a smaller, self-chosen discount off their *own* fee instead of the deep cut a PPO insurer dictates.
2. **Higher case acceptance.** With no annual maximum and no waiting period, patients say yes to full treatment plans they would otherwise phase over years.
3. **Near-zero admin cost.** Paid in full at the chair. No claims, no accounts receivable, no denials.

So the accurate one-line model is: **"Keep more of each fee, close more big cases, and spend almost nothing to collect it."** The founder's "upsell on major work" intuition points at the right pain (PPO caps, waits, and exclusions on major and cosmetic work), but the lever is *more cases closed at the dentist's own fee*, not *higher prices*.

---

## 1. No insurance write-off — the core mechanic

This is the heart of why a discount-plan patient can net a dentist *more per procedure* than a deep-discount PPO patient.

**On a PPO, the insurer sets the fee and takes a deep cut.** A contracted PPO dentist "must accept the maximum allowable fee dictated by the plan regardless of what the office bills." The ADA's 2023 Dental Fees data puts the average PPO write-off — the gap between the dentist's full fee and the contracted fee — at **30–40%**. Worked example: a $1,400 crown with a $900 contracted fee is a $500 write-off, more than 35% gone before the dentist does anything. Some practices write off up to half of gross production once they stack several PPOs. And through an "affiliated carrier clause," the discounted rate can be extended to third-party carriers the dentist never directly contracted with — so the deep rate spreads.

**On a savings plan, the dentist chooses the discount, off their own fee, and is paid directly.** A discount plan is "a group of participating dentists who agree to perform services for enrollees at a specified discounted price, or discount off their usual charge." Typical discounts run 20–60%, but the baseline is the dentist's own fee, not an insurer's schedule. Careington's plan language confirms it: for any procedure not on its published schedule, the rule is simply "discounted 20% off the provider's normal fee."

**The math:** a ~20% self-chosen discount off the dentist's full fee nets more than a 40% insurer-dictated cut off a fee the dentist did not set — and it arrives with no claims overhead. That is the whole reason a cash-discount patient can be *more* profitable than a PPO patient.

**The honesty caveat CoverCapy must honor:** a "percent off" headline is only as honest as the reference fee. Insurer UCR ("usual, customary, and reasonable") tables are proprietary and somewhat arbitrary, and a "% off UCR" can be inflated by setting a high reference fee. Practice consultants explicitly warn offices to discount off true fees, "not your PPO fees," to avoid discounting already-discounted numbers. CoverCapy's content should say plainly: the discount is real, but always check the actual dollar price, not just the percentage.

## 2. Higher case acceptance — the volume engine

The PPO gaps that savings plans exploit are real and documented:

- **Annual maximums are tiny and frozen in time.** Typical PPO annual max is $1,000–$2,000. The $1,000 level "was established some 40 years ago" and has never kept up with inflation. A single crown or a root-canal-plus-crown can exhaust an entire year's benefit.
- **Waiting periods delay major work.** New individual PPO plans commonly impose a 12-month wait on major services, then pay only 50%. Frequency limits block crown, bridge, and denture replacement for 5–7 years.
- **Cosmetic work is excluded outright.** Insurance "typically will not cover cosmetic procedures." Veneers (~$1,765/tooth) and whitening get $0 from a PPO. A savings plan still discounts them — pure upside for case acceptance.

**Does this drive more "yes"?** The evidence says yes, though the cleanest numbers are vendor-sourced and should be cited as *directional, not fact.* Membership/savings patients are reported to accept treatment at roughly 40%+ vs PPO at 30–35% and pure cash at ~24%, and to spend meaningfully more on crowns, implants, and cosmetic work. The mechanism is psychological: a PPO patient asks "will my insurance cover this?" and walks if the answer is no; a savings-plan member treats the discount as a perk to *use*, and isn't stopped by an annual cap.

**This is the precise correction to the hypothesis.** It is not that the dentist marks the crown up. It is that the patient, freed from a $1,500 ceiling and a 12-month wait, agrees to the crown *and* the implant *and* finishes the plan this year instead of stretching it across three benefit years. Same fee, far more accepted treatment.

## 3. Near-zero admin cost — the quiet third lever

A savings-plan patient pays the discounted fee directly at the time of service. There is no claim to file, no 30–60 day reimbursement cycle, no denial or downcoding, and no insurance accounts receivable. The ADA states it plainly: discount programs "eliminate the claims process. The dentist doesn't have to wait to be paid."

Why this matters financially:

- Insurance verification alone can consume 4–6 hours of front-desk time per week, and a mid-size practice may run 0.5–1.0 full-time staff on claims at $50K–$65K each. *(Directional, vendor-sourced.)*
- Dental claim denials reached roughly 15% in 2024–2025, and about two-thirds of denials are never appealed — permanent lost revenue.
- Practices that lean on membership/discount models report a 10–15% revenue lift "just from cutting administrative costs." *(Directional, vendor-sourced.)*

So even at the same headline price, the discount-plan dollar is cheaper to collect than the PPO dollar.

---

## 4. Filling the chair — the patient-mix lever

An empty chair-hour is pure loss against fixed overhead (rent, salaries, equipment financing don't pause). Estimates put the cost of an idle chair-hour at $300–$500. *(Directional.)* Savings-plan networks market themselves to dentists explicitly as patient-acquisition channels — Aetna Dental Access and Careington both pitch offices on building their patient base.

The available pool is large and underserved:

- About 27% of U.S. adults — roughly 72 million people — have no dental insurance, nearly three times the medical-uninsured rate.
- Uninsured adults visit the dentist at 16% vs 53% for the privately insured; converting one to a plan member directly fills a chair.
- Seniors are the cleanest opportunity: Original Medicare does not cover routine dental, and roughly 47% of adults 65–80 have no dental coverage.

A savings plan turns a price-sensitive non-patient into a paying, treatment-accepting one.

---

## 5. The downsides for dentists (be honest about these too)

- **100% collection risk at the chair.** No carrier backstop — if the patient can't pay the discounted fee, the practice eats it.
- **A binding maximum fee schedule.** On covered codes, the network's fee schedule is a *maximum* the dentist agrees not to exceed. Pricing latitude on those codes is gone.
- **Most-favored-nation / "lowest fee" traps.** Repeated or deep discounting can, under some insurance contracts, trigger an obligation to extend the discount more broadly; waiving certain copays can even void PPO participation. Enforceability varies — the DOJ has challenged dental MFN clauses and about 20 states restrict them.
- **A narrower steered pool.** Fewer dentists accept savings memberships than insurance, so the patient pool a given plan steers is smaller than the PPO base.

## 6. How offices process a savings plan vs a PPO

| Step | Discount plan | PPO |
|------|---------------|-----|
| Eligibility / benefits call | **None** | Verify benefits, every patient, every visit |
| Pre-authorization | **None** | Required for major work |
| Estimate accuracy | 100% (fixed fee schedule) | Uncertain until the EOB (downcoding, bundling) |
| Claim form | **None** | Detailed claim every visit |
| Payment timing | **At the chair, today** | 15–90 day reimbursement wait |
| EOB reconciliation / A/R | **None** | Required, plus balance-billing follow-up |
| Patient journey | Show membership card → pay discounted fee | Verify → estimate → collect copay → file claim → reconcile EOB → chase balance |

As one dental-billing source put it, a discount plan is "somewhat like being fee-for-service, just with a discount... your front office team members probably much prefer working with discount plans."

1. **Builds trust through transparency.** Showing the patient *why* the system works the way it does is disarming. It signals CoverCapy is explaining, not selling.

## Source reliability note

- **Firmest (independent / government):** ADA and ADA Health Policy Institute, NADP, CMS, CareQuest, Delta Dental and MetLife/Cigna/Humana product definitions. These anchor write-off magnitude (ADA 2023: 30–40%), annual-max norms, waiting periods, the uninsured/senior market, and the no-claims mechanics.
- **Directional (vendor / consultant — attribute, never state as fact):** case-acceptance percentages, the 10–15% admin-savings figure, career write-off totals, chair-hour cost, and the visit/production-lift numbers. Cite these as "practice-management sources report…"
- **Flagged unverified — do not cite as fact:** "2.4 touchpoints per claim," "$117 per denied claim," the exact share of patients who exhaust their annual max, and the "patients haggle for deeper discounts" anecdote.
