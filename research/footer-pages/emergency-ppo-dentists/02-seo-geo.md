## FAQ (mirrors FAQPage schema, 6 Q&A)
1. What counts as a dental emergency? — Severe tooth pain that painkillers do not control, a knocked-out or broken tooth, a lost crown or filling, a swollen abscess, jaw swelling, or uncontrolled bleeding. A small painless chip or mild sensitivity can usually wait for a regular appointment.
2. Can I see an emergency dentist the same day? — Yes. Same-day emergency dentistry is widely available, including after-hours slots. Call ahead, describe your symptom, and ask for the soonest urgent appointment. CoverCapy helps you find an in-network emergency PPO dentist fast.
3. Should I go to the ER or a dentist for tooth pain? — See a dentist. ER doctors cannot drill, fill, root canal, or extract teeth, so an ER visit usually ends with antibiotics, a painkiller, and a referral, after a bill that often runs $400 to $1,500 or more. Use the ER only for spreading facial swelling, trouble breathing, or trauma.
4. Does PPO insurance cover emergency dental visits? — Usually yes. PPO plans typically cover emergency palliative treatment to relieve pain, and the underlying procedure such as an extraction or root canal at the plan's normal rate. In network is cheapest because your share is based on the negotiated fee schedule. Verify your specific plan.
5. How much does an emergency dental visit cost in 2026? — Without insurance, an emergency exam runs about $100 to $300, a simple extraction $150 to $300, a root canal $900 to $1,650 on a front tooth and $1,350 to $2,750 on a molar, and abscess treatment $450 to $1,650. In-network PPO rates are lower.
6. What should I do for a knocked-out tooth? — Handle it by the crown, not the root, rinse it gently, and try to reseat it in the socket or keep it in milk. Get to a dentist within 30 to 60 minutes for the best chance of saving it.
