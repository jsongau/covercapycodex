## What counts as a dental emergency
A dental emergency is a problem that needs urgent care to stop pain, save a tooth, or prevent a spreading infection. Common ones:
- Severe or throbbing tooth pain that over-the-counter painkillers do not control (often a sign of infection or abscess).
- Knocked-out (avulsed) tooth. Best chance of saving it is within 30 to 60 minutes.
- Broken or cracked tooth, especially if the nerve is exposed.
- Lost filling or crown that exposes the tooth.
- Dental abscess (painful swelling, sometimes with bad taste or fever). Will not heal on its own.
- Facial or jaw swelling, uncontrolled bleeding after an extraction.

Not usually emergencies (can wait for a regular appointment): a small chip with no pain, a dull ache that responds to painkillers, a lost crown with no pain, mild sensitivity.

## Same-day care
Same-day emergency dentistry is widely available. Emergency dentists offer same-day appointments, after-hours slots, and rapid evaluation. Call ahead, describe the symptom, and ask for the soonest urgent slot.

### Source
- BriQ Dental, "Emergency Dentist Same Day, What to Expect" — https://www.briqdental.com/can-you-get-same-day-emergency-dental-treatment-what-to-expect/ (accessed 2026-06-26)

## What to do right now (self-care before you are seen)
- Knocked-out tooth: handle by the crown not the root, rinse gently, try to reseat it in the socket or keep it in milk, see a dentist within an hour.
- Severe pain or abscess: rinse with warm salt water, take OTC pain relief as directed, do not put aspirin on the gum, call for an urgent visit. Swelling that spreads to the eye or neck, fever, or trouble breathing or swallowing is a medical emergency, go to the ER.
- Lost filling/crown: keep the area clean, avoid chewing on that side, see the dentist to re-cement.

## PPO emergency / urgent coverage
- PPO dental plans typically cover emergency palliative treatment (pain relief and stabilizing care) when a tooth flares up, and the underlying procedure (extraction, root canal, etc.) is covered at the plan's normal category rate.
- In-network: your share is based on the negotiated fee schedule, the cheaper path.
- Out-of-network: you may owe the difference between the plan payment and the dentist's full charge.
- Restorative emergency work (extraction, root canal, crown) is usually a basic or major service, paid at roughly 50% to 80% after deductible and any waiting period, so the same plan rules apply, just on an urgent timeline. Verify your own plan.

## ER vs dental office for dental pain
- ER physicians cannot drill, fill, root-canal, or extract teeth, and ERs have no dental chair or drill. A dental ER visit usually ends with antibiotics and a painkiller plus a referral back to a dentist.
- Cost gap is large: a dental ER visit commonly runs about $400 to $1,500, with 2025 data showing average bills around $2,437, versus roughly $90 to $200 for a dentist visit for the same complaint.
- There are an estimated 2 million U.S. hospital ER visits for dental pain each year; the ADA notes most could be handled by a dentist, saving an estimated $1.7 billion.
- Go to the ER only for a true medical emergency: facial swelling spreading toward the eye or neck, difficulty breathing or swallowing, high fever, uncontrolled bleeding, or trauma. Otherwise see a dentist.
