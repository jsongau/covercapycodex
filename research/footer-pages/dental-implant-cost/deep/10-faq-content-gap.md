## B. FAQ set (10 to 12 pairs)

Each answer is self-contained, factual, 40 to 65 words, written to be lifted cleanly by
an answer engine. Items marked **[ACCORDION]** are the recommended on-page accordion plus
FAQPage schema set (7 items). The rest are strong supporting answers for prose or an
expanded accordion.

### 1. How much does a single dental implant cost in 2026? **[ACCORDION]**
A single tooth implant in the United States typically costs $3,000 to $6,000 all in,
covering the surgical post, the abutment, and the crown, with an average near $4,500.
High-cost metro areas such as New York, Los Angeles, and San Francisco can run 20 to 40
percent higher. Add-ons like extraction or bone grafting raise the total. Always confirm
your quote with the office.

### 2. Does PPO dental insurance cover dental implants? **[ACCORDION]**
Many PPO plans do, treating implants as a major service that pays roughly 20 to 60 percent
after a waiting period, up to the annual maximum. Coverage is not universal. Some plans,
such as Aetna Dental Direct and UHC Primary Dental, exclude implants entirely. Confirm the
plan covers implants before enrolling, and verify the exact terms with the carrier.

### 3. How much does a dental implant cost with insurance? **[ACCORDION]**
Even with a PPO plan, most people still pay $1,500 to $3,000 out of pocket for a single
implant. Plans commonly cap annual benefits at $1,000 to $2,000, and one implant usually
costs more than that cap, so the plan covers only part of the bill in a single benefit
year. Your share depends on your specific plan and quote.

### 4. Does Medicare or Medicaid cover dental implants? **[ACCORDION]**
Original Medicare does not cover dental implants in 2026, and it confirmed it will not
expand routine dental coverage. Some Medicare Advantage plans add limited implant
benefits, and Medicaid covers implants only in certain states, usually with strict limits.
A standalone PPO dental plan is the more common route to implant help. Verify any benefit
with the plan directly.

### 5. Why are dental implants so expensive? **[ACCORDION]**
Implants replace both the tooth root and the crown, so the price reflects oral surgery,
medical-grade titanium and zirconia, three-dimensional imaging, digital planning, lab
work, and several months of staged visits. Practices also carry heavy equipment and
sterilization costs. Geography adds 20 to 40 percent in high-cost metros. The figure is
high because it buys a long-lasting, surgically placed replacement.

### 6. Are dental implants worth the cost? **[ACCORDION]**
For many patients, yes. An implant is the only option that preserves jawbone and functions
like a natural tooth, and it can last decades with care. Bridges and dentures cost less
upfront but are typically replaced every 5 to 15 years, so implants often become more
cost-effective over time. The right choice depends on your budget, health, and dentist's
advice.

### 7. How long do dental implants last?
The titanium post is designed to last 15 to 25 years or more, and often a lifetime with
good oral hygiene and regular checkups. The crown on top usually wears sooner and may need
replacement after 10 to 15 years. Longevity depends on bone health, smoking, grinding, and
upkeep. Ask your dentist what to expect for your case.

### 8. Implant versus bridge: which costs less? **[ACCORDION]**
A bridge usually costs less upfront, often $2,000 to $3,000, while a single implant
typically runs $3,000 to $6,000. Bridges are replaced roughly every 10 to 15 years, so
implants can cost less over several decades. PPO plans more often cover bridges than
implants. Compare lifetime cost and your plan's terms, and follow your dentist's
recommendation.

### 9. What is the cheapest way to get a dental implant? **[ACCORDION]**
Stay in-network so negotiated PPO fees apply, choose a plan without a missing-tooth clause
if you already have a gap, and stage the surgery and crown across two benefit years to use
two annual maximums. Dental schools and community clinics can also reduce fees. Get a
written predetermination of benefits before treatment begins.

### 10. Do same-day or teeth-in-a-day implants cost more?
Same-day, or immediate-load, implants place a temporary tooth the day of surgery. Single
immediate-load implants fall in a similar range to traditional ones, while same-day
full-arch work is priced like other full-arch cases, often tens of thousands per arch. Not
everyone qualifies, since it depends on bone and gum health. Confirm candidacy and price
with the office.

### 11. Is getting a dental implant painful, and how long does it take?
The placement is done under local anesthesia, and most patients report only mild soreness
afterward that over-the-counter pain relief manages well. A single placement takes about
one to two hours. Traditional implants then heal over three to nine months before the final
crown, while same-day options restore a temporary tooth sooner. Your dentist will outline
your timeline.

### 12. What is the missing-tooth clause and why does it matter for implants? **[ACCORDION]**
A missing-tooth clause means the plan will not pay to replace a tooth that was already
missing before your coverage started. It is a leading reason implant claims get denied. If
you already have a gap, check this clause before buying, because some plans include it and
some do not. Confirm the wording with the carrier.

## C. Content-gap list — sections to add to outrank competitors

Each line names a subtopic the strongest 2026 ranking pages cover, with what CoverCapy
should add. Cost figures defer to workstreams 1 to 5.

1. **Implant vs bridge vs denture cost comparison** — small comparison table: upfront cost
   range, typical lifespan, how often replaced, and which PPO plans more commonly cover.
   Frame as lifetime cost, not just upfront. This is a top competitor section CoverCapy
   lacks.

2. **Are implants worth it / longevity** — a short value section pairing the higher upfront
   price with 15 to 25 plus year post lifespan and jawbone preservation. Captures the
   high-volume "worth it" and "how long do they last" queries.

3. **Why are implants so expensive** — a brief breakdown of what the price buys (surgery,
   materials, imaging, lab, staged visits, metro premium). Reframes sticker shock and sets
   up the savings and financing sections.

4. **Medicare and Medicaid coverage** — explicit callout that Original Medicare does not
   cover implants in 2026, Medicare Advantage may add limited benefits, and Medicaid
   varies by state. Positions a standalone PPO plan as the practical path. High-intent
   senior traffic gap.

5. **Same-day / teeth-in-a-day and process timeline** — explain immediate-load implants,
   candidacy, and the traditional 3 to 9 month timeline versus same-day restoration.
   Answers process and comfort questions and adds dwell-time depth.

6. **Pain, recovery, and what to expect** — one short reassurance block (local anesthesia,
   mild soreness, OTC relief, healing window). Low competition for CoverCapy's audience and
   improves topical completeness.

7. **Cheaper-care routes beyond financing** — extend the savings section to name dental
   schools and community clinics as lower-cost options, alongside the existing in-network,
   two-benefit-year, and predetermination strategies. Mention dental tourism only with a
   caution note (follow-up care, standards vary); do not endorse.

8. **Cost-by-component clarity** — ensure the staged-billing table (extraction, graft, post,
   abutment, crown) is prominent; several top pages lead with it. CoverCapy already has the
   data in workstream 1; surface it high on the page.

9. **State / metro cost variation** — a one-line note that prices swing 20 to 40 percent by
   region, with high-cost metros named, so the page reads as nationally aware. Defer exact
   figures to workstream 1.

10. **Insurance worked example** — keep and emphasize the $4,500 implant, 50 percent, $1,500
    annual-max worked example from the outline; competitors rarely show the math, and it is
    a strong GEO-extractable, trust-building block.
