## 2. Plain explainer of each option

### Implant-retained / snap-in overdenture (removable)
A full removable denture that clicks onto 2 to 4 implants per arch using locator
attachments. More stable than a traditional denture that rests on the gums, but it still
comes out for cleaning. Lowest-cost implant route to a full arch. Lower jaw often needs
only 2 implants; upper jaw usually needs 4. Aspen Dental lists snap-in implant dentures
at an average near $8,109 per arch (range $5,705 to $12,956 in 2025 internal data).
Trade-off: attachment parts (O-rings, locator caps) wear out and are replaced every 12 to
24 months at roughly $100 to $300 per visit, and the denture needs a reline or
replacement every 5 to 10 years.

### All-on-4 (fixed)
A fixed, non-removable full arch screwed onto 4 implants, with the back two implants
angled to use more available bone and often avoid bone grafting. The most cost-effective
fixed full-arch option because it uses the fewest implants. Same-day provisional teeth
("teeth in a day") are possible when the implants are stable enough at placement, but not
every case qualifies. CareCredit (April 2026) anchors All-on-4 at $11,640 to $27,500 per
arch, averaging $15,176.

### All-on-6 and 3-on-6 (fixed, 6 implants)
Both use 6 implants per arch instead of 4, adding support, redundancy, and better force
distribution for patients with enough bone or heavier bite forces. 3-on-6 specifically
splits the prosthesis into three sections so a single section can be replaced later
without redoing the whole arch. The extra 2 implants and longer surgery add roughly
$3,000 to $5,000 over All-on-4. CareCredit (Nov 2025) anchors 3-on-6 at $9,708 to $24,091
per arch, averaging $12,474. All-on-6 is commonly billed $17,000 to $32,000 per arch.

### Fixed full-arch bridge variations / full-mouth reconstruction
At the top of the range, some patients are restored with individual crown-on-implant
restorations rather than a single bridge. An upper arch can take 10 to 14 implants and a
lower arch 8 to 10. Combined with zirconia and advanced grafting, these run $60,000 to
$90,000+ for both arches.

### Implant-supported bridge (multi-tooth, partial)
Replaces 2 to 4 adjacent missing teeth using 2 implants as anchors with false teeth
suspended between them. Fixed and non-removable; does not require shaving down neighboring
natural teeth the way a traditional bridge does. Trade-off: the middle (pontic) teeth are
not anchored into bone, so the bone under them gradually shrinks. Average near $5,200,
reaching $16,000+ for larger spans or premium materials.

## 3. Material tiers (acrylic vs zirconia) and price impact

| Material | Per-arch effect | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Acrylic / PMMA hybrid (titanium frame) | Base price; All-on-4 acrylic typically $15,000 to $25,000 per arch | Cheaper upfront, easy to repair, but wears and fractures sooner |
| Monolithic zirconia | Adds roughly $5,000 to $13,000 per arch (one source: ~$7,800) | Harder, fracture-resistant, longer-lasting, harder to repair if it chips |

Durability evidence cited in market sources: monolithic zirconia full-arch prostheses
showed higher 5-year survival than titanium-acrylic hybrids (about 94% vs 83%). Zirconia
arches commonly run $25,000 to $38,000+, and full-mouth zirconia can exceed $90,000.

Implant-post material (separate from the prosthesis): titanium posts are standard and
least expensive; metal-free zirconia ceramic posts can add $500 to $1,500 per single
implant. Crown / prosthetic material runs from porcelain-fused-to-metal (least expensive)
to monolithic zirconia (mid) to layered all-ceramic (most expensive).

## 4. What drives the spread

The same solution can vary 2x to 3x in price. The main drivers:

1. **Number of implants.** Each added implant raises the surgical fee, the prosthetic
   component fee, and the imaging fee. 6 implants (All-on-6 / 3-on-6) cost more than 4
   (All-on-4); a 2-implant bridge costs less than 4 individual implants for the same span.
2. **Prosthetic material.** Acrylic vs zirconia is the single biggest prosthesis-level
   swing, roughly $5,000 to $13,000 per arch.
3. **Bone grafting and sinus lift.** Grafting commonly $549 to $5,148 per site; sinus
   augmentation adds several hundred to several thousand dollars. All-on-4's angled rear
   implants often avoid grafting, which is part of why it is cheaper.
4. **Provisional vs final prosthesis.** Same-day "teeth in a day" provisional, then a
   final prosthesis later; some cases need a healing period before the final is fitted.
   The final (especially zirconia) is the costlier element.
5. **Sedation.** Oral sedation $200 to $500; IV sedation adds $500 to $1,200; general
   anesthesia more, used for full-arch surgical days or anxious patients.
6. **Location.** Major-metro fees run 30% to 50% higher than smaller markets; big-city
   practices can list 2x to 3x rural rates. (Georgia full-arch ~$22,000 to $30,000 per
   arch; Arkansas ~$20,000 to $28,000 per arch as regional examples.)
7. **Provider experience.** Periodontists and oral surgeons with implant fellowships
   charge more than general dentists; the premium reflects training and lower complication
   risk, most relevant for complex full-arch and zygomatic work.
8. **What the quote includes.** A legitimate all-inclusive full-arch quote should cover
   implants, extraction of failing teeth in that arch, 3D CBCT scan and surgical planning,
   IV sedation, and the final prosthesis. Always ask for a CDT-coded, itemized plan to
   compare quotes line by line.

## 5. Snap-in overdenture vs fixed full-arch (decision framing)

- An implant overdenture costs roughly half of a fixed full arch (~$7,000 to $15,000 per
  arch vs ~$20,000 to $36,000 per arch for fixed All-on-4).
- Overdentures use fewer implants (2 lower / 4 upper vs 4 to 8 for fixed), which is the
  main reason for the price gap.
- Fixed arches, especially zirconia, can last 15 to 20+ years with routine cleaning;
  overdentures carry recurring maintenance (attachment swaps every 12 to 24 months,
  reline/replacement every 5 to 10 years).
- The overdenture is the value play for patients who want implant retention but cannot
  afford fixed, delivering a large quality-of-life gain at a lower entry price.

- Lead with the per-arch table (1a); it is the spine of the page. Full-mouth (1b) and
  multi-tooth (1c) are supporting tables.
- Use ranges in copy, never single "average" numbers without the range around them.
- The cleanest authoritative anchors are the CareCredit-via-NewMouth figures (named
  procedure guides with publish dates) and Aspen Dental's published patient pricing. Lead
  citations with those; treat clinic-blog ranges as corroboration.
- The strongest CoverCapy hook here: even the cheapest fixed full arch ($11,600+/arch) is
  many times a PPO annual maximum ($1,000 to $2,000), so PPO coverage offsets only a small
  slice. This connects directly to the plan-facts section in `01-research.md`.
- Tie material tiers and implant count to the "what drives the spread" list so readers
  understand why two quotes for "All-on-4" can differ by $15,000.
