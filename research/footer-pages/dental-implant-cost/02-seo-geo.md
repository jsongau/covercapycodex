## FAQ Q&A list (these go verbatim into FAQPage JSON-LD; 40–60 words each)

1. **How much does a single dental implant cost in 2026?**
   A single tooth implant in the United States typically costs $3,000 to $6,000 all in,
   covering the surgical post, the abutment, and the crown. The average lands near $4,500.
   High-cost metro areas such as New York, Los Angeles, and Chicago run 20 to 40 percent
   higher, sometimes reaching $6,000 to $8,000.

2. **Does PPO dental insurance cover dental implants?**
   Many PPO plans do, treating implants as a major service that pays roughly 20 to 60
   percent after a waiting period, up to the annual maximum. Coverage is not universal. Some
   plans, including Aetna Dental Direct and UHC Primary Dental, exclude implants entirely,
   so confirm the plan covers implants before enrolling.

3. **How much does a dental implant cost with insurance?**
   Even with a PPO plan, most people still pay $1,500 to $3,000 out of pocket for a single
   implant. Plans cap annual benefits at $1,000 to $2,000 in most cases, and a single
   implant usually costs more than that cap, so the plan covers only part of the bill in
   one benefit year.

4. **What is the missing-tooth clause and why does it matter for implants?**
   A missing-tooth clause states the plan will not pay to replace a tooth that was already
   missing before your coverage started. It is a leading reason implant claims get denied.
   If you already have a gap, check this clause before buying a plan, because some plans
   include it and some do not.

5. **How much does a full mouth of dental implants cost?**
   Full-arch options such as All-on-4 typically cost $20,000 to $35,000 per arch with an
   acrylic hybrid, and $35,000 to $90,000 or more per arch for premium zirconia. A full
   mouth, both arches, commonly runs $30,000 to $50,000, rising further for premium
   materials or more implants per arch.

6. **Can I finance a dental implant with monthly payments?**
   Yes. Most patients finance implants through CareCredit promotional plans, in-house
   office payment plans, or third-party lenders such as Cherry, several of which approve
   less-than-perfect credit. HSA and FSA funds also apply. CoverCapy helps you pair
   financing with a PPO plan so insurance covers part and payments spread the rest.

7. **What is the cheapest way to get a dental implant?**
   Stay in-network so negotiated PPO fees apply, stage the surgical and crown phases across
   two benefit years to use two annual maximums, choose a plan without a missing-tooth
   clause if you already have a gap, and get a predetermination of benefits in writing
   before treatment begins.

8. **How long is the waiting period for implant coverage?**
   Major-service waits typically run 6 to 12 months. A few plans, such as Ameritas
   PrimeStar, pay a reduced percentage from day one with no waiting period. If you need an
   implant soon, the waiting period is often the single most important plan feature to
   compare.
