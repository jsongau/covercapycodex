# How Dentists Get Contracted and Credentialed With Delta Dental

*Part of the "Inside Delta Dental" research hub. Educational reference only. This explains how the process generally works for dental offices; it is not legal, tax, or business advice, and specifics vary by state Delta Dental company and by individual contract.*

**Last reviewed: June 2026**

---

## Summary

Delta Dental is the largest dental benefits carrier in the United States, and most general dentists encounter a decision early in their careers about whether to join one or both of its two fee-for-service networks: Delta Dental PPO and Delta Dental Premier. A dentist can participate in Premier only, PPO only, or both, and the choice shapes how much the practice is paid and how much patient volume Delta Dental tends to steer toward the office.

To join, a licensed dentist submits a participation request, completes credentialing (increasingly handled through CAQH at no cost to the dentist), signs the relevant Participating Provider Agreement, and agrees to a fee schedule. Once contracted, a participating dentist agrees to accept Delta Dental's allowed amount as payment in full for covered services and cannot balance bill the patient above the patient's calculated share. Credentialing commonly takes roughly 60 to 90 days, and re-credentialing recurs on a multi-year cycle.

The core trade-off is straightforward: PPO carries lower contracted fees but typically delivers more patient steerage because plan designs reward members for choosing PPO dentists, while Premier pays higher contracted fees but offers members smaller savings and therefore creates weaker steerage.

## The two fee-for-service networks: PPO vs Premier

Delta Dental's two main networks are both fee-for-service, meaning the dentist is paid per procedure rather than a flat monthly amount per patient. (Delta Dental also runs DeltaCare USA, a separate prepaid HMO-style closed network that pays capitation, plus Medicare Advantage networks. Those are outside the scope of this PPO and Premier discussion.)

**Delta Dental Premier** is the older, broader network with higher contracted fees. Premier dentists agree to set fees, so they cannot charge a Premier patient more than the contracted amount, but those fees are typically higher than PPO fees.

**Delta Dental PPO** is the network with lower negotiated fees. PPO network dentists agree to accept the reduced PPO fees as payment in full for covered services. In exchange, members are steered toward PPO dentists through plan design.

A key structural fact: a member enrolled in a Delta Dental PPO plan can usually use either a PPO dentist or a Premier dentist, but they save the most at a PPO dentist. Delta Dental describes the member experience as a tier system: members typically save the most at a PPO dentist, a little less at a Premier dentist, and the least at a non-Delta dentist who has no contract at all.

How a practice fits into that tier system depends on which agreements it signs:

| Participation choice | Contracted fees | Patient cost protection | Typical steerage to the office |
|----------------------|-----------------|--------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Premier only | Higher (Premier fees) | Yes, capped at Premier allowance | Moderate; members save less here than at PPO offices |
| PPO only | Lower (PPO fees) | Yes, capped at PPO allowance | Strongest; plan designs reward PPO visits |
| Both PPO and Premier | Depends on member's plan | Yes | Broadest reach across plan types |

How Premier dentists get reimbursed under a PPO plan is set by the employer's plan design. The group can choose to reimburse Premier dentists at Premier fees (lowest member out-of-pocket, plan pays more) or at PPO fees (higher member out-of-pocket at Premier offices, which pushes members toward PPO dentists). This is one reason PPO participation tends to produce more reliable steerage than Premier alone.

For a dentist already in Premier who wants to add PPO, some state Delta companies allow a shortcut: complete the PPO Participating Provider Agreement and submit it, rather than restarting the entire enrollment.

Sources: Delta Dental "How our networks work" (administrators blog); Delta Dental of Washington "PPO vs Premier"; Delta Dental of Illinois "PPO vs Premier"; Delta Dental "Join the network."

## How reimbursement and balance billing work for participating dentists

When a participating dentist treats a Delta Dental patient, Delta Dental does not simply pay the dentist's billed fee. Payment is based on the lower of three numbers, as stated on Delta Dental's "Join the network" page: the applicable fee schedule, the maximum contract allowance, or the amount the dentist actually submitted.

The **maximum contract allowance (MCA)**, sometimes called the maximum plan allowance, is the total reimbursement amount on which Delta Dental calculates both its own payment and the patient's share. Delta Dental's own example: if the dentist submits a fee of $120 and the maximum contract allowance is $100, Delta Dental calculates everything based on $100, not $120.

Important characteristics of the allowance, from Delta Dental's "Maximum Contract Allowances" guidance:

- Allowances are derived from a mix of factors that vary by state, including fees seen on claims, fee filings submitted by dentists, current economic factors, and other data.
- Allowances differ by plan type. PPO allowances are, in most cases, different from (and lower than) Premier allowances.
- Allowances differ by 5-digit ZIP code, so the same procedure has different allowed amounts in different areas.
- Delta Dental reviews allowances on an ongoing basis and updates them regularly, usually every 6 to 24 months. In many locales these updates must first be filed for approval by state regulatory agencies.

**Balance-billing rule:** A contracted Delta Dental dentist may not charge a Delta Dental patient more than the amount Delta Dental determines to be the patient's portion. In other words, the participating dentist writes off the difference between their full fee and the allowed amount on covered services. The patient remains responsible only for their calculated share (such as a deductible, coinsurance, or amounts beyond the annual maximum), not for the write-off. This write-off obligation is the central financial commitment of network participation, and it is why the PPO versus Premier fee difference matters so much to a practice's economics.

(Under DeltaCare USA, the prepaid HMO product, maximum contract allowances do not apply; enrollees pay fixed copayments and likewise cannot be balance billed. This file focuses on the PPO and Premier fee-for-service model.)

Sources: Delta Dental "Maximum Contract Allowances"; Delta Dental "Join the network"; Michigan Dental Association overview of Delta Dental payment policies.

## Credentialing: requirements, CAQH, timeline, and re-credentialing

Credentialing is the verification step in which Delta Dental confirms a dentist's qualifications before activating a contract. Eligibility, per Delta Dental's "Join the network" page, requires being a licensed, practicing dentist who meets Delta Dental's credentialing requirements.

**The CAQH pathway.** Delta Dental has partnered with CAQH, an industry organization that runs automated credentialing workflows, to simplify both initial credentialing and re-credentialing at no cost to the dentist. All dentists contracted with Delta Dental can credential and re-credential online through CAQH. The two entry points Delta Dental describes:

- New to CAQH: register for the CAQH Provider Data Portal, complete the profile with current information, and authorize Delta Dental to access the data.
- Existing CAQH account: sign in, ensure the profile is current (especially if re-credentialing is due), and confirm Delta Dental has authorization to access the data (global authorization covers this automatically).

Some state Delta Dental companies use additional or alternative systems. For example, certain plans route new providers through an online enrollment system (variously branded as DDS Enroll, AppCentral, or similar DentalXChange tools) that bundles credentialing with network enrollment.

**Typical information and documents requested** during dental credentialing generally include:

- Active state dental license and DEA registration where applicable
- National Provider Identifier (NPI)
- Dental school and education history, plus any specialty training
- Malpractice (professional liability) insurance with proof of coverage
- Work history and practice location details
- Tax Identification Number (TIN or EIN) for the billing entity
- Disclosure of any disciplinary, malpractice, or sanction history

**Timeline.** Delta Dental generally expects credentialing to take roughly 60 to 90 days, and some state Delta Dental plans run longer. Industry credentialing guides commonly recommend starting 90 to 120 days before a target date such as opening a practice, onboarding a new associate, or completing a practice purchase, because approval can take several months and the dentist typically cannot bill as in-network until credentialing finishes.

**Re-credentialing.** Participation is not permanent once granted; Delta Dental periodically re-verifies credentials. Through the CAQH model, CAQH requests re-attestation of the provider's information on a recurring cycle (commonly every 120 days to keep the profile current), and full reverification of credentials is generally required every three years. Keeping the CAQH profile attested and current is what prevents lapses at re-credentialing time.

Sources: Delta Dental "Credentialing and re-credentialing process with CAQH"; Delta Dental "Join the network"; Delta Dental of Colorado / Delta Dental of Oregon provider credentialing pages; PayerReady dental credentialing guide.

## Step-by-step tutorial: how a dental office joins Delta Dental

This walkthrough describes the general sequence. The exact portal, forms, and contacts depend on which Delta Dental company serves the practice's state.

1. **Confirm eligibility and identify the right Delta Dental company.** Verify the dentist holds an active license in the state of practice. Delta Dental is a federation of state and regional companies, so the practice contracts with the company that covers its location, not a single national entity.

2. **Decide which networks to request: PPO, Premier, or both.** Weigh the lower PPO fees and stronger steerage against the higher Premier fees and weaker steerage. Many offices choose both to maximize patient reach, but that means accepting PPO fees for the large share of patients on PPO plans.

3. **Submit the participation request.** On Delta Dental's site this is the "Complete request form" / provider request webform. Some states send back an email with a link to begin enrollment in their online system once the request is received.

4. **Set up or update the CAQH Provider Data Portal profile.** Register a new CAQH account or sign in to an existing one, complete every required field, upload supporting documents (license, malpractice insurance, NPI, education, work history), and attest that the information is accurate.

5. **Authorize Delta Dental to access the CAQH profile.** Grant Delta Dental access (global authorization handles this for all participating payers at once). Without authorization, Delta Dental cannot pull the data it needs to credential the dentist.

6. **Complete credentialing review.** Delta Dental verifies the submitted credentials. Plan for roughly 60 to 90 days, and respond quickly to any follow-up requests, since missing items are the most common cause of delay.

7. **Sign the Participating Provider Agreement(s).** Execute the agreement for each network being joined. A dentist already in Premier who is adding PPO can often just complete and submit the PPO Participating Provider Agreement rather than re-enrolling from scratch.

8. **Confirm the fee schedule and the practice's ZIP-based allowances.** Review the PPO and/or Premier fee schedule that will govern reimbursement. Remember payment is the lowest of fee schedule, maximum contract allowance, or submitted amount, and that allowances vary by ZIP code.

9. **Set up payment and administrative logistics.** Complete Electronic Funds Transfer (EFT) authorization for direct deposit, confirm the correct TIN/EIN, and register the office's National Provider Identifier so claims process cleanly.

10. **Register for the online provider portal (Provider Tools).** Create an account so staff can check eligibility, verify benefits, submit and track claims, and view payment information once the office goes live.

11. **Go live and maintain the relationship.** After activation the office appears in Delta Dental's Find a Dentist directory for the joined networks. Keep the CAQH profile attested on its recurring cycle, update any practice changes (address, ownership, associates) promptly, and complete re-credentialing roughly every three years.

Sources: Delta Dental "Join the network"; Delta Dental "Credentialing and re-credentialing process with CAQH"; Delta Dental "Maximum Contract Allowances"; Delta Dental administrative and claim forms page.

## Provider portal and Provider Tools

Once contracted, dental offices manage day-to-day administration through Delta Dental's online provider portal, marketed as Provider Tools. After registering for an online account, office staff can:

- Check patient eligibility and confirm active coverage
- Look up plan benefits, coverage levels, and remaining annual maximums
- Submit claims and pre-treatment estimates, and track claim status
- View payment and remittance information
- Update practice information and access administrative and claim forms (for example, EFT authorization, address change, dentist directory update, and provider dispute forms)

Provider Tools is the practical reason many offices value network participation beyond fees alone: Delta Dental processes claims directly and pays the dentist, which reduces the office's billing burden and speeds payment compared with chasing reimbursement from patients on out-of-network claims.

Sources: Delta Dental "Provider Tools"; Delta Dental "Maximum Contract Allowances" page (Online Services and forms sections).

## Why a practice would, or would not, join PPO

This section lays out the considerations practices commonly weigh. It is descriptive, not a recommendation.

**Reasons a practice might join PPO:**

- Patient volume and steerage. Plan designs reward members for choosing PPO dentists (higher coverage percentages, higher maximums, lower or no deductibles in network), so listing as a PPO dentist can drive new-patient flow, especially in competitive markets.
- Directory visibility. PPO participation places the office in the directory tier members are actively pushed toward.
- Predictable, direct payment. Delta Dental pays the dentist directly and handles claims, improving cash-flow reliability.
- Filling chair time. A newer or growing practice with open capacity may accept lower per-procedure fees in exchange for more patients.

**Reasons a practice might decline PPO (or stay Premier-only or out of network):**

- Lower contracted fees. PPO allowances are typically the lowest of the network options, which compresses per-procedure margin.
- Write-off obligation. The practice must accept the allowed amount as payment in full and cannot balance bill on covered services, so the gap between the practice's full fee and the PPO allowance is absorbed by the office.
- Established or high-demand practices. A practice with a full schedule and strong reputation may not need the steerage and may prefer Premier-level fees or out-of-network flexibility.
- ZIP-based allowance variability. Because allowances differ by ZIP code and are revised on a 6-to-24-month cycle, a practice in a lower-allowance area may find PPO economics less attractive.

The honest framing is a volume-versus-margin trade. PPO generally trades lower per-visit revenue for more visits and easier administration; declining PPO preserves higher per-visit revenue at the cost of the steerage and directory placement PPO participation provides.

Sources: Delta Dental "How our networks work"; Delta Dental "Maximum Contract Allowances"; Delta Dental "Join the network"; Delta Dental of Washington "PPO vs Premier."

## Hub implications

For the "Inside Delta Dental" hub, this research supports several content and product angles:

- **Explainer value.** Patients rarely understand that a "Delta Dental dentist" can be Premier, PPO, or both, and that the network badge predicts their out-of-pocket cost. A clear PPO-vs-Premier explainer is high-utility and differentiates CoverCapy from generic carrier pages.
- **Verification angle reinforced.** Because allowances vary by ZIP and update every 6 to 24 months, and because a dentist's PPO versus Premier status directly changes patient cost, CoverCapy's verification flow (confirming which network a specific office actually participates in) is genuinely useful rather than cosmetic.
- **Directory accuracy matters.** Network status can change at re-credentialing or when a practice adds or drops PPO. Any CoverCapy listing that labels an office as a Delta Dental PPO office should be treated as time-sensitive and ideally re-verified, mirroring Delta's own 3-year re-credentialing rhythm.
- **Dentist-facing opportunity.** The step-by-step join tutorial is a natural lead magnet for a future provider-facing surface, and ties to CoverCapy's accredited and platinum tiers if the platform ever helps offices manage network participation or patient verification.
- **Tone guardrail.** Keep this content educational and non-advisory. The PPO join decision is a business judgment for each practice, so CoverCapy should explain the trade-offs rather than tell dentists what to do.
