# Dental Infection, Swelling & Abscess Management

**Educational reference only. Not medical or dental advice. A dental abscess will not heal on its own and is a true urgency, see a dentist promptly, and go to the ER for the red-flag signs below.**

---

## What a dental abscess is

A dental abscess is a pocket of pus caused by a bacterial infection in or around a tooth. Two main types:

- **Periapical abscess** forms inside the tooth, at the tip of the root. Bacteria enter the pulp (the innermost tissue) through a deep cavity, a crack, a chip, or prior dental work, then spread to the root tip and surrounding bone (Cleveland Clinic; Mayo Clinic).
- **Periodontal abscess** forms in the gums, in the space between the tooth and gum. Gum disease (periodontitis) is the number one risk factor (Cleveland Clinic).

### Why infection causes pain and swelling
As bacteria multiply, the body sends immune cells and fluid to fight them, producing pus. Pus has nowhere to escape, so pressure builds inside a rigid tooth or confined gum pocket. That pressure on nerves drives the classic severe, throbbing pain, and the inflammatory fluid spreading into soft tissue causes facial and gum swelling.

### Common symptoms
- Severe, throbbing toothache, often radiating to the ear, jaw, or neck (Cleveland Clinic)
- Pain when chewing or biting; sensitivity to hot and cold
- Facial or gum swelling; a swollen "bump" on the gum (periodontal)
- Bad breath or a bad/salty taste (a draining abscess)
- Swollen, tender lymph nodes in the neck or under the jaw
- Fever

---

## How dentists treat infection and reduce swelling

The goal is to eliminate the infection by removing its source. **Antibiotics alone do not cure an abscess**, the source must be removed (NHS; Mayo Clinic).

1. **Incision and drainage (I&D).** The dentist makes a small cut to drain the pus, then washes the area with saline. A small rubber drain may be left to keep the area open while swelling goes down (Mayo Clinic).
2. **Remove the source, the definitive treatment:**
   - **Root canal** to remove diseased pulp and drain the abscess while saving the tooth, or
   - **Extraction** if the tooth cannot be saved, draining the abscess at the same time (Mayo Clinic).
3. **Antibiotics, only when indicated.** Prescribed when infection is spreading, when there are systemic symptoms (fever, feeling unwell), or in high-risk/immunocompromised patients. They are not generally needed for a localized abscess in an otherwise healthy person (NHS; Mayo Clinic; ADA guideline).
   - **Amoxicillin** is the typical first-line oral antibiotic (ADA; AAE).
   - For penicillin allergy, alternatives such as **cephalexin** (mild allergy), **clindamycin**, or **azithromycin** may be used. Note recent guidance cautions against routine clindamycin due to a high risk of *C. difficile* colitis (ADA; UBC Therapeutics Initiative).
   - *No dosing is given here, prescribing is a clinician's decision.*

> **Antibiotics are a bridge, not a cure.** They can slow spread, but repeated antibiotics without drainage are ineffective and do not prevent the infection from spreading (NHS).

## Adjuncts patients are commonly told to use (temporary relief only)

- **Warm saltwater rinses**, about 1/2 teaspoon salt in a glass of warm water, swished ~30 seconds, repeated every few hours. Eases inflammation and helps cleanse the area.
- **Cold compress** on the outside of the cheek, 15–20 minutes on/off, to ease facial swelling and pain.
- **Head elevation** (extra pillows when lying down) to reduce throbbing and swelling.
- **Hydration** and soft foods.
- **OTC analgesics** such as ibuprofen or acetaminophen for pain, used per label.

None of these treat the infection, they only buy comfort until a dentist removes the source.

---

## GO TO THE ER (or call 911) IMMEDIATELY IF YOU HAVE:

- **Difficulty breathing** or noisy/whistling breathing (stridor)
- **Difficulty swallowing** or drooling because you cannot swallow saliva
- **Swelling spreading to the eye, neck, or floor of the mouth** (possible **Ludwig's angina**, a rapidly progressing, life-threatening cellulitis of the mouth floor)
- **Swelling that is rapidly worsening** or feels firm/hard rather than soft
- **High fever**, chills, or feeling very unwell
- **Confusion, rapid heartbeat, very fast breathing, dizziness, or clammy skin** (possible **sepsis**)

These signal that infection may be threatening the airway or has entered the bloodstream.

---

## Why an untreated dental infection can become life-threatening

A confined dental infection can spread along tissue planes into the deep spaces of the neck and the floor of the mouth. **Ludwig's angina** can swell the airway shut, and odontogenic infection is its leading cause, with a reported mortality around 8% from airway loss and oxygen deprivation (NIH/StatPearls; Cleveland Clinic). Infection can also enter the bloodstream and trigger **sepsis**, a body-wide reaction that can cause multiple-organ failure and death. Deep neck infections and Ludwig's angina are hospital emergencies treated with IV antibiotics plus surgical drainage (Medscape; NIH).

## Timeline: why you should not "wait it out"

A tooth abscess will not resolve on its own (Mayo Clinic). Pain may briefly ease if the abscess bursts and drains, but the infection remains and continues to spread. Severe odontogenic infections can progress to airway-threatening swelling or sepsis within hours to a day or two (NHS guidance defines severe infection by sepsis, difficulty swallowing, or impending airway obstruction). Earlier drainage and source removal mean simpler treatment, a better chance of saving the tooth, and a far lower risk of an ER visit. **Seek care the same day, and use the ER list above without delay.**
