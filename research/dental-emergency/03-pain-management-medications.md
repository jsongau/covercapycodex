# How Dentists Manage Acute Dental Pain

*Educational overview of the standard of care. Last reviewed: 2026-06-24.*

> **Important:** This page is for education only. It is **not** dosing
> guidance and **not** a substitute for professional care. Only a licensed
> dentist or physician can diagnose your problem and decide whether any
> medication is appropriate for you. What is safe and effective depends on
> your diagnosis, the severity, your medical history, allergies, other
> medications, and local regulations. Every dentist is different, and
> prescribing choices vary by patient and situation. If you have a
> toothache, swelling, or a dental injury, contact a dentist promptly.

---

## The big picture: relieve pain, then fix the cause

Medication for a toothache is almost always a **bridge**, not a cure. Acute
dental pain usually comes from an inflamed or dying tooth nerve (pulpitis),
an infection (abscess), or trauma. Painkillers can quiet the symptoms, but
the pain reliably returns until a dentist treats the underlying problem.
The two pillars of dental pain care are therefore:

1. **Short-term symptom control** with non-opioid medication, and
2. **Definitive treatment** that removes the source of the pain.

---

## First-line: non-opioid analgesics (NSAID + acetaminophen)

In **February 2024**, the American Dental Association (ADA) published a new
clinical practice guideline establishing **non-steroidal anti-inflammatory
drugs (NSAIDs)** — alone or combined with **acetaminophen** — as the
**first-line** treatment for short-term dental pain in adults and
adolescents aged 12 and older. This applies to pain after simple or
surgical tooth extractions and to the temporary management of toothache.

**Why this is the standard of care — the evidence:**

- A JADA overview of systematic reviews covering **more than 58,000
  patients** after third-molar ("wisdom tooth") extractions found that a
  combination of **ibuprofen plus acetaminophen** was **more effective than
  any opioid-containing regimen** at reducing pain — and was associated with
  a **lower risk of adverse events**.
- Randomized trials show the ibuprofen-acetaminophen combination provides
  **greater relief than either drug alone**, because the two work through
  different mechanisms (anti-inflammatory vs. central pain modulation).
- The U.S. **CDC** echoes this: non-opioid therapies are *at least as
  effective* as opioids for many common acute pain conditions, including
  dental pain, and NSAIDs have been found **more effective than opioids for
  surgical dental pain**.

Because NSAIDs are not appropriate for everyone (e.g., certain stomach,
kidney, cardiovascular, bleeding, or pregnancy considerations), a dentist
tailors the choice to the individual — another reason self-medication is
discouraged.

---

## Local anesthesia: immediate relief, but temporary

To stop pain quickly in the chair, dentists use **local anesthetics**
(e.g., lidocaine) to numb the tooth and surrounding area. Relief often
arrives within minutes. This is what makes definitive procedures
comfortable, but the numbness wears off in hours — so local anesthesia
**buys time and enables treatment; it does not fix the problem.**

---

## Definitive treatment is the real fix

Lasting relief comes from addressing the source:

- **Root canal therapy** removes the infected or inflamed pulp, disinfects
  and seals the tooth, and typically preserves the natural tooth.
- **Extraction** removes a tooth that cannot be saved.
- **Drainage / incision of an abscess** releases pus and pressure, often
  giving near-immediate relief — but drainage alone is not enough; the
  infection source must still be treated to prevent recurrence.

Once the cause is treated, the need for ongoing pain medicine usually drops
sharply.

---

## Antibiotics: when they are — and are NOT — indicated

A persistent myth is that a toothache needs antibiotics. The ADA's
evidence-based guideline on antibiotic use (antibiotic **stewardship**) is
explicit: for most cases of **pain alone**, antibiotics are **not**
indicated. The fix is **dental treatment**, supported if needed by
**over-the-counter pain relievers**.

**Generally NOT indicated** (in healthy/immunocompetent adults):

- Symptomatic **irreversible pulpitis** (with or without apical
  periodontitis) — antibiotics do not treat it; the tooth needs a procedure.
- **Pulp necrosis** with symptomatic apical periodontitis.
- A **localized** acute apical abscess in a patient who can get prompt
  dental treatment.

**Generally indicated:** signs of **spreading infection** or **systemic
involvement** — facial swelling, fever, malaise, lymph node involvement — or
in medically compromised patients, where antibiotics are an **adjunct** to
(not a replacement for) definitive dental care.

**Common agents (educational, not a prescription):** when antibiotics are
warranted, **amoxicillin** is typically the first-line choice; for patients
with a penicillin allergy, **clindamycin** or other alternatives may be
considered. Antibiotic selection, dose, and duration are decisions only a
clinician can make.

---

## Opioids: last resort, used sparingly

Dentistry has shifted decisively **away from routine opioid prescribing**:

- Dental opioid prescriptions in the U.S. fell roughly **45%** between 2016
  (~16 million) and 2022 (~8.9 million).
- The ADA and CDC agree that opioids (e.g., hydrocodone or codeine
  combinations) should be reserved for the **limited cases** where
  non-opioids are insufficient or contraindicated — not given "just in
  case."
- When opioids are warranted, guidance calls for **immediate-release
  formulations at the lowest effective dose for the shortest duration**
  (often 1–3 days), shared decision-making with the patient, extra caution
  in adolescents and young adults, checking prescription-monitoring
  databases, and counseling on the real risk that short-term use can lead to
  long-term dependence.

**Practices vary.** Some clinical situations and patients differ, and a
dentist's judgment — within professional guidelines and local law —
determines the plan.

---

## First-line vs. last-resort, at a glance

| Tier | Approach | Role |
|------|----------|------|
| First-line | NSAID (e.g., ibuprofen) ± acetaminophen | Default for most acute dental pain |
| Procedural | Local anesthesia + definitive treatment | Stops pain at the source |
| Conditional | Antibiotics | Only for spreading/systemic infection, as an adjunct |
| Last resort | Opioids | Rare, short, lowest effective dose, when non-opioids fail/are contraindicated |

---

## Key takeaways

- Modern evidence shows an **ibuprofen + acetaminophen** combination is
  often **as effective as or better than opioids** for acute dental pain.
- **Local anesthesia and definitive treatment** — not pills — are what
  truly resolve the problem.
- **Antibiotics treat infection, not pain**, and are unnecessary for most
  toothaches.
- **Opioids are a last resort**, used sparingly and briefly.
- **Only a licensed dentist or physician can prescribe.** Always consult one
  before taking or combining medications.
