# The Emergency / Limited Dental Exam: What to Expect & What It Costs

*Educational research notes. Accessed 2026-06-24. US figures.*

---

## The Code: D0140 — "Limited Oral Evaluation, Problem-Focused"

When you call a dentist with a toothache, broken tooth, swelling, or knocked-out tooth, the visit is usually billed as **ADA/CDT code D0140 — limited oral evaluation, problem-focused.** It is the standard "emergency exam" or "urgent visit" code.

- **Problem-focused, not a checkup.** The dentist examines only the area or condition that brought you in. There is no requirement for a full-mouth exam, a complete periodontal charting, or a full medical-history review.
- **Used for:** emergencies, dental trauma, acute pain, infection/abscess, swelling, lost filling or crown, a specific complaint.
- It can be billed on the **same day** as the treatment that follows it (extraction, filling, root canal start, etc.).

### D0140 (Limited) vs D0150 (Comprehensive) — the key contrast

| | **D0140 — Limited** | **D0150 — Comprehensive** |
|---|---|---|
| Purpose | One specific problem / emergency | Full baseline evaluation |
| Scope | Just the problem tooth/area | All soft tissue, hard tissue, perio, oral-cancer screening, full history |
| When used | Pain, trauma, infection, urgent | New patient, or first visit after a long gap |
| Treatment plan | Focused (just the issue) | Complete diagnostic treatment plan |

A comprehensive exam (D0150) is the deep, whole-mouth baseline. A limited exam (D0140) is the targeted, "let's find out what's wrong with *this* tooth, now" visit.

## What the Dentist Actually Does at an Emergency Exam

A focused, fast diagnostic sequence — usually 10–20 minutes:

1. **Focused history.** When did it start? Sharp or dull? Worse with hot, cold, sweets, biting, or lying down? Lingering or quick? Any swelling, fever, bad taste?
2. **Visual exam of the problem tooth/area.** Looking for fracture, decay, lost filling, broken crown, gum swelling, abscess, or a draining sinus tract.
3. **Percussion test (tapping).** Tapping the tooth. Pain to percussion is an early clinical sign of **apical periodontitis** (inflammation at the root tip) — often points to an endodontic (root) problem.
4. **Cold (thermal) test.** A cold spray on a cotton applicator held to the tooth to gauge **pulp vitality.** A quick "ouch that fades" can mean reversible inflammation; **lingering pain** suggests irreversible pulpitis (often needs a root canal); **no response** can mean a non-vital (dead) nerve.
5. **Palpation.** Pressing the gum and bone beside the tooth, feeling for tenderness, swelling, fluctuance (pus), or firmness — signs of spreading infection.
6. **Periodontal probing** of the area if a gum cause is suspected.
7. Sometimes an **electric pulp test (EPT)** to confirm whether the nerve is alive.

## Which X-Rays Are Typical (and Why)

| X-ray | CDT code | What it shows | Typical cash cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Periapical (PA)** | D0220 (first), D0230 (each additional) | The **whole tooth root tip + surrounding bone** — best for abscess, fracture, infection at the root | **$25–$75** (often ~$25–$50) |
| **Bitewing** | D0270–D0274 | Crowns of upper/lower teeth touching — best for **decay between teeth** | **$50–$100** |
| **Panoramic** | D0330 | Single flat image of **both jaws, all teeth, sinuses, TMJ** — for trauma, wisdom teeth, jaw | **$100–$250** |
| **CBCT (3D)** | D0364–D0368 / D0380–D0384 | 3D scan — complex fractures, surgical planning, hidden anatomy | **$100–$400+** |

- The **periapical (D0220)** is the workhorse of emergency dentistry: it images the root tip where most acute pain and abscesses live. Periapicals rarely have insurance frequency limits, so they're routinely reimbursed at emergency visits.
- A **panoramic** is used for trauma, suspected jaw involvement, or wisdom-tooth pain. (Note: D0330 is the 2D pano, *not* a CBCT — billing a CBCT as D0330 is a coding error.)

## Typical Costs — With vs Without Insurance

**The limited exam (D0140) itself:**
- Without insurance: **$50–$100** typical; national average cited around **$92** (range ~$59 in lower-cost cities to ~$110+ in higher-cost cities). Some sources cite ~$150 for emergency/limited exams.
- One Medicaid reimbursement reference: **~$55** for D0140.
- **With insurance:** diagnostic exams and X-rays are commonly covered at **80–100%** under the diagnostic/preventive category, often subject to deductible. Emergency X-rays are generally covered as diagnostic benefits.

**The whole emergency visit (exam + X-rays, before treatment):**
- A focused emergency visit (exam + a film or two) commonly lands around **$100–$200** out of pocket without insurance.
- Total emergency care, *including* treatment, ranges widely: **$75 to $3,000** depending on what's needed (filling $100–$250; simple extraction $75–$250; surgical extraction $200–$600; root canal ~$700–$900 front teeth, $1,000–$1,200 molars).

**Context — dentist vs ER:** A hospital ER visit averages around **$749**, roughly 3x a dental visit, and ERs usually have **no dentist on staff** — they can give pain relief and antibiotics but will refer you back to a dentist for the actual fix. Seeing a dentist directly is almost always cheaper and more definitive.

## Immediate Treatment vs Prescription + Come Back

After the exam, the dentist decides between:

- **Treat now** — if the problem is straightforward and time allows: place a filling, smooth a fracture, re-cement a crown, do a simple extraction, or start a root canal to relieve pressure.
- **Stabilize + prescribe + return** — if there is **active swelling/spreading infection** (may need **antibiotics** first), if a longer procedure is needed, or if scheduling requires it. You leave with a prescription (antibiotic and/or pain control) and a booked follow-up. Antibiotics are **not** always given — only when there's a true infection.

---

## What You Leave With — The Real Value of Being Seen

Even when the fix happens later, the emergency exam itself almost always delivers:

- **A diagnosis** — the cause of the pain identified (decay into the nerve, abscess, crack, infection, dry socket, etc.).
- **A plan** — exactly what needs to happen and roughly what it costs.
- **Often a prescription** — antibiotic and/or pain relief.
- **Frequently immediate relief** — draining an abscess, opening a tooth, or removing the source.

**The single biggest benefit is certainty.** A toothache with no diagnosis is frightening — the unknown ("is this serious? will I lose the tooth?") drives much of the anxiety. The limited exam converts an unknown crisis into a named problem with a clear next step. Just being seen and getting a diagnosis sharply reduces uncertainty and stress, even before treatment begins.
