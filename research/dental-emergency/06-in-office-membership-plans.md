# In-Office Dental Membership Plans & Set-Price Emergency Exams

*Research brief — accessed 2026-06-24*

## What an in-office membership plan is

An in-office (a.k.a. **in-house**) dental membership plan is a **non-insurance program a practice sells directly** to patients for a flat annual or monthly fee. It is a savings/loyalty program, not insurance: the dentist sets the price, keeps the revenue, and there is **no third-party payer, no claims, and no network beyond that one office**.

**Typical structure:**
- **Annual fee** paid to the office (sometimes billed monthly).
- **Preventive care bundled in at no extra charge** — usually 2 cleanings/year, 2 exams, routine X-rays, often an oral-cancer screening, fluoride, and frequently **one emergency/limited exam**.
- **A standing discount (~10–25%) on everything else** — fillings, crowns, root canals, extractions, whitening, even cosmetic work.
- **No deductibles, no annual maximums, no waiting periods, no co-pays, no pre-existing-condition exclusions, no claim forms.**

**Real price examples (2025–2026):**
- Maple Grove Dentistry: **$395/yr** first member, $345 each additional adult, $305 kids under 12.
- Wahlen Dental: **$549/yr** individual; perio plan **$759/yr or $69/mo**.
- KFF/NBC-cited typical adult plan: **~$29/mo (~$348/yr)** for 2 exams, 2 cleanings, X-rays, and an emergency exam — services valued near **$492**.

Most individual adult plans land in roughly the **$300–$600/yr** range.

## How they differ from insurance and from third-party discount plans

There are **three distinct products** people confuse:

| Feature | **In-office membership plan** | **Third-party discount/savings plan** (e.g. DentalPlans.com) | **Real PPO insurance** (e.g. Ameritas PrimeStar) |
|---|---|---|---|
| What it is | Subscription sold by ONE practice | Discount card honored at a network of dentists | Insurance that pays a % of treatment |
| Who you pay | The dentist directly | A plan company (~$150/yr avg, ~$12.50/mo) | An insurer (premiums) |
| Pays for treatment? | **No** — preventive bundled, rest discounted | **No** — discounts only | **Yes** — pays a share of basic & major work |
| Cleanings/exams | Usually included free | Discounted | Typically 100% covered |
| Major work (crown, root canal) | ~10–25% off list price | Discounted rate | Insurer pays 20–50% of allowed cost |
| Waiting periods | None | None | Varies (PrimeStar = none) |
| Annual maximum | None | None | Yes (e.g. $2,000–$3,500) |
| Deductible | None | None | Yes (e.g. $50) |
| Where it works | That office only | Network of participating dentists | Any in-network PPO dentist |

**Pros of membership / discount plans:** no waiting periods, no maximums, no deductibles, no claims, often cover cosmetic work that insurance excludes, predictable upfront cost, lower entry price than premiums. Good for **predictable, mostly-preventive needs**.

**Cons:** they **do not pay your bills** — you still pay the (discounted) full price of treatment. On big restorative work the discount is modest. In-office plans **lock you to one practice**; discount cards limit you to a network. Neither is real coverage or risk-sharing.

## Set-price "emergency exam" / new-patient specials

Many offices advertise a **flat-fee limited exam (often + X-ray)** to pull in new and uninsured patients in pain:

- New Tempo Family Dental: **$69** emergency exam with two X-rays.
- Aspen Dental: **$29** new-patient exam + X-rays (min. $80 value) for uninsured adults 21+.
- Union Square Dental / Beach Cities Dentistry: **$79** exam + X-rays.
- Coast Dental: **free** new-patient or emergency exam + X-ray coupons.
- Walk-in market rate without a special: an emergency/limited exam plus a targeted X-ray typically runs **$100–$200** (exam ~$150, single X-ray $25–$250).

**Why offices offer them:** cost is the #1 reason people skip dental care — nearly 1 in 3 Americans can't afford needed treatment. A cheap or free diagnostic visit removes the barrier, gets the patient in the chair, builds loyalty, and converts into a treatment plan (and revenue). Membership patients "act like insured patients" — they come in as often, pay the office directly, and cut out the insurer middleman.

## Where these help in an emergency — and where they fall short

**They help because:**
- **No waiting period** — discount/membership benefits and the cheap exam are usable **immediately**, the day you walk in with a toothache.
- A **set-price exam + X-ray** makes the *diagnosis* affordable and predictable.
- The membership discount trims the bill on whatever comes next.

**They fall short because:**
- The **big procedure still costs a lot.** A 10–25% discount on a $1,200–$1,800 root-canal-and-crown still leaves four figures out of pocket. The plan **pays nothing**.
- It's **not coverage** — no risk transfer, no annual maximum protecting you from a catastrophic bill.
- In-office plans only work **at that one office**; if you move, switch, or need a specialist, the value evaporates.

## The contrast that points to real coverage

For a genuine emergency involving **major work**, a plan that *pays a percentage* changes the math. **Ameritas PrimeStar Complete** is a useful balanced comparison: **no waiting periods**, **100% preventive day one**, **basic care 80% and major work 20% from day one** (major rising to 50% after year one), a **$2,000 first-year maximum (→$3,500)**, ~**$54/mo** premium, $50 deductible, zero enrollment fee.

**Balanced takeaway:** A set-price emergency exam plus a membership/discount plan is genuinely useful for **getting seen fast and cutting routine costs**, especially for the uninsured. But it is **not protection against a large restorative bill** — it discounts, it doesn't pay. When the emergency turns into major work, **day-1 PPO coverage that pays a share of the procedure** (like PrimeStar Complete) is the product that actually limits the out-of-pocket hit. The right move is often **both**: use the cheap exam to get diagnosed today, and carry real coverage for the work that follows.
