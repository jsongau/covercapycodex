# Dental Emergency Types & Triage — Research Notes

**Compiled:** 2026-06-24 | For CoverCapy educational/SEO content. Not medical advice. Readers should always contact a licensed dentist or, for life-threatening signs, call 911 / go to a hospital ER.

---

## The Core Triage Question: Emergency vs Urgent vs Non-Urgent

A **dental emergency** is any mouth problem causing **uncontrolled bleeding, severe pain that doesn't respond to medication, broken facial bones, or signs of spreading infection** (Cleveland Clinic). Not every dental problem is an emergency.

**First step in ANY dental emergency:** call your dentist — many have an after-hours emergency line. If you have no dentist or it's after hours, go to urgent care or the nearest ER. Note: ER staff can give antibiotics and pain relief but **cannot do restorative work** (fillings, crowns, replanting); you'll still need a dentist (Cleveland Clinic).

### TRUE EMERGENCY — see dentist same-day / go to ER
- Knocked-out (avulsed) permanent tooth
- Extruded (partially dislodged) tooth
- Badly cracked tooth with severe pain or exposed nerve
- Dental abscess with facial/jaw swelling
- Uncontrolled bleeding
- Severe soft-tissue laceration (deep cut, busted lip)
- Suspected jaw / facial-bone fracture → **ER**

### URGENT — see dentist within ~24–72 hrs
- Severe/throbbing toothache (possible pulp infection)
- Lost filling or crown (exposed, sensitive tooth)
- Dry socket after extraction
- Pericoronitis (inflamed gum over partially erupted wisdom tooth)

### NON-URGENT — routine appointment is fine
- Dull or mild toothache
- Small chip or crack with no pain
- Broken braces/wire (unless bleeding) — cover sharp end with orthodontic wax
- Object stuck between teeth
- Minor soft-tissue cut or sore

## Emergency Types — Specifics & Time Windows

### Knocked-Out (Avulsed) Permanent Tooth — TIME-CRITICAL
The single most time-sensitive dental emergency. **Re-implant within 30 minutes for best prognosis**; Cleveland Clinic notes the dentist has the best chance of saving it **when returned to the socket within 1 hour**. Extraoral dry time over ~60 min sharply lowers survival because periodontal ligament (PDL) cells die.
- **Do this now:** Hold by the **crown only — never the root**. Rinse gently with water; **do not scrub or remove attached tissue**. If possible, reinsert into the socket facing the right way (never force). If not, store in a **physiologic medium**: cold milk is the most recommended easy option (good pH/osmolarity), or Hank's Balanced Salt Solution (HBSS, e.g., Save-a-Tooth), saliva, or saline. **Avoid plain water** (low osmolarity damages PDL cells). See a dentist/endodontist immediately.
- Baby (primary) teeth are **not** re-implanted.

### Extruded (Partially Dislodged) Tooth
Tooth pushed partly out of socket. **See a dentist right away.** Cold compress to the cheek and OTC pain reliever (acetaminophen/ibuprofen/naproxen) until seen.

### Cracked / Fractured / Chipped Tooth
Symptoms range from erratic pain on chewing to sharp sensitivity to hot/cold; a fragment large enough to expose the nerve causes significant pain. Cracks may also be **asymptomatic**.
- **Emergency** if badly cracked, painful, bleeding, or nerve exposed — save any pieces, rinse with warm water, gauze pressure ~10 min for bleeding, cold compress, see dentist ASAP.
- **Non-urgent** if a small painless chip — cover sharp edges with orthodontic wax, book a normal visit.
- Why it matters: an untreated crack lets bacteria reach the pulp → abscess that can spread to jawbone, face and neck (Cleveland Clinic / AAE).

### Severe / Throbbing Toothache
Often signals pulp inflammation or infection. **Do this now:** rinse with warm water, floss to dislodge trapped food, cold compress for swelling, OTC pain relievers. **Never place aspirin directly on the gum or tooth — it chemically burns the tissue** (Cleveland Clinic). Pain not relieved by medication is an emergency.

### Dental Abscess — Can Become Life-Threatening
A pus-filled infection at the tooth root or between tooth and gum. **See a dentist as soon as possible.** Untreated, it can swell the face/jaw and spread elsewhere in the body. Temporary relief: rinse with warm salt water (½ tsp salt in 8 oz water) several times daily. **Escalate to ER for the red flags below.**

### Uncontrolled Bleeding & Soft-Tissue Lacerations
Cuts to tongue, lips, cheeks, gums. **Do this now:** rinse with mild salt water; apply firm pressure with moist gauze (or a moistened tea bag — tannic acid helps clotting) for **15–20 minutes**; cold compress 5–10 min for pain/swelling. **If bleeding doesn't stop after pressure, go to the dentist right away or to a hospital ER**, continuing pressure en route.

### Lost Filling or Crown
Urgent but rarely an ER matter. Exposed tooth is sensitive/sharp. Temporary measures: sugarless gum or OTC dental cement in a cavity; for a lost crown, coat the inside with OTC dental cement/toothpaste/denture adhesive and reseat it, and bring it to the dentist. **Never use super glue** (Cleveland Clinic). See a dentist soon.

### Broken Orthodontic Wire / Braces
Usually **non-urgent unless bleeding**. A poking archwire can cut the cheek/tongue. Gently bend the end flat with a pencil eraser, cover with orthodontic wax, and call the orthodontist to replace it.

### Pericoronitis
Inflammation of gum tissue (operculum) over a **partially erupted/impacted wisdom tooth**, most common on lower third molars. Symptoms: localized pain, swelling, bad taste, sometimes trismus. Treated with cleaning/irrigation, antibiotics, or surgery. Urgent if swelling spreads or fever develops — then treat as abscess red flags.

### Dry Socket (Alveolar Osteitis)
Painful complication when the blood clot fails to form or dislodges after extraction. **Onset typically days 3–5 (within the first 3 days too)**; if no symptoms by **day 5, the risk has largely passed** (Cleveland Clinic / Mayo / Healthline). Signs: intense throbbing radiating pain, empty-looking socket with **visible bone**, bad taste, halitosis. Contact the extracting dentist for socket dressing and pain control.

### Jaw Injury / Suspected Fracture — GO TO THE ER
A broken jaw is a **medical emergency.** Symptoms: pain, swelling, bruising of jaw/cheek, inability to open or close the mouth normally, misaligned bite, numbness, and bleeding from the mouth/nose. **Bleeding into the mouth can block the airway.** Go directly to the ER for any suspected jaw or facial-bone fracture (Cleveland Clinic / Healthline).

## RED FLAGS — Go to the ER / Call 911 (do not wait for a dentist)

- **Difficulty breathing or swallowing, or stridor** — possible airway compromise.
- **Rapidly spreading facial/neck swelling**, especially **bilateral neck swelling or swelling of the floor of the mouth with tongue displacement** — signs of **Ludwig's angina**, a life-threatening rapidly progressing infection that can block the airway or cause sepsis (Cleveland Clinic / Healthline / WebMD).
- **Fever combined with facial swelling** from a dental infection.
- **Swelling spreading toward the eye** or closing the eye.
- **Signs of sepsis** — high fever, chills, fast heart rate, confusion, feeling very unwell (severe spreading odontogenic infection).
- **Uncontrolled bleeding** that won't stop with sustained pressure.
- **Trauma with possible jaw or facial-bone fracture.**

Severe odontogenic infections (cellulitis plus sepsis signs, difficulty swallowing, impending airway obstruction, or Ludwig's angina) require **urgent hospital admission** for airway protection, surgical drainage, and IV antibiotics (NHS clinical guidance).
