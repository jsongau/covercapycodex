# Common Emergency Treatment Sequences: What Actually Happens and What Follows

*Educational reference. Updated 2026-06-24. Costs are rough US cash ranges and vary widely by region, tooth, and materials. Not medical advice.*

A dental emergency almost never ends in a single visit. The first appointment is about **stabilizing** the problem (stopping pain, draining infection, protecting the tooth or socket). The **definitive restoration** that makes the tooth whole and functional again usually comes weeks to months later. This "stabilize now, restore later" pattern is why a single toothache often turns into a multi-step plan. Critically, the *restore-later* steps, root canals, crowns, post-and-core buildups, extractions, bone grafts, and implants, are exactly the "major" services that day-1 major dental coverage is built to offset.

---

## 1. Severe toothache from deep decay / irreversible pulpitis -> Root canal + crown

When decay or trauma inflames the pulp past the point of healing (irreversible pulpitis), root canal therapy removes the infected pulp, then the tooth is sealed (Cleveland Clinic). Sequence:

- **Visit 1 (stabilize):** Root canal, pulp removed, canals cleaned/sealed; pain relief. Often a **post-and-core buildup** is needed to rebuild lost tooth structure, plus a **temporary crown**.
- **Visit 2 (restore, weeks later):** A **permanent crown** caps the now-brittle tooth. A definitive crown should follow root canal as soon as practical.

**Rough costs:** Root canal $700–$1,600 (molar ~$1,175, premolar ~$1,017, anterior ~$900, excluding final restoration); post-and-core buildup $200–$500; crown $1,000–$2,000. A root-canal-plus-crown bundle commonly runs **$1,700–$3,200** cash. This is a textbook "major" service pair.

---

## 2. Non-restorable / abscessed tooth -> Extraction (+ bone graft) -> Implant or bridge later

If a tooth is shattered or too decayed to save, it is extracted. To preserve the socket for a future implant, surgeons often place a **socket preservation / bone graft** at the same visit, the gum is retracted, the tooth removed, graft material packed into the socket, covered with a barrier membrane, and sutured. This significantly reduces ridge collapse versus simple extraction.

- **Visit 1 (stabilize):** Extraction, often with same-day bone graft / socket preservation.
- **Heal (3–4+ months):** Implant placement is recommended at least ~4 months after grafting.
- **Visit(s) later (restore):** Implant post -> abutment -> implant crown, or a bridge.

**Rough costs:** Simple extraction $225, surgical extraction ~$325; bone graft $400–$3,000 (graft type/defect dependent); single implant $3,000–$7,000 all-in (post $1,000–$2,000, abutment $200–$500, crown $1,000–$2,000), averaging ~$4,259/implant. Extraction + bone graft + implant is the highest-cost "major" pathway and where major coverage matters most.

---

## 3. Knocked-out (avulsed) tooth -> Re-implant + splint (+ later root canal)

A permanent avulsed tooth is a true emergency; fast re-implantation matters. Per AAE / IADT guidance:

- **Immediate:** Re-implant the tooth into its socket, then place a **flexible splint** (typically ~2 weeks; 4 weeks if dry time exceeded ~60 min). Systemic antibiotics are usually given.
- **~2 weeks (restore the nerve):** **Root canal** is typically started within 2 weeks of replantation, before splint removal, because the pulp usually dies. (Exception: a quickly re-implanted immature tooth with an open apex may revascularize and avoid root canal.)

**Rough costs:** Emergency exam plus splinting, then root canal $700–$1,600, and frequently a crown later. Multi-visit by definition.

---

## 4. Cracked tooth -> Crown, or (if vertical root fracture) Extraction

Treatment depends entirely on **how deep the crack goes**:

- **Cusp fracture / crack above the root:** Often a **crown**; a deeper crack into the pulp needs **root canal + crown**.
- **Vertical root fracture (VRF):** Poor prognosis, the crack is a permanent bacterial highway that cannot be sealed, so **extraction** is usually the only option in single-rooted teeth (then pathway #2). Depth/direction often require a microscope and 3D imaging to diagnose.

**Rough costs:** Crown $1,000–$2,000 (+root canal $700–$1,600 if pulp involved); VRF -> extraction + future implant ($3,000–$7,000).

---

## 5. Lost filling or lost crown -> Re-cement, or replace

A loose-but-intact crown can often be **re-cemented**: roughly **$75–$350** (lower if undamaged, no new X-rays/decay). If the crown is cracked, lost, or decay is found underneath, it must be **replaced**: **$800–$3,000** (porcelain $800–$2,500; zirconia $1,000–$3,000). Many plans cover re-cementation as a basic restorative service. Seen quickly as an urgency.

---

## 6. Pericoronitis / impacted wisdom tooth -> Irrigation/cleaning, sometimes extraction

Pericoronitis is inflammation of gum tissue over a partially erupted (usually wisdom) tooth.

- **Stabilize:** Local **debridement and irrigation** under the gum flap; antibiotics reserved for severe/systemic spread.
- **Restore/resolve:** If episodes recur or the tooth won't erupt properly, **extraction** (often by an oral surgeon) removes the uncleanable space. AAOMS notes retaining wisdom teeth raises pericoronitis risk.

**Rough costs:** Cleaning/irrigation visit modest; surgical/impacted extraction higher and often specialist-referred.

---

## Stabilize Now, Restore Later

In every scenario above, the emergency visit buys **control**, pain relief, infection drainage, a re-implanted or splinted tooth, a protected socket, but not a finished result. The definitive steps that restore chewing function (the permanent crown, the implant, the bridge) arrive on a healing timeline of weeks to months. That second half is almost always the "major" category: **root canal + crown** and **extraction + bone graft + implant** are the marquee major-services examples, precisely the high-cost work that day-1 major coverage is designed to offset.
