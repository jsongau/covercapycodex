# 09 — Simulated Patient Testing: PPO Plans Hub
## CoverCapy PPO Hub Rebuild | Research Program
**Spec:** 09 of 20
**Status:** Draft — June 26, 2026
**Method:** Moderated usability role-play across 6 distinct patient/shopper personas; scenario-first hub concept evaluated

---

## Methodology note

These sessions are simulated. Each persona was constructed from realistic demographic, psychographic, and dental-need parameters consistent with the hub's stated audience. No quantitative metrics (click rates, time-on-page, conversion rates) are fabricated. Qualitative patterns, emotional responses, questions, and friction points are the output. Session format: persona summary, situation, first reaction, where they get confused or distrustful, unanswered questions, what makes them feel in control, what would trigger a click to a plan, and what would make them share with family or friends.

## Persona 1 — Panicked Cracked Tooth Adult ("Marcus")

**Profile:** 38, warehouse supervisor, San Antonio TX. Bit into something hard at lunch and now half a molar is gone. No dental insurance, never had it as an adult. Landed on the hub after Googling "dental insurance that covers a cracked tooth right now." It is a Tuesday at 2 PM. He is at work on his phone.

**Goal:** Find out if any plan will cover a crown soon, and how fast he can get coverage. He does not care about monthly premiums being optimized; he cares about speed and whether the plan actually pays.

**First reaction to the scenario-first hub:**
Marcus responds well to seeing a scenario labeled something like "Dental emergency or urgent crown" near the top. He does not want to read eight plan cards in a grid. Seeing a scenario that matches his situation makes him feel the site understands him rather than filing him into a demographic category.

His second reaction: he immediately tries to find out how long he has to wait before a crown is covered. He scrolls for a waiting period disclosure. If the page leads with each plan's best features without prominently disclosing major-work waiting periods upfront, he reads the page as hiding something.

**Where he gets confused or distrustful:**
- The word "major" in "major services" is insurance jargon. He thinks of his broken tooth as urgent; calling it a "major service" sounds bureaucratic. He does not automatically know a crown is classified as "major."
- He sees Ameritas PrimeStar's "no waiting periods" claim and becomes suspicious. He assumes there is a catch buried somewhere. If the page does not immediately explain that year-one major coinsurance is 20% (not 50%), he feels tricked when he reads the fine print later. The absence of that disclosure up front reads as dishonest to him.
- He sees Mutual of Omaha's "no waiting period on major" and then notices major coinsurance is 20% in year one. He is not sure if that means the plan pays 20 cents of every dollar or he pays 20 cents. Insurance coinsurance language is opaque.
- If CoverCapy describes itself only as an "educational marketplace" without clarifying that it is not the insurer, he is confused about who he is dealing with and whether a plan he clicks buys through will actually exist.

**Unanswered questions:**
1. If I sign up today, when exactly does my crown become eligible?
2. If the plan only pays 20% on major in year one, how much would I actually owe on a $1,200 crown? (He needs a dollar estimate, not a percentage.)
3. Is a cracked tooth that still has the root intact treated as "major" or something different?
4. Can I use my own dentist, or do I have to switch?
5. Who exactly is CoverCapy? Are you the insurer, a broker, or something else?

**What makes him feel in control:**
- A plain-language translation next to every waiting period: "You can book a crown appointment as soon as [date]."
- A worked dollar example: "For a $1,200 crown in year one, this plan would pay approximately $240 (20%), leaving you about $960 before your annual maximum."
- A clear statement of what CoverCapy's role is (marketplace, not insurer).
- Carrier name, AM Best rating, and a link to buy directly if he distrusts marketplaces.

**What would make him click to a plan:**
Seeing Ameritas PrimeStar or Mutual of Omaha clearly labeled as "available day one for major work, though year-one reimbursement is 20%" plus a real-money crown example. He would click if he saw the dollar math clearly. He would not click on any plan that hides the year-one major rate.

**What would make him share:**
He would text his brother-in-law immediately if the hub had a one-liner he could copy: "CoverCapy has plans that cover crowns with no waiting period, but year one is 20% coinsurance, so you'd owe about $960 on a $1,200 crown. Still way better than nothing." The page needs to give him that sentence in plain language.

## Persona 2 — Budget Mom Shopping Kids' Braces ("Priya")

**Profile:** 36, part-time school aide, Mesa AZ. Two kids, ages 9 and 13. The orthodontist told her the 9-year-old needs braces within the next 12 to 18 months and the 13-year-old is overdue. Employer dental covers cleanings only, no ortho. Her husband is self-employed. She is on a laptop at home after the kids are in bed. She has bookmarked several comparison pages.

**Goal:** Find a plan that covers orthodontics for both kids at the lowest total cost (monthly premium plus out-of-pocket). She already knows waiting periods exist; she is planning ahead.

**First reaction to the scenario-first hub:**
She looks directly for a scenario called something like "Braces for my kids" or "Orthodontics." If the hub surfaces this scenario near the top and points directly to Guardian Premier 2.0 (child ortho, 60% in-network, $1,500 lifetime max per child), she feels seen. She immediately starts doing the math in her head.

**Where she gets confused or distrustful:**
- She reads that Guardian's child ortho is "$1,500 lifetime maximum" and does not know if that is per policy or per child. This is a critical ambiguity. If two children each need braces, she needs to know whether the $1,500 is shared across both kids or whether each child gets their own $1,500 cap.
- She also reads that child ortho has a 12-month waiting period. She wants to know the exact start date. She thinks of a "waiting period" as meaning she can enroll today, wait 12 months, and then the benefit kicks in. She does not know if a partially applied brace that started before the waiting period counts.
- She notices that adult ortho is not covered on Guardian. She wonders if the 13-year-old, who may turn 19 during treatment, would lose the benefit mid-braces.
- She reads that the $750 per benefit year for child ortho means her $1,500 lifetime benefit comes out over at least two calendar years, in installments. This surprises and mildly frustrates her. She planned the money as a lump sum.
- She does not see a "family plan" option and wonders if she needs separate policies for each family member.

**Unanswered questions:**
1. Is the $1,500 lifetime ortho maximum per child or shared across both kids on one policy?
2. If my 13-year-old starts braces at age 17, does the benefit continue if treatment finishes at age 20?
3. Can I buy one Guardian policy that covers two children, or do I need separate policies?
4. What if my current dentist is not in the DentalGuard Preferred network?
5. Delta Dental covers adult ortho. Would it make more sense for the older child?

**What makes her feel in control:**
- A worked family scenario: "Two children, ages 9 and 13, both need braces. With Guardian: each child's ortho benefit is $1,500 lifetime (two policies or two members on one family policy, confirm with carrier). Expected plan contribution per child: roughly $900 over two years."
- A side-by-side: Guardian (child ortho, 60%) vs Delta Dental PPO Premium (adult ortho, 50%) with age-at-appliance-placement clearly explained.
- An explicit age cutoff note: "The dependent must be under 19 when the active appliance is first placed."

**What would make her click to a plan:**
She would click on Guardian if she understood the per-child cap clearly and the dollar math added up. She would also seriously consider Delta Dental for the older child. She might end up clicking on two different plans. A comparison "stack" of the two plans for her exact scenario would be the trigger.

**What would make her share:**
She is the most likely persona to share the hub with her spouse and with other parents from the school. The share trigger is a shareable scenario summary or a simple "copy this plan recommendation" text she can paste into a family group chat. She would share if the hub gave her a digestible "here is the two-kid braces strategy" summary she could forward.

## Persona 3 — Newly Laid-Off COBRA Gap Worker ("Derek")

**Profile:** 42, former marketing manager, Austin TX. Was laid off 3 weeks ago. His employer dental plan (Delta Dental Group) ran through the end of last month. He has a crown scheduled in 6 weeks that he has been putting off; his dentist already took impressions. He looked at COBRA and it was $87/month just for dental. He is on the hub because a friend mentioned individual plans might be faster and cheaper.

**Goal:** Get dental coverage back for himself (and his wife) before the crown appointment in 6 weeks. He knows his situation is time-sensitive. He does not want to lose the crown scheduling.

**First reaction to the scenario-first hub:**
Derek searches for a scenario labeled "Lost employer dental" or "Gap in coverage." If the hub has this scenario and clearly explains which plans can waive waiting periods with proof of prior coverage (within a 63-day gap), he feels the hub was built for him. His prior Delta Dental Group plan was major-work eligible; he wants to know if he can port that creditable coverage.

**Where he gets confused or distrustful:**
- He sees "waiting periods can be waived with proof of prior coverage" on Delta Dental PPO Premium and Humana. He does not know what "prior comparable coverage" means in practice. He does not know whether a group Delta plan counts. He is afraid of submitting the wrong paperwork.
- He notices the prior-coverage waiver on Delta Dental PPO Premium has a "no more than 63-day gap" rule. He counts back: it has been about 30 days since his group plan ended. He is relieved but now urgently wants to know how to submit prior coverage proof. The hub does not tell him the process.
- He reads Humana Extend 5000 and sees the implant 6-month wait "cannot be waived." He needs a crown, not an implant, so this is not directly relevant, but the "cannot be waived" language plants seeds of distrust: are there other unwaivers he is missing?
- He cannot figure out whether any plan on the hub would cover a crown scheduled in 6 weeks even with a waiver. Six weeks is well inside the 12-month major wait window even after a waiver speeds things up. He is unsure if "waived" means immediate major coverage or something else.
- He wonders if CoverCapy is going to sell his email to a call center.

**Unanswered questions:**
1. What exactly qualifies as "prior comparable coverage"? Does my former employer's group Delta Dental plan qualify?
2. What documentation do I need to submit, and to whom, to get the waiting period waived?
3. If my gap is under 63 days and I get the waiver, does major work (my crown) become eligible immediately?
4. Is 6 weeks enough lead time to enroll, submit prior coverage proof, get the waiver approved, and have the crown covered?
5. Does CoverCapy share my information with insurance companies or telemarketers?

**What makes him feel in control:**
- A concrete "COBRA gap" checklist: days-since-coverage, waiver document checklist, how to contact the carrier.
- An honest answer: "With a waiver, major work (like a crown) is typically eligible immediately, but the plan's annual maximum applies and we recommend confirming with the carrier that your prior group plan counts as comparable coverage before you schedule."
- A privacy statement that is short and plain: "We do not sell your information. Clicking a plan takes you to the insurer's site or a licensed partner."

**What would make him click to a plan:**
He would click on Delta Dental PPO Premium or Humana within minutes if the hub confirmed that his prior Delta group plan qualifies as comparable coverage (or explained how to confirm this). The decision is fast once the waiver path is de-risked. He is not price-sensitive at this moment.

**What would make him share:**
He would share the specific "COBRA gap waiver" section or scenario page with his laid-off colleagues from the same company. He sent a Slack message to three former coworkers within days of being laid off. If the hub had a shareable "lost your dental? here is the 63-day window" explainer, he would forward it.

## Persona 4 — Pre-Retiree About to Lose Employer Dental ("Sandra")

**Profile:** 63, healthcare administrator, Charlotte NC. Retires in 4 months. Has had employer dental through her employer for 20 years. She knows Medicare does not cover dental. Her teeth are in decent shape, but she expects to need implants within a few years (her dentist mentioned bone density). She is methodical, reads everything, and distrusts "salesy" websites.

**Goal:** Find a plan she can keep long-term through retirement that covers implants, has a high annual maximum, and does not expire at 65. She wants to compare Mutual of Omaha (which her friend mentioned) against at least one other option.

**First reaction to the scenario-first hub:**
Sandra reads the page carefully from top to bottom. She appreciates a scenario labeled "Turning 65 / retiring soon." She is immediately suspicious of the word "luxury" in CoverCapy's brand positioning (if it appears). She associates luxury with overpriced. She trusts the page more if it uses precise, numerical language and cites sources.

**Where she gets confused or distrustful:**
- She reads Mutual of Omaha's "no waiting period on major" claim and is skeptical because she has seen many insurance promises that evaporate in the fine print. She looks for a "but" clause. When she reads that major coinsurance is 20% in year one and only rises to 50% in year two, she thinks: "So it is not really no waiting period, it just starts badly." She is not wrong. She feels the headline is misleading.
- She reads the "$5,000 annual maximum" and "$3,000 lifetime implant maximum" on Mutual of Omaha and is confused about how the two interact. Does the implant use up her annual maximum? (Yes, it does.) This is unclear on a typical hub.
- She looks at the ~$90/month premium for MOO and wants to know if the price is "community-rated," meaning it will not spike when she turns 65. The hub probably does not answer this in those words.
- She sees Ameritas PrimeStar at ~$56/month, "no waiting periods," and is attracted to the price. But she notices the year-one implant coverage is only $1,000 (deducted from the annual maximum). She calculates that an implant costing $4,000 would leave her holding $3,000 out of pocket in year one. She needs to see this math clearly.
- She notices the hub does not mention Medicare Advantage dental riders as a comparison point. She wonders if she is missing a better option. This may cause her to leave the page.

**Unanswered questions:**
1. Will my premium increase significantly when I turn 65? Which plans are community-rated (not age-banded)?
2. If Mutual of Omaha says "no waiting period," why is the year-one major rate only 20%? Is that different from a waiting period in practice?
3. On the $5,000 annual maximum: does an implant procedure costing $3,500 eat my entire annual maximum?
4. Does any plan on this hub coordinate with Medicare supplemental benefits?
5. Are there age limits? Can I keep this plan at 70, 75?

**What makes her feel in control:**
- Explicit "community-rated" versus "age-banded" labels on each plan.
- A 65-plus callout box: "Plans available through retirement. No Medicare required. No age out."
- A worked implant dollar example across years one and two for Mutual of Omaha and Ameritas.
- Source citations or at least "verify this with the carrier" prompts next to every number.
- No countdown timers, no urgency pressure, no chatbots popping up.

**What would make her click to a plan:**
She would click to Mutual of Omaha's plan page first because it has the highest maximum and no waiting period on paper. She would not convert on the first visit. She returns. The second visit click happens if the hub remembered her scenario context (even via a simple bookmark CTA like "save your plan comparison") or if she found a phone number to speak with someone rather than buy online.

**What would make her share:**
She would share the hub with her husband and two work friends also approaching retirement. The share trigger is a printable or emailable "retirement dental comparison" summary. She would forward a PDF summary if it existed. A "share with your spouse" CTA on the hub would work for her.

## Persona 5 — Young Couple Just Off Parents' Insurance ("Caleb and Mae")

**Profile:** Both 26, recently married, Denver CO. Caleb just aged off his parents' dental plan at 26. Mae works a gig job with no benefits. Neither has had dental coverage as independent adults. They are price-conscious but not broke. They are on the hub together on a Saturday morning, splitting a screen.

**Goal:** Find a plan (or two separate plans) that covers their cleanings plus covers cavities and probably will not bankrupt them if something goes wrong. They want to start "doing the adult thing."

**First reaction to the scenario-first hub:**
They respond well to a scenario called "Getting dental for the first time" or "Young adult, first plan." They are scanning, not reading. They look at monthly price first. They see UHC Primary Dental at ~$30/month and immediately consider that as an anchor. They compare it against Aetna Dental Direct (CVS perks, basic coverage, balanced plan).

**Where they get confused or distrustful:**
- Caleb reads that UHC Primary Dental does not cover "major services" and does not know what major services are. He assumes "major" means like surgery, not a root canal or crown. He does not realize that if he chips a tooth badly enough to need a crown, he gets no help from UHC Primary.
- Mae sees the Aetna Dental Direct listing and is interested in the CVS ExtraCare Plus $10/month reward. She does not know if this is $10 off her dental premium or $10 in CVS store credit. (It is a $10/month CVS Health rewards credit.) This ambiguity reduces her confidence in the benefit.
- They both notice that most plans have a 12-month or 6-month waiting period on major work. They try to figure out if they need to enroll now to be ready for anything serious in a year. They understand the concept but feel rushed by it.
- They look at the plans side by side and cannot figure out which is "the best deal." Without a "recommended for you" signal based on their situation (young, healthy, need cleanings and basic), they feel paralyzed.
- They trust CoverCapy less when they cannot see who is behind it. They want a real company name, a real address, or something that proves this is not a lead-gen scam.

**Unanswered questions:**
1. Does UHC Primary Dental actually cover cavities? (Yes: basic = fillings. But this is not obvious.)
2. What exactly is the CVS $10 monthly reward from Aetna? Do we get store credit? Is it per person?
3. If we both enroll separately, is it actually cheaper than a family plan?
4. If we both stay healthy and never use it, are we wasting money?
5. Who is CoverCapy? Is this website safe to buy from?

**What makes them feel in control:**
- A plain-language definition of "preventive," "basic," and "major" early on the page, before any plan comparisons.
- An explicit "young and healthy" recommended path: "If you mainly want cleanings and cavity coverage, start here."
- For the CVS perk: a one-sentence explainer: "Aetna Dental Direct includes a $10-per-month credit to spend on CVS Health brand oral-care products at CVS stores or CVS.com."
- An "About CoverCapy" trust module: not the company's origin story, just who built the site, that it is not an insurer, how it makes money (if applicable), and a privacy note.
- A "recommended for first-timers" badge on one or two plans.

**What would make them click to a plan:**
They would click on Aetna Dental Direct if the CVS perk was explained clearly and if the page surfaced a simple "this plan is designed for people like you" signal. Alternatively they click on UHC Primary Dental because of price, then read enough on the plan page to see major is not covered, and come back to reconsider. The conversion happens on the second or third engagement, not the first.

**What would make them share:**
They would post the hub to a group chat of friends the same age, saying something like "we used CoverCapy to find our first dentist plan, way less confusing than going direct." The share trigger is: a genuinely clear explanation of plan tiers that makes them feel smart for understanding it, and a brief moment where they realize they understood something that felt complicated before. A "share this plan" or "send this to your partner" micro-CTA would help.

## Persona 6 — Adult Wanting Invisalign ("Natalie")

**Profile:** 34, software engineer, Seattle WA. Has wanted to straighten her teeth for years. Just got a consultation; Invisalign treatment is quoted at $5,800. She does not have dental insurance and has been told "most individual plans do not cover adult braces." She is on the hub after Googling "individual dental insurance that covers adult Invisalign."

**Goal:** Find out if any individual dental plan covers adult orthodontics, how much it would actually contribute to a $5,800 case, and whether it is worth buying the plan just for ortho.

**First reaction to the scenario-first hub:**
Natalie lands on the hub skeptical that it will have what she needs. She has already read three comparison sites that listed "orthodontics: not covered" for most plans. When she sees a scenario labeled "Adult braces or Invisalign" and the hub surfaces Delta Dental PPO Premium as the only individual plan on the shelf covering adult ortho, she is surprised and interested. Her skepticism converts to cautious attention.

**Where she gets confused or distrustful:**
- She reads Delta Dental PPO Premium covers adult orthodontics at 50% up to a $1,500 lifetime maximum with a 12-month waiting period and a separate $50 orthodontic deductible. She does the math: $1,500 maximum on a $5,800 case means Delta pays at most $1,500 and she still owes $4,300 out of pocket. Her first reaction is "that is not much." She does not walk away, but she recalibrates.
- She reads the note that adult ortho on Delta Dental PPO Premium is available on the broker-sold Premium tier, not on exchange certificates. She does not know what "broker-sold" or "exchange certificate" means and feels she might accidentally buy the wrong version.
- She notices Delta Dental is only sold in 16 states plus DC. She is in Seattle, WA. She wants immediate confirmation that Washington is one of those 16 states. If the page does not surface state availability, she bounces to confirm elsewhere.
- She reads the missing-tooth/initial-placement exclusion and is confused about whether it applies to Invisalign. (It does not: Invisalign is orthodontics, not prosthodontics.) The legalese makes her nervous.
- She calculates that at $75/month premium, she would pay $900/year. After the 12-month wait, she enrolls, waits a year, then gets $1,500 back over the life of treatment minus the $50 ortho deductible. Net benefit: roughly $550 after 24 months of premiums. She wonders aloud: "Is it even worth it?"

**Unanswered questions:**
1. Is Washington state one of the 16 states where Delta Dental PPO Premium individual plans are sold?
2. If I buy the broker-sold Premium tier, am I guaranteed the adult ortho benefit, or do I need to confirm which certificate applies?
3. Does the $1,500 lifetime maximum cover both the Invisalign trays AND any retainers afterward?
4. Does Invisalign specifically qualify, or just traditional braces?
5. Is buying this plan worth it financially for just the ortho benefit, or should I treat it as a plan that does other things too?

**What makes her feel in control:**
- A clear state-availability checker or at minimum a list of the 16 states where individual Delta Dental PPO Premium is sold.
- A plain-English note: "The adult ortho benefit is specific to the broker-sold Individual Premium tier, not plans sold on the ACA marketplace. Make sure you are buying 'Individual Premium.'"
- An explicit "Does Invisalign qualify?" answer: yes, clear aligners are covered on the same terms as traditional braces on the Delta PPO Premium tier.
- A worked financial calculation: "$5,800 Invisalign case. Delta pays up to $1,500 lifetime (minus $50 ortho deductible). Your estimated out-of-pocket for the ortho benefit alone: roughly $4,350. The plan also covers cleanings, fillings, and other dental needs during the treatment period."
- Confirmation that no other individual plan on the CoverCapy shelf covers adult ortho, so this is the only option in this category.

**What would make her click to a plan:**
She would click through to Delta Dental PPO Premium's plan page if the hub confirmed: (1) Washington state availability, (2) Invisalign specifically is covered, and (3) the worked dollar example showed the math honestly. The financial case is thin, but if the hub framed it as "Delta also covers your cleanings and fillings at 80% and 50% during treatment, so you are getting full dental coverage plus a $1,500 ortho contribution," she sees the plan as worthwhile overall and clicks.

**What would make her share:**
She would share the hub with her partner and her best friend (also late 30s, also considering Invisalign) because she found something no other comparison site told her: one individual plan covers adult ortho. The share is driven by the exclusivity of the insight. A "did you know one individual plan covers adult Invisalign?" hook in the page headline or in a shareable snippet card would multiply this.

## Synthesized Findings

### Top Comprehension Blockers

**1. Insurance jargon without plain-language anchors**
"Major services," "basic services," "coinsurance," "creditable coverage," "in-network/out-of-network": none of these are self-explanatory to the lay visitor. Every persona except Sandra (who is a healthcare administrator) stumbled on at least one term. The hub must define these terms at first use, inline, without sending users to a glossary page.

**2. Year-one graduated major benefit misread as a waiting period**
Ameritas PrimeStar and Mutual of Omaha both advertise "no waiting period" while paying only 20% on major work in year one. Multiple personas (Marcus, Sandra) read this as either deceptive or confusing. The hub must clearly distinguish: "no waiting period" (eligible from day one) from "graduated benefit" (percentage increases over time). These are not the same and should not appear under the same headline.

**3. Per-person vs. per-policy ambiguity on lifetime maximums**
Priya (budget mom) is stymied by whether Guardian's $1,500 lifetime ortho cap is per child or per family per policy. Sandra is confused about whether implant costs eat into the annual maximum. The hub must explicitly label every cap as per-person or per-policy, and must note whether sub-maximums (implant caps, ortho caps) count against the annual maximum.

**4. State availability is invisible**
Natalie needs to know if Delta Dental PPO Premium is available in Washington before she invests any more time. Derek needs to know if his prior group Delta plan qualifies for waiver. Neither answer is on the hub. State-specific restrictions that are real (Delta sold in 16 states plus DC; Ameritas not in MA) must be surfaced before the user invests decision energy in a plan they cannot buy.

**5. What CoverCapy is**
Four of six personas expressed confusion or distrust about CoverCapy's role. Is it an insurer? A broker? A lead-gen site? The hub must answer this in the first screen: "CoverCapy is an independent educational marketplace. We are not an insurer. Clicking a plan takes you to the carrier or a licensed insurance partner."

---

### Top Trust Builders

**1. Worked dollar examples, not just percentages**
Every persona needed a real-money scenario, not a coinsurance percentage. "50% after a $50 deductible on a $1,200 crown = you pay roughly $650 in year one" is more trusted than "50% coinsurance on major services." Dollar examples make abstract math concrete and demonstrate the hub is honest about what the plan actually costs the patient.

**2. Honest framing of plan limits before benefits**
Personas trust the hub more when it leads with the honest constraint (year-one major at 20%, ortho only up to $1,500 lifetime) before promoting the plan. When limitation disclosures are buried or absent, savvy users feel the site is hiding something, which collapses trust. Limitation-first framing paradoxically increases conversion because it signals the hub is on the user's side.

**3. Carrier credentials cited specifically**
AM Best ratings, carrier founding years, underwriter names, and specific policy form numbers (even if only linked to a disclosure document) signal that CoverCapy has read the actual policy. Sandra and Derek particularly responded to source-attribution cues. Precision is a trust signal.

**4. Privacy and data handling transparency**
Derek and Caleb both expressed concern about email spam and data selling. A concise, plain-language data statement prominently placed: "We do not sell your information" with a link to a full privacy policy performs trust work that no plan card can.

**5. State availability confirmation before plan detail**
For plans with geographic restrictions (Delta Dental, Ameritas), surfacing a state check or a note before the full plan story begins prevents the user from investing in a plan they cannot buy. This prevents the feeling of being misled, even if the restriction was clearly disclosed in fine print somewhere.

---

### Top Friction Points

**1. Waiting period math is invisible**
No persona could quickly calculate "I enroll on August 1, when can I book a crown?" The hub must provide either a waiting-period countdown framing ("enroll today, major work eligible around [month]") or a plain statement ("12-month wait means no crown coverage until your second policy year").

**2. Family plan mechanics are unexplained**
Priya and Caleb both ran into the wall of "is this one policy covering both of us or do we need two?" Individual PPO plans are typically one person per policy. If family plans exist, they must be explained. If they do not exist for a given plan, that must be stated plainly rather than left ambiguous.

**3. Prior-coverage waiver process is unexplained**
Derek knows the waiver exists but has no idea how to execute it. The hub mentions the waiver in plan copy but provides no process guidance. This is a critical drop-off point for the COBRA gap persona, who is the most motivated buyer on the page. A "how to use your prior coverage waiver" explainer with carrier contact info is required.

**4. No recommended action for the specific scenario**
After each scenario, the hub (as imagined) surfaces one or two plan fits. But it does not tell the user what to do next: "Click through to Delta Dental's enrollment page and select Individual PPO Premium. When prompted, note that you are enrolling for adult orthodontics coverage." Users stop at the plan card and do not click through because the next step is unclear.

**5. "Plans from $30/mo" anchor creates false expectations**
Caleb and Mae see UHC Primary Dental at $30/month and anchor to it. When they discover it covers no major work, they feel the site front-loaded a misleading price. Lowest-price anchoring without immediate context ("preventive and basic only, no crowns or root canals") creates disappointment and erosion of trust for the broader hub.

---

### Top Share Triggers

**1. Exclusive insight the user could not find elsewhere**
Natalie's share trigger: "One individual plan covers adult Invisalign." Derek's share trigger: "There is a 63-day gap window to keep major-work coverage." Sandra's share trigger: a retirement-facing comparison no Medicare site provides. The hub is shareworthy when it surfaces something the user did not know before and could not find quickly on a generic comparison site.

**2. Scenario-specific summary the user can forward**
Every persona expressed willingness to share a one-paragraph summary of their specific situation and recommendation: Priya to other parents, Derek to laid-off coworkers, Caleb and Mae to friends. A "share this recommendation" CTA that generates a brief text or email summary of the user's matched scenario and plan would be the single highest-impact share mechanic.

**3. Dollar math the user can relay to a family member**
"The plan would pay about $1,500 of a $5,800 Invisalign treatment" is a sentence Natalie can say out loud. "20% of a $1,200 crown is $240, you still owe $960 but that beats full price" is a sentence Marcus can text his brother. Copy that is written in relayable sentences (not bullet points of percentages) is naturally shareable.

**4. A spouse or partner CTA**
Sandra and Priya are explicitly making decisions with or for another person. Caleb and Mae are literally shopping together. A "send to my spouse / partner" or "save for later" feature that does not require account creation (a simple mailto or share link) captures the moment of decision-sharing before the user navigates away.

**5. Feeling smart after reading the page**
The most universal share driver across all personas: the feeling of having understood something complicated. If the hub explains dental insurance in a way that is genuinely clear (not dumbed down, not padded with caveats) and users feel they finally "get it," they share the page as social proof of their own competence. The hub's voice should treat users as capable adults who need accurate information, not hand-holding.

## Design Implications for UX/SEO Specs

The following implications are concrete outputs for specs 10 through 18. Each is tied directly to a finding above.

**For spec 10 (UX flow/wireframe):**
- Position a terminology anchor ("what does major mean?") above the first plan card, not in a footer glossary.
- Design the scenario finder to end with a "next step" CTA, not just a plan match.
- Include a "send to partner/spouse" CTA in the scenario result.
- Surface state availability before plan story, not after.

**For spec 11 (interaction widgets):**
- A "waiting period to coverage date" calculator is higher priority than a premium comparison slider.
- A COBRA gap tool ("days since employer plan ended" calculator surfacing the 63-day window) would serve Derek's persona at a moment of high urgency and high intent.
- A "is it worth it for ortho?" quick calc for Natalie's scenario (lifetime max vs total treatment cost vs premium cost over enrollment period) addresses the explicit purchase question.

**For spec 12 (conversion psychology):**
- Limitation-first plan framing (honest constraint before the upsell) is the highest-trust conversion approach for this audience.
- "Dollar example" copy (real money, not percentages) is mandatory in plan cards.
- The hub should not use urgency or countdown mechanics: Sandra actively distrusts them, and Marcus does not need artificial urgency because his cracked tooth creates real urgency.

**For spec 14 (rails / monetization):**
- A right rail "talk to a licensed agent" CTA would serve Sandra (who will not buy online without a human touchpoint) and Derek (who needs prior-coverage waiver guidance).
- The CVS perk module for Aetna needs a plain one-sentence explainer of exactly what the $10/month reward is and how to redeem it, before any visual treatment.

**For spec 15 (on-page SEO) and spec 16 (GEO):**
- "Does any individual dental plan cover adult Invisalign?" is a high-intent, low-competition GEO question. The hub must answer it in a direct, quotable paragraph.
- "What happens to dental coverage after you lose your job?" and "how long can you go without dental insurance before losing coverage continuity?" are Derek-persona questions the hub must answer.
- "What is the best dental insurance for a 64-year-old retiring soon?" is a direct Sandra-persona GEO query.

**For spec 18 (content outline):**
- Every plan's "story" must include at minimum: a plain-language waiting period translation, a worked dollar example for the plan's primary use case, and a "this plan is NOT right for you if..." callout.
- The hub must include a plain-language insurance vocabulary section visible before any plan cards.
- A "about CoverCapy" trust module is required on this page, not linked away to an about page.
