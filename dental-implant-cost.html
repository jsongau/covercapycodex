How Much Does a Dental Implant Cost?

A clear, current 2026 breakdown of what one tooth and a full mouth actually cost, what your PPO plan will and will not pay, and how to spend the least getting there.

Quick answer

A single dental implant typically costs $3,000 to $6,000 all in. Most PPO plans pay 20 to 60 percent after a waiting period, capped by the annual maximum and sometimes blocked by a missing-tooth clause, so plan on $1,500 to $3,000 out of pocket even with coverage.

What you are paying for

The pieces of a single implant

A dental implant replaces a missing tooth with a titanium or zirconia post that fuses to your jawbone and supports a custom crown. Unlike a bridge, it stands on its own without grinding down neighboring teeth. The process is staged over several months, and each stage carries its own fee, which is why a single "implant" is really a small stack of separate charges.

Tooth extraction (if needed)$300 to $800+
Bone graft (if needed)$300 to $1,200 (complex grafts and sinus lifts $1,500 to $5,000)
Implant post (titanium fixture)$1,000 to $3,000

A typical single-implant fee bundles the consultation, 3D imaging, post, abutment, and crown. Preparatory work like extraction, bone grafting, or a sinus lift is billed on top. In high-cost metros such as New York, Los Angeles, and Chicago, expect prices 20 to 40 percent higher, sometimes reaching $6,000 to $8,000 for one tooth.

Price drivers

What moves the number up or down

  • Geography. Major metros run 20 to 40 percent above suburban and rural areas for the same procedure.
  • Who places it. An oral surgeon or periodontist usually costs more than a general dentist, and complex cases call for the specialist.
  • Materials. Zirconia posts and premium-grade crowns cost more than standard titanium and porcelain.
  • Preparatory surgery. An extraction, a bone graft, or a sinus lift each add their own fee before the implant can be placed.
  • How many. One tooth is priced per implant. Full-arch designs change the math entirely (see below).
Full mouth and All-on-4

What full-arch implants cost

When most or all teeth in an arch are gone, dentists use a fixed full-arch design such as All-on-4, where a non-removable bridge is anchored on four to six implants. These are larger cases priced per arch, not per tooth.

OptionTypical 2026 range (USD)
All-on-4, acrylic hybrid (per arch)$20,000 to $35,000
Premium zirconia arch (per arch)$35,000 to $90,000+
Full mouth, both arches (acrylic)$30,000 to $50,000
More implants per arch (6 to 8)adds roughly $5,000 to $15,000 per arch

Acrylic is the budget material; zirconia is the most durable and the most expensive, adding several thousand dollars per arch. Because these totals run far above any PPO annual maximum, insurance typically offsets only a small fraction, and most full-arch patients lean on financing.

With vs without insurance

What a PPO plan actually pays

Implants sit in the "major" service category alongside crowns, bridges, and dentures. That usually means a lower coinsurance rate than the 80 to 100 percent plans pay on cleanings and fillings, plus a waiting period and an annual maximum. Two numbers do most of the damage: the annual maximum and the missing-tooth clause. Most plans cap what they pay per benefit year at $1,000 to $2,000, and a single implant usually costs more than that, so the plan covers only part of one tooth in one year.

A worked example

Say your implant runs $4,500 and your plan covers major work at 50% with a $1,500 annual maximum. Fifty percent of $4,500 is $2,250, but the plan stops at its $1,500 cap. The plan pays $1,500 and you owe about $3,000 for the year. Staging the surgery and the crown across two benefit years can let you tap two annual maximums instead of one.

The trap nobody mentions

The missing-tooth clause

Read this before you buy a plan

A missing-tooth clause states the plan will not pay to replace any tooth that was already missing before your coverage began. It is the most common reason implant claims get denied, and many patients have never heard of it.

If you already have a gap, this clause can mean your shiny new plan pays nothing toward that specific implant, no matter how long you keep it. Some plans include the clause, some do not, and a few waive it for tooth loss caused by an accident with documentation. The only reliable way to know is to check the Summary of Benefits or call the carrier before enrolling.

Spend the least

How to make your plan pay the most

  • Stay in-network. PPO plans use negotiated fees, so the plan pays more and you owe less at an in-network office than out of network.
  • Stage across two benefit years. If the implant exceeds your annual maximum, time the extraction and graft in December and the post, abutment, and crown in January. You then tap two annual maximums instead of one.
  • Get a predetermination of benefits. Have your dentist submit the proposed treatment so the insurer estimates what it will pay before you commit. It is an estimate, not a guarantee, and shrinks if other claims use up your maximum first.
  • Choose a plan without a missing-tooth clause if you already have a gap, even if its premium runs a little higher.
  • Buy coverage before the gap exists where you can, so the clause never applies in the first place.
Paying over time

Financing an implant

Because implants often outrun a single year of benefits, most patients combine insurance with a payment plan. The common 2026 routes:

  • CareCredit. Promotional plans over 6, 12, 18, or 24 months with no interest if you pay the full balance before the window closes. If a balance remains, deferred interest is added back at a published rate around 32.99%, so the no-interest window only helps if you clear it in time.
  • In-house office plans. Offered directly by the practice, often low or no interest within a set window such as 12 months. No third-party lender involved.
  • Third-party patient lenders. Cherry, Proceed, Sunbit and similar services finance implants, and several approve less-than-perfect credit, with soft prequalification that does not ding your score.
  • HSA and FSA funds. Implants are a qualified medical expense, so pre-tax dollars apply.

Questions

Dental implant cost FAQ

How much does a single dental implant cost in 2026?+
A single tooth implant in the United States typically costs $3,000 to $6,000 all in, covering the surgical post, the abutment, and the crown. The average lands near $4,500. High-cost metro areas such as New York, Los Angeles, and Chicago run 20 to 40 percent higher, sometimes reaching $6,000 to $8,000.
Does PPO dental insurance cover dental implants?+
Many PPO plans do, treating implants as a major service that pays roughly 20 to 60 percent after a waiting period, up to the annual maximum. Coverage is not universal. Some plans, including Aetna Dental Direct and UHC Primary Dental, exclude implants entirely, so confirm the plan covers implants before enrolling.
How much does a dental implant cost with insurance?+
Even with a PPO plan, most people still pay $1,500 to $3,000 out of pocket for a single implant. Plans cap annual benefits at $1,000 to $2,000 in most cases, and a single implant usually costs more than that cap, so the plan covers only part of the bill in one benefit year.
What is the missing-tooth clause and why does it matter for implants?+
A missing-tooth clause states the plan will not pay to replace a tooth that was already missing before your coverage started. It is a leading reason implant claims get denied. If you already have a gap, check this clause before buying a plan, because some plans include it and some do not.
How much does a full mouth of dental implants cost?+
Full-arch options such as All-on-4 typically cost $20,000 to $35,000 per arch with an acrylic hybrid, and $35,000 to $90,000 or more per arch for premium zirconia. A full mouth, both arches, commonly runs $30,000 to $50,000, rising further for premium materials or more implants per arch.
Can I finance a dental implant with monthly payments?+
Yes. Most patients finance implants through CareCredit promotional plans, in-house office payment plans, or third-party lenders such as Cherry, several of which approve less-than-perfect credit. HSA and FSA funds also apply. CoverCapy helps you pair financing with a PPO plan so insurance covers part and payments spread the rest.
What is the cheapest way to get a dental implant?+
Stay in-network so negotiated PPO fees apply, stage the surgical and crown phases across two benefit years to use two annual maximums, choose a plan without a missing-tooth clause if you already have a gap, and get a predetermination of benefits in writing before treatment begins.
How long is the waiting period for implant coverage?+
Major-service waits typically run 6 to 12 months. A few plans, such as Ameritas PrimeStar, pay a reduced percentage from day one with no waiting period. If you need an implant soon, the waiting period is often the single most important plan feature to compare.
