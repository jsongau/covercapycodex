I. The Southern California Years

A practice shaped by the
communities it served.

Long before Abri Dental became a network of ten practices across California, Dr. David Abri was a young dentist working across some of Southern California's most diverse neighborhoods — Beverly Hills, Burbank, Huntington Park, Long Beach. From the start, his chair drew a mosaic of patients: families from Latin America, immigrants from East Asia, working-class neighbors who hadn't seen a dentist in a decade, executives squeezing in appointments between meetings.

What he learned in those years was simple, but it changed everything. People do not arrive at a dental office only with teeth. They arrive with histories. With fear. With distrust of healthcare systems that hadn't always served them well. With language barriers and grandmothers' remedies and questions they were sometimes too embarrassed to ask.

So Dr. Abri did something quietly radical for the profession: he slowed down. He listened. He learned that a fifteen-minute conversation before the first instrument touched a mouth could change the entire arc of care.

II. A Broader Philosophy

Lessons from Taiwanese
wellness, written into a
dentist's daily practice.

Some of the most meaningful conversations of Dr. Abri's career happened not in dental conferences, but across coffee tables — with Taiwanese acupuncturists and holistic wellness practitioners who became friends, mentors, and quiet collaborators in how he came to think about care.

What he absorbed from them was not a technique. It was a frame. A way of seeing a body — and a person — as one continuous, breathing whole. Jaw tension as something connected to the shoulders, to sleep, to the unspoken stress of a long week. Oral pain as something that lived inside a fuller story.

This perspective did not replace evidence-based dentistry. It enriched it. It shaped the way Dr. Abri began approaching TMJ disorders, chronic oral-facial pain, dental anxiety, and the dozens of small fears patients bring through the door. It taught him that easing a clenched jaw sometimes begins with easing a clenched mind.

It is a philosophy he speaks about with deep gratitude — and a generosity of credit — to the Taiwanese and Chinese practitioners who broadened his understanding of what it means to truly care for another person.

You cannot treat a jaw without treating the person it belongs to. That is the lesson I keep with me, every day, in every chair. — Dr. David Abri, DDS

IV. Community Impact

Every patient.

Whether you arrive at the chair from West LA, from a Taiwanese family in the San Gabriel Valley, from an immigrant household in Huntington Park, from anywhere — you are met first as a person, second as a patient.

Standard of Care

The Welcome

An open chair, no matter who is sitting in it.

Dr. Abri has long advocated for a practice that welcomes patients of every cultural, linguistic, and economic background. Chinese, Taiwanese, Latino, Black, white, immigrant, fourth-generation — the standard of care does not change, and neither does the warmth of the welcome. It is, he says, the only kind of dentistry worth doing.

The Conversation

Removing fear, one honest exchange at a time.

So much of Dr. Abri's reputation rests on what happens before treatment ever begins. Transparent explanations. Patient questions, taken seriously. Sedation and comfort options discussed without judgment. For many patients — especially those who have avoided the dentist for years — that conversation is itself the beginning of healing.
