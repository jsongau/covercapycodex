Inside Delta Dental · implants

Does Delta Dental cover implants?

Short answer: yes, on a PPO plan, but as major work with a waiting period, an annual cap, and a couple of clauses that decide the real number. Here is how it pays.

Does Delta Dental cover dental implants?
Yes, on a Delta Dental PPO plan. Implants are treated as major (prosthodontic) work, so a PPO plan typically pays 50% after a 12-month waiting period, up to the plan's annual maximum (for example $2,000 on the individual Premium tier) and after the deductible. A missing-tooth clause and an alternate-benefit rule can lower what the plan pays. DeltaCare USA, the dental HMO, instead charges a state-specific copay and has no annual maximum. The numbers

How much does Delta Dental pay toward an implant?

Coverage is a percentage of the plan's allowed amount, not the dentist's full fee, and it stops at the annual maximum.

On a Delta Dental PPO Premium planWhat applies to implants
Coverage level50% (major/prosthodontic), after the deductible
Waiting period12 months (waivable with prior comparable coverage, 63-day gap rule)
Annual maximum$2,000 per person, per calendar year, shared with all covered work
Deductible$50 per person ($150 family), per calendar year

Alternate benefit (LEAT)May pay at a lower-cost alternative allowance

A worked example, using round numbers: if an implant, abutment and crown total about $4,500 and you have used none of your annual maximum, the plan pays 50% of its allowed amount up to the $2,000 cap, so it could pay toward $2,000 and you would owe the rest, before any alternate-benefit reduction. This is an estimate, not a quote. Your allowed amounts, remaining maximum, and plan terms decide the real number, so confirm with the office and carrier first.

The waiting period

What is the waiting period for implants?

On a PPO plan implants sit in the major-services tier, which usually carries a 12-month waiting period before the plan pays. The useful exception: the wait can be waived if you had prior comparable major coverage with no more than a 63-day gap before the new plan started, per the brochure language. If major work is coming and you can document prior coverage, that waiver is worth asking about before you enroll.

Waiting period: the delay after coverage starts before a benefit tier begins to pay. For Delta PPO, major work including implants is commonly 12 months.
The fine print that matters

The missing-tooth clause and the alternate-benefit rule

These two terms decide more implant claims than the coverage percentage does.

Missing-tooth clause: a plan term that may exclude coverage to replace a tooth lost before your coverage began. On some Delta Premium certificates an implant for a previously lost tooth is not a benefit unless the extraction happened while you were covered under a Delta plan. This varies by state and effective date.
Alternate benefit (LEAT, least expensive alternative treatment): when more than one treatment can fix the problem, the plan may pay based on the lower-cost option. For an implant, the plan may reimburse at the allowance for a conventional bridge or denture, and you pay the difference.

Neither clause means implants are not covered. They mean the plan may pay less than 50% of the full implant fee. Ask the office to run a pre-treatment estimate so the plan states, in writing, what it will pay before you start.

On the HMO

Does DeltaCare USA (HMO) cover implants?

DeltaCare USA works differently. It is a dental HMO with a set copay per service and no annual maximum, so it does not cap what it pays in a year. Implant-related copays are not listed on the standard individual plan summary, so the amount is state-specific. Check your plan's copayment schedule, or ask your assigned network dentist, for the exact implant copay before treatment.
