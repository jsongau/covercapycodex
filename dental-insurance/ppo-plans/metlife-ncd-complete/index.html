What stands out

Five things that set NCD Complete apart

Most individual PPO dental plans cap out near $1,500 a year and charge a deductible every single year. This one is built differently. Here is what is genuinely uncommon, all drawn from the NCD Complete by MetLife brochure.

$10,000 annual maximum

  • One of the highest annual maximums available, several times the typical $1,000 to $1,500 ceiling. It resets every calendar year.

Lifetime deductible (one-time $100)

  • You pay the $100 deductible once, ever, in year one. From year two on there is no deductible at all. Most plans charge one every year.

No waiting periods

  • Every category is payable from your start date. Coverage is graduated by tenure, not gated behind a wait.

3 cleanings and 3 exams a year

  • Three routine exams and three cleanings per 12 months at 100%, above the usual two. Useful for gum-disease maintenance.

High implant allowance

  • Implants, implant repairs and implant-supported prosthetics are covered up to $3,000 per calendar year inside the $10,000 max, and that allowance resets each year rather than being a one-time lifetime cap.
In short

What is NCD Complete by MetLife?

NCD Complete is an individual PPO underwritten by Metropolitan Life Insurance Company and sold as NCD by MetLife, built around one of the highest annual maximums available: $10,000, with a $100 lifetime deductible you pay only once, in year one, and never again. It has no waiting periods, but it reimburses on a graduated schedule: preventive at 100% from day one, basic around 65% and major around 10% in year one, both rising over the next two years to roughly 90% basic and 60% major. Implants are covered up to a $3,000 per calendar year allowance inside the $10,000 max. It runs on the MetLife PDP Plus network, one of the largest in the country, and pays both in network and out of network.

Does NCD Complete have waiting periods?

No. Every covered category is available from day one. The plan replaces a waiting period with a graduated reimbursement model: rather than make you wait, it pays a smaller share in year one and increases that share in years two and three. So you can have major work done immediately, but the plan covers only about 10% of it in the first year.

How does the $100 lifetime deductible work?

You pay the $100 deductible once, ever, toward basic and major work in year one, and then it is gone for the life of the policy: there is no deductible in year two or any year after. NCD calls this a lifetime deductible (some sellers market the same thing as a "vanishing" deductible). Preventive care never carries a deductible. Most dental plans charge a deductible of around $50 every single year, so a deductible you pay only once is a genuine differentiator. See the glossary for how deductibles and coinsurance interact.

Does major coverage really reach 60%?

Yes, but only by year three. Major work pays roughly 10% in year one, about 50% in year two, and about 60% from year three onward. The headline $10,000 maximum is largely theoretical in year one given that low early reimbursement: this is a long-game plan that rewards members who stay several years.

Best for you if…

  • You will stay three or more years. By year three basic reaches about 90% and major about 60%, and the high $10,000 max finally pays off.
  • You are planning bigger work after year one: an implant plus several crowns timed for year two or three, when major reaches 50 to 60 percent and the $10,000 maximum is high enough to cover a lot of treatment in a single year.
  • You want the highest ceiling available and a $3,000/yr implant benefit with no waiting period.
  • You value a lifetime deductible: you pay the $100 once, ever, in year one, then never again, instead of every year.

Skip it if…

  • You need major work in year one. At about 10% reimbursement, the plan covers little of a crown or root canal in the first 12 months.
  • You need orthodontics. Braces are not a featured benefit on this plan.
  • You want a plan whose headline number matches day-one reality. Here the $10,000 ceiling and first-year value diverge.
When each benefit turns on

Your coverage builds on a schedule

There are no waiting periods here, so every category is live on your effective date. What changes over time is how much the plan reimburses.

Day one
Everything is live. Preventive at 100%, basic around 65%, major around 10%, implants on the graduated schedule. No waits on any category.
Year two
Reimbursement rises. Basic moves to about 80% and major to about 50%. A good window to time a larger case.
Year three+
The plan matures. Basic reaches about 90% and major about 60%, and the high $10,000 maximum finally works in your favor.

Good to know

The fine print that changes the bill

Exact wording varies by state. Read your own plan's exclusions section to confirm.

Low year-one major rate

Major work reimburses only about 10% in year one, the weakest first year on our shelf. The $10,000 ceiling is hard to actually use until the plan matures by year three.

Implant sub-maximum

Implant benefits draw on a separate $3,000 per calendar year cap inside the plan, on top of the graduated reimbursement schedule. A complex case can use it up fast.

Ortho not featured

Orthodontics is not a featured benefit. If braces or aligners are the goal, confirm on the official document or choose a plan built for ortho.

State variations

Availability, premium and exact graduated rates differ by state. Confirm availability for your state at enrollment.

Plain about the gaps

What NCD Complete does not cover

A high maximum is only useful if you know its limits. Per the brochure, these are not covered:

Orthodontia

No braces or aligners for adults or children. If ortho is the goal, choose a plan built for it.

TMJ and night guards

No TMJ treatment (except in Minnesota) and no bruxism or night-guard appliances.

Cosmetic services

Purely cosmetic procedures, including whitening, are not a covered benefit.

Missing-tooth limitation

Dentures or implant prosthetics to replace teeth missing before your coverage started are not covered, except congenitally missing teeth.

FAQ

Common questions

What is the annual maximum on NCD Complete?
$10,000 per person per year, the highest annual maximum of any plan on CoverCapy's shelf. Implants draw on a separate $3,000 per calendar year sub-maximum inside that ceiling. The high cap pays off most once the graduated reimbursement matures by year three.
Does NCD Complete have waiting periods?
No. Every category is available from day one. Instead of a wait, the plan uses graduated reimbursement: it pays a smaller share in year one and a larger share each following year, reaching about 90% basic and 60% major by year three.
How much does major work cost in year one?
Year-one major reimbursement is only about 10%, so a $2,000 crown reimburses roughly $200 in the first year and the rest is yours. This is the plan's main weakness. If you need major work immediately, a plan that covers more on day one fits better. Compare crown plans
How does the $100 lifetime deductible work?
You pay the $100 deductible once, ever, in year one, and then there is no deductible from year two onward. NCD calls this a lifetime deductible (sometimes marketed as a "vanishing" deductible). It applies to basic and major work only, never preventive. Most dental plans charge a deductible of around $50 every year, so a deductible you pay only once is a real advantage.
Is NCD Complete good for implants?
It covers implants, implant repairs and implant-supported prosthetics up to $3,000 per calendar year inside the $10,000 maximum, and that allowance resets each year rather than being a lifetime cap. That makes it a strong fit for a single implant or several crowns in a year. If you are planning two or more implants, Humana Extend 5000 may be the better choice, since it offers a higher dedicated implant allowance (about $4,000 lifetime).
What does NCD Complete cost per month?
Roughly $72 to $100 per month for individual coverage, varying by state, age and ZIP. There is no single national price. Confirm the exact premium on an official NCD by MetLife quote for your state.
Which dentists accept the MetLife PDP Plus network?
You pay the least when you see a dentist contracted with MetLife PDP Plus, one of the largest dental networks in the country. Participation varies by area and can change, so check the official MetLife or NCD provider search and call the office to confirm before booking. Find MetLife dentists near you
NCD Complete vs Humana Extend 5000?
NCD Complete carries a $10,000 maximum, twice Humana's, plus a one-time deductible and no waits, but its graduated schedule reimburses only about 10% of major work in year one. Humana Extend 5000 has a documented $5,000 maximum and pays 50% on major after a 6-month wait, rising to 60% in year two. The right pick depends on whether you value the bigger ceiling over several years or stronger early-year reimbursement. See the Humana breakdown
