✶ Dental insurance guide

Dental insurance: plans, costs, and how to get covered in 2026

Dental insurance is a plan that covers part of your dental care in exchange for a monthly premium. The best dental insurance for most people in 2026 is a PPO plan with no waiting period on the work you need, an annual maximum high enough to cover it, and your own dentist in-network. A PPO usually beats a DHMO or discount plan because it covers preventive care at 100 percent and lets you keep your dentist. Individual PPO plans run about $30 to $90 per month. Key takeaways

  • A PPO dental plan lets you see any dentist and pays the most when that dentist is in-network. It covers preventive care at 100 percent, basic work around 80 percent, and major work around 50 percent.
  • Individual dental insurance costs about $30 to $90 per month in 2026. Premiums depend on the annual maximum, the waiting period, your ZIP code, and your age.
  • Most plans apply a 6 to 12-month waiting period to crowns, root canals, and implants. Ameritas PrimeStar and UnitedHealthcare Primary Dental have no waiting period.
  • The annual maximum is the cap an insurer pays per year, usually $1,000 to $5,000. A single crown costs about $1,300 and an implant $3,000 to $5,000, so the maximum matters for major work.

How to choose a dental plan in 5 steps

  1. List the care you need. Write down the work you expect this year: a cleaning, a filling, a crown, a root canal, braces, or an implant. The plan follows the treatment.
  2. Match the waiting period to your timeline. Need major work soon? Choose a no-waiting-period plan like Ameritas PrimeStar or UnitedHealthcare Primary Dental.
  3. Check the annual maximum against your costs. A crown runs about $1,300 and an implant $3,000 to $5,000, so a $1,000 maximum will not cover major treatment.
  4. Confirm your dentist is in-network. A PPO only pays its full rate in-network. Verify before you buy so you are not balance-billed.
  5. Compare the monthly premium last. Once the coverage and timing fit, compare premiums. Most individual PPO plans run $30 to $90 per month.

✶ How it works

How does dental insurance work?

Dental insurance works on a 100/80/50 structure: a PPO plan covers preventive care such as cleanings and exams at 100 percent, basic care such as fillings at about 80 percent, and major care such as crowns and root canals at about 50 percent, after you meet a small deductible. The plan stops paying once you hit the annual maximum. Six terms decide what you actually pay.

Term Deductible The amount you pay first, usually $50 to $100, before the plan starts paying its share. Term Coinsurance Your share of each bill after the deductible, such as 20 percent of a filling or 50 percent of a crown. Term Annual maximum The most the plan pays in a year, usually $1,000 to $5,000. You pay everything above it. Term Waiting period The delay before a plan covers a service, often 6 to 12 months for crowns and major work. Term In-network A dentist who has agreed to the carrier's rates. You pay the most when you go out-of-network. Term Out of pocket What you pay yourself: the deductible, coinsurance, and anything above the annual maximum.

✶ Decision

What is the difference between PPO, DHMO, and a discount plan?

A PPO dental plan lets you see any dentist and pays the most in-network, with a deductible and an annual maximum. A DHMO has no annual maximum and lower premiums but ties you to one assigned dentist and fixed copays. A dental discount plan is not insurance; it gives a reduced cash price at member dentists with no claims, no waiting period, and no maximum. For most people a PPO is the strongest choice because it keeps your dentist and covers preventive care fully.

✶ Section 6 · Cost

How much does dental care cost, and how do I get the most from my plan?

Dental work is expensive without coverage: a cleaning runs about $100 to $200, a filling $150 to $300, a crown about $1,300, a root canal $700 to $1,500, and an implant $3,000 to $5,000. Because most plans reset the annual maximum each year, timing major work across two plan years can roughly double what insurance pays. The benefit-maxing guides show how.

✶ Common questions

Dental insurance FAQ

Individual dental insurance costs about $30 to $90 per month in 2026. Basic preventive-only PPO plans like UnitedHealthcare Primary Dental start near $30 per month, while plans with high annual maximums and no waiting period, such as Mutual of Omaha Dental and Ameritas PrimeStar, run $60 to $90 per month. Premiums vary by ZIP code, age, and tobacco use.
The best dental insurance is a PPO plan with no waiting period on the services you need, an annual maximum high enough to cover your expected work, and your own dentist in-network. For most people a PPO beats a DHMO or discount plan because it lets you keep your dentist and covers preventive care at 100 percent. There is no single best plan for everyone; the right plan depends on your treatment, timeline, and dentist.
A PPO dental plan lets you see any dentist and pays the most when that dentist is in-network, with a deductible and an annual maximum. A DHMO has no annual maximum and lower premiums, but you must use an assigned in-network dentist and pay fixed copays per procedure. A discount plan is not insurance; it gives a reduced cash price at member dentists with no claims or maximum.
Some PPO dental plans cover implants as a major service, typically at 50 percent after a 6 to 12-month waiting period and subject to the annual maximum. Mutual of Omaha Dental and Ameritas PrimeStar include implant coverage. Many basic plans exclude implants or apply a missing tooth clause, so confirm implant coverage on the plan summary before you buy.
Ameritas PrimeStar and UnitedHealthcare Primary Dental have no waiting period and cover basic services from day one. Preventive care, including cleanings and exams, is covered from day one on nearly every PPO plan. Most plans still apply a 6 to 12-month waiting period to crowns, root canals, and other major work unless the plan states otherwise.
Dental insurance is usually worth it if you expect more than two cleanings a year or any restorative work. Two preventive visits alone cost about $200 to $400 out of pocket, which a $30 per month plan covers at 100 percent. If you only need one cleaning a year and no other work, a dental savings plan may cost less than insurance.
